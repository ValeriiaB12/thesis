Keywords: With environmental, social, and corporate governance (ESG) issues gaining global prominence, it is increasingly 
Corporate risk-taking recognized that sustainable practices must extend beyond individual organizations to encompass their entire

Supply chain highlighting how supply chain transmission influences corporate sustainability initiatives. Using data from 
Sustainability

Chinese listed firms from 2009 to 2023, our results reveal that supplier risk-taking positively influences the ESG 
performance of client firms. This positive effect is found to be progressively strengthened as corporate reputation 
and supplier dependence increase but gradually weakened with rising investor confidence. These findings not 
only deepen the understanding of the supply chain’s role in shaping corporate ESG performance but also 
highlight ESG as a proactive strategy for client firms to anticipate and mitigate risks transmitted from suppliers. 
This offers valuable insights for managers and policymakers seeking to enhance sustainable practices through 
effective supply chain risk management.

Prior research suggests that outward foreign direct investment 
(OFDI), R&D expenditure, and asset financialization are often regarded 
as indicators of a firm’s propensity to undertake higher levels of risk in 
pursuit of long-term strategic returns (Li, 2011; Nielsen and Nielsen, 
2011; Bargeron et al., 2010). Although these "high-risk, high-return" 
behaviors may not necessarily lead to operational or financial distress 
for the supplier, they introduce supply chain uncertainty for client firms.

First, OFDI often requires suppliers not only to allocate part of the 
funds and products needed for domestic operations to overseas markets, 
but also to adjust their product structure and business strategies ac
cording to the characteristics of the target regions, while seeking new 
client firms based on local demand. These actions lead to adjustments in 
domestic production and the restructuring of the supply chain, thereby 
increasing the uncertainty faced by local client firms. Second, increased 
R&D investment may divert funds away from the supplier’s production 
of existing products, thereby altering the supply of raw materials and 
intermediate goods, which disrupts the client firm’s production plans.

Appendix 1 Functional form test of partially linear functional-coefficient model.
In this appendix, we introduce the method for testing the stability of our moderating model. To examine whether the functional form assumptions 

we adopt for the moderating effects are more appropriate than those of the conventional linear interaction term model, we construct a statistic and use 
the wild bootstrap method to obtain the sample distribution of this statistic. Specifically, we first present the two models as follows: 
C ESGit =α + β1⋅S Risk-takingit + β2⋅S Risk-takingit × Zit + ω⋅Othersit + Yearst + Industryit + firmi + ε1,it (1a) 

C ESGit = c+ β(Zit) ⋅ S Risk-takingit +ω ⋅ Othersit +Yearst + Industryit + firmi + ε2,it (2a) 
Model 1 is a linear interaction term panel data model, while Model 2 is a partially linear functional-coefficient panel data model.
Second, we propose two hypotheses:
H0: Model (1) is the correct model.
H1: Model (2) is the correct model.
Under the null hypothesis, all model coefficients are fixed, including S Risk-takingit, which does not change with Zit . In other words, the moderating 

effect variable Zit is linear. The alternative hypothesis (H1) is that Model (2) is the correct model, suggesting that the relationship between S Risk- 
takingit and C ESGit is nonlinear and varies with Zit.

To test these hypotheses, we develop the T-statistic, defined as follows:

Following prior studies (Kreiss et al. (2008), Cai (2007) and Cai et al. (2000)), we use the non-parametric wild bootstrap method to directly obtain 
the sample distribution of T. We compute 1000 bootstrap statistics to determine the 1 %, 5 %, and 10 % critical values of the empirical distribution of 
the bootstrapped statistic. If the p-value is less than 10 %, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that Model 2 (the partially linear 
functional-coefficient panel data model) is the correct model.

The testing method employed in this study has the following three key features: First, the statistic constructed uses the partially linear functional- 
coefficient model and the linear interaction term panel data model effectively tests whether the impact of the core explanatory variable on the 
dependent variable is influenced by the non-linear effect of the moderating variable. Second, during the wild bootstrap process, the residuals from the 
model estimation are used. Regardless of whether the null hypothesis holds, the residuals we use are asymptotically consistent, which improves the 
power of our test statistic. Third, by calculating the p-value through wild bootstrap, we ensure that the test maintains favorable large-sample 
properties when using non-parametric estimation in panel data. As shown in Appendix Table 1, all the T-statistics are statistically significant at 
the 1 % (or 5 %) level across all estimations, suggesting that the partially linear functional-coefficient panel data model is statistically superior to the 
linear interaction term panel data model at the 1 % (or 5 %) significance level.
