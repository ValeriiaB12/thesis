Investor‑level common ownership, stock return 
comovement, and competition

Markus Münster1 · Martin Walther2

Accepted: 6 July 2025

Abstract
This study examines the relationship between common ownership and the correla-
tion of stock returns. Using S&P 500 data, we find that higher levels of common 
ownership at the individual investor level are associated with stronger pairwise cor-
relations of stock returns in subsequent periods. This relationship holds across mul-
tiple industries and remains robust after controlling for factors such as market risks, 
stock prices, liquidity, financial leverage, index additions, geographic proximity, 
correlated trading, and the level of competition in the industry. Our findings indi-
cate that these effects are not solely driven by large institutional investors. Another 
contribution of our study is distinguishing between the effects driven by the level 
and the similarity of these control variables. Moreover, since reduced competition 
is associated with higher stock return correlation, our results are consistent with the 
existence of anticompetitive effects of common ownership across several industries. 
We provide further support for this competition channel by demonstrating that this 
effect is mainly driven by longer-term investors. Our findings have important policy 
implications, suggesting that antitrust regulations should consider indirect competi-
tive effects from common ownership, even without explicit coordination between 
companies.

1  Introduction

Understanding the determinants of the correlation between stocks is crucial for port-
folio allocation, as it is the decisive factor for diversification effects. In this study, 
we investigate the relationship between common ownership at the individual inves-
tor level and the comovement of stock returns. Using S&P 500 data from Refinitiv 
Eikon’s Ownership Summary for the period 1998–2022, we show that increases in 
common ownership are followed by increases in the pairwise correlation of stock 
returns. This result is robust to various control variables, including Fama–French 
factors, stock prices, and index inclusions. Figure 1 illustrates a strong positive rela-
tionship between changes in common ownership and subsequent changes in pair-
wise correlation. This pattern, based on observations grouped into 100 clusters, 
highlights the potential relevance of ownership structure for stock return comove-
ments and motivates our study.

Furthermore, building on the findings of Abdoh and Varela (2017), who show 
decreased competition is associated with higher pairwise correlations of stock 
returns, we link our results to the ongoing discussion on anticompetitive effects of 
common ownership. Specifically, our observation that increases in common own-
ership is followed by increases in pairwise correlation which aligns with the idea 
that shareholders with stakes in multiple companies use their influence on corpo-
rate policy to reduce competition between these companies to maximize the overall 
value of their holdings. This, in turn, leads to higher pairwise correlations of stock 
returns, i.e., stronger stock return comovement. In an extension to our analysis, we 
differentiate between short-term (holding periods up to 2 years) and non-short-term

common ownership (holding periods of more than 2 years).1 We find a significant 
positive effect of long-term common ownership on pairwise correlations, while the 
effect of short-term common ownership, depending on the specification, is negative 
or statistically insignificant. This result strengthens our proposed channel, as inves-
tors with longer investment horizons are more likely to exert influence on corporate 
policies to reduce competition.

Our key contributions are as follows. First, our analysis is not limited to specific 
investor types or sizes, such as actively managed or ETF funds. Instead, we provide 
novel analyses at the individual investor level. Notably, our results indicate that the 
effect of common ownership on pairwise correlations is driven by more than just a 
few large investors. Second, we extend the analyses of studies such as Antón and 
Polk (2014) by additionally considering recently developed vector-based measures 
for common ownership from Boller and Morton (2020) in the analysis of stock pair 
return correlations. Third, we use a current dataset that provides data from 1998 
to 2022. This is particularly relevant given the increased relevance and influence 
of institutional investors in recent years. For instance, according to Lewellen and 
Lewellen (2022), institutional ownership of publicly traded US firms has more than 
doubled from 1980 to 2017. Fourth, we account for the possibility of reverse causal-
ity by considering the effect of a change in common ownership on the correlation in 
subsequent periods. Fifth, we include control variables that represent the pairwise 
similarity and the absolute level, respectively. In this way, we can show whether 
corporate similarity drives pairwise correlations (i.e., the similarity of stock return 
movements) or whether the absolute level of a particular factor is decisive. Sixth, 
our paper closes the gap between the analysis of stock return correlation and the 
discussion of anticompetitive effects of common ownership (e.g., Azar et al. 2022, 
2018). Seventh, we differentiate between short-term and non-short-term common 
ownership, which further strengthens the proposed competition channel.

Our study is related to several streams of literature, which we present in the fol-
lowing paragraphs. First, we contribute to the research on stock comovement. 
In their seminal study, Barberis et al. (2005) present evidence on friction- or sen-
timent-based comovement by examining inclusions and removals of stocks in the 
S&P 500 index. Using data from 1979 to 2000, they show that stock returns have 
a higher correlation with the returns of other members of the index after the stock 
has been added to the S&P 500. Conversely, the correlation with the stocks that are 
not included in the index decreases. Green and Hwang (2009) provide evidence of 
price-based comovement. Specifically, they find that the stock’s correlation to low-
priced stocks increases after a stock split, while its correlation to stocks with higher 
prices decreases. This indicates that stocks within the same price category are more 
correlated. However, with a modified methodology, Chen et al. (2016) find limited 
empirical evidence for the results of Barberis et al. (2005) and Green and Hwang 
(2009).

Regarding the determinants of comovement, Bekaert et al. (2009) find that risk-
based factor models fit stock comovements reasonably well, while studies by Chan 
et  al. (2013) and Broman (2016) emphasize the role of liquidity. Information and

networks also play a critical role in stock return comovement. For example, stocks 
affiliated with the same underwriter or with shared analyst coverage exhibit stronger 
comovement (Grullon et al. 2014; Muslu et al. 2014). Hameed et al. (2015) further 
demonstrate that firms with high analyst coverage tend to have stronger comove-
ment with other stocks, as investors use the well-covered firms’ information to value 
lesser-followed stocks. Other factors, such as geographical proximity (Li and Zhao 
2016), commonalities in newswire text (Box 2018), and political networks (Piotroski 
et al. 2022), also contribute to stock return comovement.

Regarding the impact of shared ownership, Antón and Polk (2014) establish a 
positive relationship between shared ownership of mutual funds and the correlation 
of stock returns in the US stock market. In a later study, Box (2018) also finds a pos-
itive relationship between shared mutual fund and institutional ownership and stock 
price comovement. Ben‐David et al. (2018) find that ETF membership significantly 
increases the volatility of the underlying securities. Analyzing data from the banking 
sector, Iselin et al. (2021) find that common ownership mainly via passive mutual 
funds increases a bank’s contribution to systemic risk. Coles et al. (2022) show that 
index investing causes a higher correlation of returns between stocks contained in 
the index and with the index itself. Based on a data set of ETF holdings in the US 
market, Da and Shive (2018) find a positive relationship between the ETF owner-
ship of a stock and its synchronicity with the market. They show that the comove-
ment depends on the ETF’s activity: The higher the ETF’s turnover, the stronger the 
comovement of the shares in its portfolio. Boller and Morton (2020) create owner-
ship vectors and use similarity measures to capture common ownership. They find 
that entering the S&P 500 is associated with an increase in common ownership and 
stock returns. Furthermore, they show that their measures of common ownership 
predict cumulative abnormal returns.

The aforementioned studies each focus on a specific type of common ownership, 
such as through mutual funds or ETFs. In contrast, we are among the first to con-
sider the overall stock holdings of investors, regardless of the investment type. This 
approach provides novel evidence on the effects of common ownership at the indi-
vidual investor level. Considering common ownership at the investor level is crucial 
because it enables us to link it to anticompetitive effects through investors’ influence 
on corporate policies. Another distinction from previous studies is that, due to this 
mechanism, we do not weight common ownership by the market values of the two 
companies. Weighting by market values causes common ownership to be driven by 
the share in the larger company, which fails to account for the fact that influencing 
the policy of the smaller company can significantly impact the competition and, con-
sequently, the correlation between both companies. Our approach captures the pos-
sibility of such anticompetitive effects more effectively.

Second, our study is related to the several articles that study stock price synchro-
nicity at the market level. Based on the approach of Roll (1988), Morck et al. (2000) 
and Jin and Myers (2006) empirically investigate stock price synchronicity using the 
R-squared from cross-sectional regressions. Jin and Myers (2006) argue that the less 
transparent the company is, the more of its operating cash flow can be captured by 
internal managers. Thus, less firm-specific risk is absorbed by external investors. 
This leads to the opacity of companies being linked to stock price synchronicity. An

and Zhang (2013) extend this work and establish a connection to institutional inves-
tors by using the classification of Bushee (1998) for dedicated and transient insti-
tutional investor as well as quasi-indexers. While dedicated institutional investors 
monitor the extraction of cash flows, transient institutional investors trade frequently 
and have less incentive to monitor the companies they have invested in. Analyzing 
data from 1987 to 2010, An and Zhang (2013) find a negative relationship between 
dedicated institutional investors and stock price synchronicity. However, the pres-
ence of transient institutional investors is accompanied by an increase in stock price 
synchronicity. In contrast with the presented research, our study does not analyze 
correlations at the market level but instead focuses on pairwise correlations, i.e., 
correlations at the company level.

Third, our study also contributes to the literature on anticompetitive effects of 
common ownership. Azar et al. (2022) and Azar et al. (2018) analyze the impact of 
common ownership on competition in the banks and airline industry. In both indus-
tries, an increase in common ownership results in higher prices, likely via lower 
product market competition. In contrast, other studies, such as Dennis et al. (2022), 
Gramlich and Grundl (2018), and Backus et  al. (2021) provide limited empirical 
support for anticompetitive effects of common ownership in the airline, banking, 
and ready-to-eat industry, respectively.

Regarding the link between pairwise correlation and competition, Abdoh and 
Varela (2017) analyze data from 2005 to 2014. They show that increasing product 
market competition measured with the Herfindahl–Hirschman index (HHI) is asso-
ciated with an increase in idiosyncratic volatility relative to systematic volatility, i.e., 
a decrease in pairwise correlation. A possible explanation is that when market power 
and concentration are high, firm-specific cost shocks can be more easily passed on 
to customers because fewer substitutes are available to them. When competition and 
pairwise correlations have a negative relationship, and common ownership leads to 
a lower level of competition, pairwise correlations should be positively related to 
common ownership. Therefore, our analysis can be interpreted as evidence on anti-
competitive effects of common ownership. Note that our analysis includes changes 
in industry HHIs as control variables, meaning that our results do not merely reflect 
effects of industry-level competition.

The remainder of this study is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the data 
set. Section 3 presents the empirical method. In Sect. 4, the results are described. 
Section 5 contains a discussion, and Sect. 6 concludes.

2  Data set

We use data from the US stock market. Specifically, we consider stocks listed in the 
S&P 500 index. In addition to reducing the high effort required for data collection 
and processing, focusing on the S&P 500 has the following advantages. First, the 
index contains stocks from multiple industries, and covers the industries of airlines 
and banks examined by Azar et al. (2018) and Azar et al. (2022). Second, the index 
is a good single indicator of large capitalization equities, covering approximately 



	 M. Münster, M. Walther 

80% of the available market capitalization in the USA.2 Therefore, our results are 
likely to reflect the behavior of large investors and institutional investors, which 
means that they are likely to apply to other indices, stock markets, and countries. 
Third, the data regarding shareholder reports on the investor level for stocks con-
tained in the S&P index are reliably available. Specifically, for each stock, quarterly 
ownership reports can be collected from Refinitiv Eikon’s3 Ownership Summary 
(“Shareholder History Report”). Each report contains all individual shareholders of 
a company with their name and the percentage of outstanding shares held. In con-
trast with the Thomson Reuters Spectrum data set, which has been used in several 
related studies (e.g., Antón and Polk 2014; Azar et al. 2018), the Ownership Sum-
mary is not limited to 13F filings on institutional investors, but also includes insider 
trading, mutual funds, and private investors. This provides a more holistic view of 
changes in shareholder composition and allows us to investigate common ownership 
at the individual investor level.

In order to account for different notations, all investor names are converted to 
lower case, and special characters are removed. However, for complex investor net-
works, e.g., due to globally operating companies with national subsidiaries, the cor-
responding investor names vary. Thus, the investor names are manually harmonized 
by replacing different tightly connected investor names with the corresponding 
umbrella organization. For example, various Blackrock subsidiaries such as “Black-
Rock Investment Management (UK) Ltd.” and “BlackRock Investment Management 
(Australia) Ltd.” are replaced with “blackrock” as the investor name. However, our 
results are robust to using the raw investor names. The corresponding results are 
presented in Appendix A. After the adjustments, the ownership share is calculated 
as the sum over each unified investor name.

3  Methodology

This section presents our methodology to analyze the hypothesized relationship 
between common ownership at the investor level and stock return comovement. 
Instead of focusing on specific events, such as the addition to an index, we consider 
changes in common ownership and correlation over a period of 24 years. Our meth-
odology consists of three parts. First, all relevant variables are computed for each 
time point. Second, the change of these variables over a specified period is calcu-
lated. Third, the relationship between the change of common ownership of a stock 
pair and the subsequent change of pairwise return correlations is estimated by means 
of linear regression.

sification, all possible combinations of stocks are pooled to create stock pairs. We 
then calculate all variables for these stock pairs. We measure common ownership in 
three ways. First, we consider ownership overlap by adopting the formula of Mün-
ster and Walther (2021)5:

In the following, we present and explain the used control variables. Common fac-
tors (Fama and French 1993) are likely to have an effect on pairwise correlations. To 
ensure that these effects are not the drivers of our results on common ownership, we 
include control variables for factor similarity into our regressions.7 Specifically, we 
control for the common factors presented in Fama and French (2015). As Antón and 
Polk (2014), we compute percentiles for each control variable and period to elimi-
nate the impact of outliers. Furthermore, for each factor, we consider the similarity 
between both companies (the absolute difference in the respective factor), and the 
absolute level, respectively. In this way, we can test whether similarity in fundamen-
tals drives correlations (i.e., the similarity of stock return movements), or whether 
particular levels of the respective factor increase comovement. We expect a positive 
relationship between correlation and the similarity of factors, while the effect of the 
factor level depends on the specific variable.

In contrast with Antón and Polk (2014), our analysis focuses on the change of vari-
ables instead of their absolute values. First, the change in common ownership between 
two points in time is calculated. Second, the subsequent change of the pairwise stock 
return correlation is computed. In the last step, we analyze whether a change in com-
mon ownership is followed by a variation in the pairwise stock return correlation in 
future periods. Our approach addresses the possibility of reverse causality, without hav-
ing to rely on external events, such as the addition to an index. The intuition behind it is 
as follows. If we observe that a change in common ownership is followed by a change 
in pairwise correlations, it is more likely that the change in common ownership led to 
the subsequent change in correlation than that investors deliberately chose to invest in 
stocks with a higher correlation in future periods. Another argument against reverse 
causality is that risk-averse investors have an incentive to invest in stocks with low cor-
relation in order to minimize the variance of portfolio returns, which means that from 
a portfolio theory perspective, one would expect a negative relationship between com-
mon ownership and correlation (see, e.g., Münster and Walther 2021).

Because the data set covers companies from various industry classifications, 
it is likely that the data contains heterogeneous patterns across the different busi-
ness sectors. To account for these, in additional regressions, we divide the dataset 
into subsamples for each super-sector and estimate the slopes from Eq.  (34) for 
each subset of data, i.e., for each super-sector. To avoid multicollinearity, changes 
in industry, HHIs are excluded from these regressions.

As an extension of our analysis, we distinguish between short-term and non-
short-term common ownership. The underlying idea is that investors with longer 
investment horizons have stronger incentives and greater ability to influence 
competition strategically, while short-term investors lack both the incentive and 
capacity to do so. Based on the proposed competition channel, we expect the 
positive relationship between common ownership and pairwise correlation to be 
primarily driven by non-short-term investors. To implement this, we classify each 
investor as either short-term or non-short-term for every stock and boundary date 
in our sample, using a symmetric two-year window around that date.9 An investor 
is classified as non-short-term for a stock and boundary date if their ownership 
share for that stock is greater than zero throughout the entire window. Based on 
this classification, we create two subsamples: one including only holdings of non-
short-term investors and the other including only holdings of short-term investors 
for each stock and boundary date. We then compute the ownership measures for 
each subsample according to Eqs. (2), (4), and (5) and include them separately in 
the regressions.

All regression coefficients are estimated using the ordinary least squares (OLS) 
method.

coefficient in the banking sector. This provides support for the recent results of Ise-

(2018). However, our insignificant coefficient is consistent with the reevaluation of 
Dennis et al. (2022), which concludes that common ownership is likely to not have 
anticompetitive effects in the airline industry.

factor similarity and the absolute level of the respective factor. Inter alia, we can 
confirm that stocks of larger companies and stocks with similar price levels have 
higher correlations (Green and Hwang 2009), and the finding of Barberis et  al. 
(2005) that the addition to an index is accompanied by stronger comovement. Fur-
thermore, we find that decreases in profit margins are accompanied by an increase in 
correlations, supporting the notion that undercutting profit margins increases com-
petition and thus leads to a higher fraction of idiosyncratic risks (Dou et al. 2021).

Overall, our study contributes to the literature on stock return synchronicity and 
comovement by using current data to demonstrate a robust relationship between 
common ownership of any investor and investment type, and the pairwise correla-
tion of stock returns. This extends the analysis of Antón and Polk (2014) who focus 
on actively managed funds. Furthermore, for a large selection of variables dis-
cussed in the literature, we show and clarify whether their level or similarity drives 
comovement. Finally, we link our results to the controversy regarding anticompeti-
tive effects and show that the pairwise correlations of stock returns are consistent 
with anticompetitive effects of common ownership across a wide range of industries.

5.2  Limitations

Although our data set includes a selection of companies from different sectors and 
with different market capitalizations, our sample only consists of companies from 
the USA, and focuses on larger capitalizations. Therefore, the generalizability of our 
findings to other countries or smaller firms is uncertain. However, given that the 
institutional investors of our sample are likely to invest in companies across various 
market sizes and regions (e.g., Döring et al. 2021), we expect the effects observed in 
our study may also apply to other markets and smaller companies, though this war-
rants further investigation.

In some super-sectors, the number of companies is small. Therefore, our insignif-
icant coefficients should not be taken as a strong indication of the absence of a posi-
tive relationship between common ownership and pairwise correlations. However, 
most of our significant positive coefficients are in industries with a high number of 
observations, such as “Technology,” “Health Care,” and “Industrial Goods and Ser-
vices,” and can therefore be considered a strong indication of the proposed relation-
ship between common ownership and correlation.

Extending the sample to more companies is problematic as the effort of collecting 
and processing data is substantial, and reports on companies not listed in the S&P 
500 are often not available in the data base. However, focusing on the S&P 500 has 
the advantage of comparable companies, especially regarding the index affiliation, 
which means that we can rule out that index affiliation drives our results.

Another concern is the possibility of reverse causality, meaning that investors 
could deliberately choose to invest in companies with higher correlation. Existing 
research, e.g., Boller and Morton (2020), Barberis et al. (2005) or Antón and Polk 
(2014), addresses this problem by analyzing external events, such as index addi-
tions. In our study, we make use of the time series data and consider how a change 
in common ownership affects the pairwise correlation in subsequent periods. Our 



	 M. Münster, M. Walther 

finding that an increase in common ownership is accompanied by an increase in cor-
relation in later periods indicates that reverse causality is unlikely. Another argu-
ment against reverse causality is that portfolio theory suggests that investors with 
preferences according to the mean–variance framework prefer to invest in stocks 
with a low correlation, in order to minimize the variance of portfolio returns, and 
as shown for strategic investors by Münster and Walther (2021). From a portfolio 
theory perspective, one would therefore expect investors to select stocks that have a 
low correlation, i.e., common ownership to be associated with a significantly lower 
correlation. It is therefore unlikely that investors deliberately select highly correlated 
stocks, which means that reverse causality is unlikely.

Finally, we do not show a direct link between common ownership and competi-
tion but rely on the results of Abdoh and Varela (2017) to relate our results on pair-
wise correlations to competition. While this means our findings do not constitute 
definitive evidence of anticompetitive effects, they are consistent with such effects 
across a wide range of industries. Moreover, the inclusion of changes in industry 
HHIs as control variables and the distinction between short-term and non-short-term 
common ownership provide additional support to the proposed competition channel. 
Our study thus contributes both to the literature on stock return comovement and 
synchronicity, and to the ongoing discussion on the anticompetitive implications of 
common ownership.

6  Conclusion

Using S&P 500 data from 1998 to 2022, we show that pairwise correlations of stock 
returns within an industry are positively related with common ownership. Given the 
negative relationship between competition and correlation, our findings suggest that 
common ownership may have anticompetitive effects in multiple industries.

Our findings have several policy implications. Antitrust regulations may need to 
consider indirect competitive effects arising from common ownership from active 
and passive investors, even if companies do not explicitly coordinate. Our analy-
sis per industry highlights the sectors Technology, Health Care, Banks, Insurance, 
Real Estate, Media, and Industrial Goods and Services, for which the effects are 
statistically significant. Enhancing disclosure requirements on ownership structures 
and improving the availability of data on ownership could help policymakers better 
monitor common ownership. This would enable researchers to identify anticompeti-
tive effects and investigate potential countermeasures. These include limits on cross-
holdings in competing companies within the same industry, implementing policies 
that reduce voting rights of common owners in competing companies, and enforcing 
a stricter oversight of board appointments to prevent a disproportionate influence 
on multiple companies’ boards within the same industry. However, when consider-
ing limitations on common ownership, policymakers need to consider that this may 
reduce diversification opportunities, and consequently reduce market stability.

In terms of future research, several important avenues remain. First, while we have 
addressed reverse causality, additional evidence is needed to confirm whether our pro-
posed mechanism is indeed causal. Second, understanding the heterogeneous effects 
observed across industries and uncovering the underlying mechanisms present an 
intriguing research avenue. Third, assessing the generalizability of our findings to dif-
ferent market capitalizations and other countries with different regulatory environments 
would provide valuable insights. Moreover, examining the long-term implications of 
common ownership, such as its influence on innovation, is essential for a deeper under-
standing of its overall welfare impact. Across all these areas, improving the availability 
and quality of ownership data will be vital to advancing research.
