IT developments have made it easier for manufacturers to open a direct channel, leading to manufacturer encroachment. For
example, Hewlett-Packard Co. has a direct channel for selling its products to consumers without using retail stores. Additionally,
reputed companies such as Fast Retailing Co., Ltd., Procter and Gamble, and Apple Inc. use direct channels to sell their products.
With manufacturer encroachment, the manufacturer becomes a retailerвҖҷs competitor rather than a supply chain partner. Specifically,
it becomes challenging to consider the ownership of supply chain parties if the owner is a partner or a competitor. For example, the
Vanguard Group holds an 11.50% share of Best Buy4 and 7.4% of General Electric Co..5 This example represents the case in which
the retailer (Best Buy) is encroached by the manufacturer (General Electric) through common ownership. How do manufacturersвҖҷ
and retailersвҖҷ decision-making change in the threat of manufacturer encroachment? This study considered the following research
questions: (i) What is the effect of manufacturer encroachment and common ownership on supply chain partiesвҖҷ decision-making?
(ii) What is the effect of manufacturer encroachment and common ownership on retailer profits? (iii) What is the surplus effect of
common ownership?

This study constructs an analytical model based on game theory to explore the impact of common ownership on decision-making
processes and surplus management. In our model, the supply chain comprises one retailer and one manufacturer. The manufacturer
produces goods and sells them to the retailer at wholesale price. The retailer supplies the product to the final goods market by
determining its quantity. The manufacturer can also decide whether to encroach on the final goods market. When a manufacturer
encroaches quantity competition among the retailer and manufacturer occurs in the retail market. Our model assumes common
ownership among firms to emphasize the other partiesвҖҷ profit. The timeline of these events is as follows: First, the manufacturer
chooses whether to encroach. Second, the manufacturer decides on the wholesale price. Finally, in the non-encroachment scenario,
only the retailer determines the quantity, whereas, in the encroachment scenario, the retailer and manufacturer determine the
quantity. We describe our basic model in Section 3.6

From our analysis, we obtained the following results. In the non-encroachment case, the degree of channel substitution does
not affect the retailerвҖҷs profit, because the competition does not occur between the participants in the supply chain. In the non-
encroachment case, the wholesale price charged by the manufacturer increases as the degree of common ownership increases. Under
large common ownership, while the retailer raises the supplied quantity to enhance the manufacturerвҖҷs profit, anticipating retailer
behavior, the manufacturer raises the wholesale price to avoid excessive supply in the retail market. In the case of encroachment,
the degree of channel substitution plays an important role in considering our result. Under a large degree of channel substitution,
it is not profitable for the manufacturer and retailer to supply large quantities in the retail market, because intense competition
leads to cannibalization. Therefore, the manufacturer manages the retail market to enhance its profit and the retailerвҖҷs profit under
common ownership. From the discussion presented above, the following two effects are important when considering the effect of
common ownership on wholesale prices: (i) Increasing wholesale prices because the retailer raises quantity by emphasizing the
manufacturerвҖҷs profit, and (ii) decreasing wholesale prices to provide the market share to retailers. In the case of a small degree
of channel substitution, the wholesale price increases and the retailer increases the quantity as the degree of common ownership
increases. Therefore, each firm improves its profit under common ownership by increasing its profit. In this case, one can infer that
consumer surplus may decrease with increasing common ownership, because large common ownership leads to tacit collusion in
the product market by supply chain parties. However, this is not always true in our model. In the case of a small degree of channel
substitution, when the wholesale price increases, although the quantity supplied by the manufacturer decreases with increasing
common ownership, that supplied by the retailer increases. The retailer radically increases the quantity in the retail market by
reducing the quantity supplied by the manufacturer. Consequently, the total quantity supplied to the retail market increases as
common ownership increases. In our additional analysis, we check the robustness of this result by assuming a direct selling cost
for the manufacturer in Section 5.4. In addition, by using a numerical example, we confirm that our main results are presented in
Section 5.2.

These results represent important contributions to supply chain management practices and earlier studies from the perspective of
decision-making and policy-making. First, our results on retailer profits suggest that common ownership improves a retailerвҖҷs profit.

2. Related literature

Our contributions can be clarified through the earlier supply chain and common-ownership studies. Additionally, using the
ame theoretical approach, many supply chain management studies elucidate the effects of opening a direct channel by a
anufacturer (e.g., Chiang et al., 2003; Hamamura & Zennyo, 2021; Hsiao & Chen, 2013; Matsui & Tsunoda, 2022; Matsushima &
izuno, 2018; Mizuno, 2012; Tsay & Agrawal, 2004). In this context, Chiang et al. (2003) presents a traditional economic analysis of
dual-channel supply chain. Additionally, Hamamura and Zennyo (2021), Pei and Yan (2015), Yoon (2016), and Zhang et al. (2020)
se non-cooperative game theory to demonstrate the positive effects of spillover investments. From a managerial perspective, Matsui
nd Tsunoda (2022) considers the effects of exclusive territories on supply chain management. Additionally, Hua et al. (2010)
ncludes the assumption of a delivery lead-time for a manufacturer in a direct channel. Few studies claim that manufacturer
ncroachment positively affects a retailerвҖҷs profit. Only a few studies have examined the effects of common ownership on supply
hain management. We focus on the study that is closest to our model.

The effects of the dual-channel on the retailerвҖҷs profit and surplus were also investigated in previous studies (Arya et al., 2007; Sun
t al., 2019; Yoon, 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). Arya et al. (2007) demonstrates the bright side of manufacturer encroachment, which
nhances retailersвҖҷ profit. Additionally, Yoon (2016) describes that a manufacturerвҖҷs cost-reducing investment improves the retailerвҖҷs
rofits through the spillover effects of its investment. Furthermore, Zhang et al. (2020) considers demand-enhancing investments. Pei
nd Yan (2015) conducts supportive retailersвҖҷ services that coordinate the dual-channel distribution as an effective incentive. They
ssume that additional retail services positively impact demand. Hamamura and Zennyo (2021) describes the effects of a retailerвҖҷs
oluntary investment in cost reduction on manufacturer encroachment. In addition, Hsiao and Chen (2013) explores an asymmetric
hannel structure despite the ex-ante symmetry between manufacturers.

In the supply chain study, Aviv and Shamir (2021) is closest to our research. They also explore the impact of common ownership
n supply chain parties using non-cooperative game theory. However, while we assume common ownership between a manufacturer
nd retailer, Aviv and Shamir (2021) considers common ownership between horizontal retailers. In other words, while we shed light
n the impact of common ownership on the strategies of supply chain parties with supplier encroachment, Aviv and Shamir (2021)
ocus only on indirect channels through retailers, assuming that one manufacturer does not open the direct channel. Additionally,
hile Aviv and Shamir (2021) assumes asymmetric information between supply chain parties, we do not assume this to clarify the

mpact of common ownership on strategies and surplus. Moreover, assuming a two-echelon supply chain comprised of one assembler
nd multiple upstream suppliers, Xiao et al. (2021) considers backward shareholding among supply chain parties. However, they
o not examine the direct channels of the manufacturer.

Additionally, in the economics field, based on non-cooperative game theory, several findings in the study have described
conomic analyses of common (overlapping) ownership (e.g., Li & Zhang, 2021; LГіpez & Vives, 2019; Sato & Matsumura, 2020)
ecause common ownership is an important issue from the perspective of antitrust policy. Li and Zhang (2021) demonstrates the
dverse effects of common ownership on the consumer and total surplus in a duopoly, whereas Sato and Matsumura (2020) shows
hat increasing common ownership engenders decreased entry and may or may not improve total surplus. Therefore, common
wnership helps improve the total surplus under a particular economic environment. Additionally, LГіpez and Vives (2019) shows
hat considering R&D spillover, common ownership enhances consumer surplus and total surplus. Among these reports, few have
escribed studies demonstrating the positive aspects of common ownership on consumer surplus and total surplus with product
arket competition. Our study also assesses the positive effects of common ownership.

Empirical studies have indicated the importance of common ownership in supply chain management (Chen, 2021; Freeman,
021).7 In the recent study, Chen (2021) claims that common ownership facilitates innovation by improving relationships between
upply chain parties. Additionally, Freeman (2021) demonstrates that common ownership enhances innovative collaboration
y increasing R&D, patenting, and patent cross-citations. These empirical results indicate that common ownership has positive
anagement. While common ownership among supply chain parties influences decision-making in supply chain studies, Aviv and

hamir (2021) only examines its effects. Therefore, one can infer that some light must be shed on common ownership among supply
hain parties.8

. Conclusion

This study examines the effects of common ownership on supply chain management with the manufacturerвҖҷs encroachment
ecision. The results of our analyses demonstrate that given a large share of ownership by the manufacturer, the retailerвҖҷs profit is
mproved by manufacturer encroachment. Additionally, we demonstrate that increasing ownership share improves the total surplus
n a specific case. In our additional analysis, we confirm the robustness and extensibility of our model. In particular, an asymmetric

wnership share and the manufacturerвҖҷs direct selling cost have an important role in practice.

These results constitute important contributions to supply chain management practice and financial economics studies. First,
he result obtained for the retailerвҖҷs profit suggests that common ownership directly improves the retailerвҖҷs profit and indirectly
nhances the profit in the encroachment case. This result implies that the retailer should aggressively engage in common ownership
etween the retailer and manufacturer. Second, we can ascertain some positive aspects of common ownership regarding the total
urplus. Therefore, for supply chain management, society should accept common ownership by manufacturers and retailers.

We can consider the following future research based on our model. First, for our main model, we assume a monopoly case
y using one supply chain. Therefore, future research should include analyses incorporating the assumptions of a competitor in a
roduct market. Second, while this study considers only quantity competition, future research can consider cases that include price
ompetition. Third, recently, to explore optimal decision-making by supply chain parties, several studies assume that other kinds of
gents have a specific utility. In particular, risk-averse agents are supposed to analyze the supply chain coordination (e.g., Matsui,
019). Therefore, in future research, one can consider the effect of risk-averse agents under common ownership among supply chain
arties. While one can consider the above future studies based on our model, there are beyond our scope, and we propose a basic
nalysis of the impact of common ownership on the dual-channel supply chain.
