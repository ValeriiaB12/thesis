1. Introduction

Large, diversified institutional shareholders have become increasingly prominent in the United States’ financial markets. The ‘Big 
Three’ asset management firms – BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street – own substantial stakes in most publicly traded companies 
and consequently hold positions in firms that are natural product-market rivals.1 Such ‘common owners’ may wish to discourage 
competition and even foster collusion among their portfolio firms to extract rents above those implied by a competitive market 
equilibrium (Bresnahan and Salop, 1986; Reynolds and Snapp, 1986). This paper tests whether institutional investors influence ex
ecutive compensation in a manner consistent with achieving anti-competitive outcomes, specifically reducing relative performance 
evaluation (RPE), and finds clear evidence to the contrary.

Seminal work on contract theory contends that an agent’s performance should not be measured in a vacuum, but relative to the 
performance of peers that operate under similar circumstances (Lazear and Rosen, 1981; Holmstrom, 1982; Green and Stokey, 1983). 
This literature shows that principals and risk-averse agents can achieve Pareto improvement by incorporating RPE into compensation 
arrangements.2 However, the foundational theories supporting RPE assume that each principal’s utility is based solely on the

Indeed, theory shows that RPE can intensify competition and even incite destructive sabotage (Lazear, 1989; Gibbons and Murphy, 
1990). Recent empirical studies support this idea, consistently concluding that RPE heightens competition among rival firms.3 Since 
common owners have stakes in competing firms and could profit from collusion, they may seek to discourage or abolish RPE among 
their portfolio constituents.

This paper examines whether common owners reduce RPE and finds no evidence to support this across three distinct dimensions. 
First, firms with higher levels of common ownership are no less likely to use RPE in executive compensation. Second, common owners 
do not exhibit differential support for RPE in ‘say-on-pay’ shareholder referendums. Third, firms do not avoid benchmarking RPE 
against peers that share a common owner with them. In all settings, the point estimates for the effects of common ownership are 
statistically and economically small or oppositely signed, contradicting the hypothesis that common owners discourage RPE to make 
collusion incentive-compatible for their firms’ managers.

To test this theory, I begin with two independent variables at the firm-year level: Institutional Direct Ownership is the fraction of 
outstanding shares held by institutional investors, and Institutional Common Ownership is the fraction of other firms in the same industry 
that share an institutional blockholder in common with the firm.4 The first measure captures direct ownership by institutions with over 
$100 million in assets as reported to the SEC in Form 13F, and the second captures the extent to which these institutions also own the 
firm’s competitors. Using detailed data on executive compensation awards from Incentive Lab, I build a dummy variable, RPE, that 
flags if the firm-year uses RPE and a continuous variable, RPE%, that equals the dollar-weighted fraction of CEO pay linked to RPE 
awards.

I begin by regressing the RPE outcomes on the ownership measures. I find no effect of Institutional Direct Ownership on RPE, but 
surprisingly, I find a strong, positive effect of Institutional Common Ownership on RPE. These results are inconsistent with collusion, but 
because RPE increases competitive pressures, it is unclear why common ownership would increase RPE.

To understand why RPE loads positively on aggregate common ownership, I decompose the two ownership measures into those 
associated with the Big Three asset managers and those attributable to other institutional investors. The Big Three are central to the 
ongoing debate on common ownership because in addition to their diversified holdings, their voting power grants them considerable 
sway over corporate policies, including executive compensation.5 Additionally, the Big Three use dedicated stewardship teams to 
engage directly with portfolio companies and influence governance practices behind the scenes.6 The Big Three may demand RPE for 
its pay-for-performance properties, as their size uniquely positions them to hold managers accountable. Alternatively, they may 
advocate for RPE because, as large asset managers subject to significant scrutiny, they seek to avoid any appearance of collusion or 
conflicts of interest.

I define Big 3 Direct Ownership as the fraction of shares held by the Big Three asset managers, and I define Big 3 Common Ownership as 
the fraction of other firms in the same industry that share a Big Three blockholder in common with the firm. I build analogous measures 
for non-Big Three institutional investors: Other Direct Ownership and Other Common Ownership. Regressing RPE on these four refined 
measures produces a strong, positive coefficient on Big 3 Direct Ownership and insignificant coefficients on the remaining measures. The 
positive effects of the former are the opposite of what a collusion hypothesis would predict, and the statistically insignificant co
efficients on the latter are inconsistent with the idea that common owners discourage competition. The fact that Big 3 Direct Ownership 
loads positively while Big 3 Common Ownership is statistically indistinguishable from zero suggests that these asset managers favor RPE 
for its ability to align pay with performance, rather than to avoid apparent conflicts of interest among their commonly owned 
companies.

To establish causality and address the possibility that ownership and compensation are endogenously determined, I use an 
instrumental variables specification that exploits the fact that much of the Big Three’s holdings are mechanically determined by 
passive ownership that tracks major stock index lists. I instrument Big 3 Direct Ownership with a dummy variable that flags whether the 
firm is an S&P 500 index constituent, and I instrument Big 3 Common Ownership with a variable equal to the fraction of other firms in 
the same industry that are S&P 500 members. Since S&P 500 membership is primarily driven by firm size, I argue that these in
struments plausibly satisfy the exclusion restriction (see section 3.1 below).

presence of firm fixed effects, which satisfies the relevance condition. In 2SLS, the instrumented effects are consistent with the OLS

Next, I study how common ownership and RPE affect shareholder advisory votes on executive compensation known as ‘say-on-pay’. 
If common owners wish to facilitate collusion, they may use say-on-pay to voice support for compensation that does not include RPE. 
However, Appendix 2 reports an excerpt from Vanguard’s recent U.S. Proxy Voting Policy, in which Vanguard lists the alignment of 
pay and relative performance as the first consideration when evaluating executive compensation. Specifically, Vanguard states that it 
mainly assesses this through “analysis of three-year total shareholder return and realized pay over the same period vs. a relevant set of 
peer companies.” Including relative metrics in pay awards directly remedies this by increasing (decreasing) realized pay following 
outperformance (underperformance) of peers. Vanguard further states that a warning sign is raised if “The plan uses absolute metrics 
only.” Thus, these voting guidelines communicate a clear preference for RPE in executive pay, and funds do not typically deviate from 
their voting guidelines (Couvert, 2021).

I obtain firm-level say-on-pay vote tallies from ISS Voting Analytics and define Votes as the percentage of votes cast in favor of say- 
on-pay. Although BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street are inherently distinct institutions, their voting is significantly correlated due 
to factors including their overwhelming coincidence of interests (Bebchuk and Hirst, 2022), their shared philosophy concerning 
corporate governance (Bubb and Catan, 2022), and their overlapping investor ideology (Bolton et al., 2020). As such, I continue to 
focus on measures that separate the Big Three’s ownership (i.e., Big 3 Direct Ownership and Big 3 Common Ownership) from ownership by 
other institutional investors (i.e., Other Direct Ownership and Other Common Ownership). This approach is consistent with Bebchuk and 
Hirst (2022), who state, “As a result, it makes sense to aggregate the voting power of the Big Three in order to properly understand their 
power.”

I regress Votes on the four ownership measures and their interactions with the RPE variables. The hypothesis that the common 
owners support pay without RPE predicts negative coefficients on these interaction terms, but that is not what I find. Instead, the 
coefficient on Big 3 Direct Ownership × RPE is positive while the coefficients on the remaining interaction terms are statistically and 
economically insignificant. Taken together, the Big Three reveal a unique preference for RPE through their voting behavior, which is 
not mitigated by their common ownership, and other institutions appear indifferent to RPE in their votes. These results hold in 
alternative specifications that additionally interact the ownership measures with other dimensions of pay, including the log of total 
compensation and the proportion of pay delivered in stock and option grants.

Last, I test whether common owners attempt to circumvent the competitive effects of RPE by altering the composition of underlying 
award reference groups. Redesigning and recalibrating an entire compensation program to exclude RPE could be prohibitively 
challenging and costly. Instead, the common owners could simply pressure firms to avoid peer groups that pit their portfolio firms in 
direct contest with each other. This could manifest as benchmarking RPE against a general market index that is too broad to capture the 
gains from industry-level collusion or against a custom peer group that excludes commonly held rivals by design. In tests of the former, 
I find no evidence that common ownership alters benchmark group choice.

To test the latter, I build an annual firm-peer pair variable, RPE Peer, that flags if the benchmarking firm chooses the other firm as an 
RPE peer that year. I define pairwise variables, Big 3 Common Block and Other Common Block, as dummies that indicate if the two firms 
share one of the Big Three or one of the non-Big Three institutions in common as a blockholder, respectively. Regressing RPE Peer on 
these measures of common ownership produces small but positive effects for the former and insignificant effects for the latter, both 
inconsistent with avoiding competition. I then interact the common ownership measures as well as the direct ownership measures (at 
the benchmarking firm) with a measure of how closely the firms compete. To measure competition, I use the pairwise product sim
ilarity score developed by Hoberg and Phillips (2010, 2016), which measures the extent of product market overlap. Firm-pairs that 
score higher on this metric compete to offer similar goods and thus stand the most to gain from collusion. The coefficients of RPE Peer 
on Big 3 Direct Ownership × Competition are large and positive even in the presence of pairwise fixed effects. This implies that after a 
firm experiences an increase in Big Three ownership, the firm tends to benchmark RPE against closer competitors. Interacting 
Competition with Big 3 Common Block or the non-Big Three measures produces coefficients that are statistically near zero. Thus, 
common owners do not avoid RPE that rewards outperforming their other portfolio companies. Additionally, I define a market-based 
measure of competitive overlap using pairwise stock return correlations and find similar effects.

This paper contributes to the debate on the anticompetitive effects of common ownership by showing that common owners do not 
eschew RPE, which intensifies competition. The statistically insignificant results on common ownership and RPE align with those on 
common ownership and product market competition in Koch et al. (2021) and Lewellen and Lowry (2021). Although those papers do 
not study compensation, they carefully address various methodological issues and find no consistent anticompetitive effects of 
common ownership in the broad economy, in contrast to empirical literature that reaches mixed conclusions in narrower settings.7 My 
paper complements theirs by eliminating RPE as a potential channel through which common owners might attempt to achieve 
anticompetitive outcomes.

demonstrates the importance of disentangling the effects of common ownership from the direct effects of ownership by large 
stewardship-focused asset managers that happen to be common owners. By introducing this separation, my paper helps clarify results 
from Kwon (2016), who finds that common ownership is positively related to RPE, and Bloomfield et al. (2024), who find that common 
ownership and RPE are positively related or unrelated depending on specification.8 Specifically, my paper shows that the seemingly 
positive effect of All Common Ownership on RPE is in fact driven entirely by the Big Three’s direct ownership. Common ownership by 
the Big Three and other institutions has no discernable effect on RPE. My result that the Big Three’s direct ownership increases RPE 
corroborates the consensus of the extant compensation and governance literatures that institutional blockholders are competent 
monitors of executive activity and serve an important role in motivating and disciplining managers (Edmans and Holderness, 2016; 
Edmans et al., 2017).

Additionally, by testing for explicit RPE as disclosed in mandatory SEC regulatory filings, my paper contrasts with those that study 
implicit RPE, which only infers RPE through regression analysis.9 Papers that study common ownership and implicit RPE include Anton 
et al. (2016) and Liang (2016), who find that common ownership decreases implicit RPE, and Kwon (2016) who finds the opposite.10

Testing for implicit RPE requires assumptions including peer group composition, performance metrics, and payout style that may 
induce measurement error and cloud inference (Murphy, 1999; Bannister and Newman, 2003). Importantly, implicit tests will fail to 
detect RPE even if an executive’s entire realized pay is contingent upon it unless the target award value also covaries with peer 
performance.11 Using explicit RPE data from Incentive Lab allows me to observe contract details and conclude that common ownership 
is not negatively related to RPE.

Furthermore, studying explicit RPE allows me to observe variation in executive incentive design that is not captured by measures of 
executives’ wealth-performance sensitivity (WPS), which quantifies how executive wealth changes with firm value, as calculated by 
functions of stock- and option-based pay.12 Although Anton et al. (2023) find that common ownership lowers WPS in a manner that is 
potentially consistent with an anticompetitive effect, WPS inherently ignores whether the underlying pay is delivered as RPE-based 
awards. Cash-, stock-, and option-based awards may all be contingent upon or scale with relative performance, and thus WPS is 
unable to capture RPE or its competitive effects.13 My results show that the largest common owners – the Big Three – prefer RPE at their 
portfolio firms, intensifying competitive pressures, while other common owners do not have anticompetitive effects.

Last, this paper contributes to an emerging literature on how the Big Three shape corporate outcomes. The Big Three can potentially 
have large effects on firms’ policies because their large ownership stakes in portfolio firms grant them high propensities to be pivotal 
voters in shareholder votes (Bebchuk and Hirst, 2019; Griffin, 2020). Additionally, Brav et al. (2024) note that the Big Three have 
greater access to management through their prominent shareholder status and can shape firms’ outcomes through direct communi
cation. Azar et al. (2021) show that Big Three ownership reduces carbon emissions, consistent with the idea that their common 
ownership may help resolve certain collective action issues. Gormley et al. (2023) find that the Big Three’s activism efforts increased 
the number of female directors on boards following the #MeToo movement. To understand why the Big Three and other passive 
investors engage as shareholders, Lewellen and Lewellen (2022) demonstrate that these institutions derive substantial benefits from 
stock price appreciation, primarily through assets under management fees and fund flows generated by their activism. Brav et al. 
(2023) survey the literature on passive ownership and show that the benefits from engagement are higher for the Big Three than other 
institutions due to economies of scale. My paper adds to this literature by showing that Big Three ownership increases RPE use – while 
non-Big Three ownership does not – and importantly, common ownership does not offset this effect.

3. Data and empirical design

To test the idea that the common owners discourage RPE among portfolio firms, I develop hypotheses and build models in three 
distinct settings: (1) firm-level RPE use, (2) voting in say-on-pay referendums, and (3) RPE benchmark choice and peer selection.

3.1. RPE use

The paper’s core thesis is that if common owners wish to soften competition or encourage coordination among portfolio firms that 
are otherwise natural product market competitors, they will avoid compensation plans that explicitly reward executives for out
performing competitors. Formally: 
Hypothesis 1. If common owners discourage competition, firms that share a higher degree of common ownership with their competitors will 
be less likely to use RPE in executive compensation.

To test this hypothesis, I obtain detailed data on explicit RPE in executive incentive awards from ISS Incentive Lab.15 This database 
contains the compensation items reported in annual proxy statements for the largest 750 public firms in the United States economy. As 
the set of firms can change from year to year, Incentive Lab back- and forward-fills the data once a firm is ranked within the top 750. 
Merging with BoardEx, Compustat, CRSP, and Thomson/Refinitiv produces a panel of 11,516 firm-years covering 1375 unique firms 
from 2010 to 2021, with nearly 1000 firms per year.16

Incentive Lab reports compensation awards granted to firms’ top executives as disclosed in SEC filings. I focus on performance- 
based compensation awards (as opposed to unrestricted or time-based grants) where the payout is contingent upon achieving 
measurable goals, which Incentive Lab classifies as absolute or relative. Payouts from absolute goals are determined by the firm’s

fraction of shares owned by Blackrock, Vanguard, and State Street, and I define Other Direct Ownership as the fraction of shares owned

industry, while this fraction is only 11 % for other investors.

growth of RPE and the Big Three’s measures challenges the notion that common owners’ expanding influence has produced anti
competitive effects. If these asset managers were softening competition, RPE usage would be expected to decline as their ownership 
increased.

cause S&P 500 turnover are float-adjusted market capitalization, a float-adjusted stock liquidity ratio, and trailing four-quarter

major IPOs, continued inclusion of current constituents following their acquisition by non-constituent firms, and delayed deletion of 
longtime constituents that might only violate the inclusion criteria temporarily. Additionally, although S&P 500 membership could 
affect RPE if other investors who prefer it tend to target firms in that index, such activists often act in at least indirect coordination with 
large passive investors such as the Big Three (Appel et al., 2019). Thus, Firm in S&P 500 likely satisfies the excludability condition.

My second instrument, Industry in S&P 500, is the fraction of other firms in the same industry that are members of the S&P 500. This 
measure identifies plausibly exogenous variation in the extent of the Big Three’s common ownership of the firm and its competitors, Big 
3 Common Ownership. Since this instrument identifies variation through other firms entering or exiting the S&P 500, it is improbable 
that it could directly affect the firm’s governance choices including executive compensation design. Even if a rival’s inclusion in the 
S&P 500 was a factor in the firm’s pay-setting process, it would only feasibly affect RPE in whether the firm names the rival as a 
benchmark peer. That is, even if a firm is differentially inclined to benchmark RPE against rivals in the S&P 500, it is unlikely that it 
would affect the firm’s use of RPE as a component of pay. Thus, Industry in S&P 500 is also likely excludable.

First-stage regressions confirm these instruments exhibit strong relationships with their respective endogenous variables even in 
the presence of firm fixed effects. I estimate two-stage least-squares (2SLS) instrumental variables models of Eq. (1). The 2SLS models 
show that the Big Three increase RPE at the firms they own, and their common ownership does not counteract this effect.

3.2. Say-on-pay votes

My second set of tests investigates ‘say-on-pay’ advisory shareholder votes on executive compensation. These votes serve as a 
referendum where shareholders express their approval or disapproval of executive pay packages. Although say-on-pay votes are 
nonbinding, dissent often produces changes to executive compensation design resulting in increased pay-to-performance sensitivity 
(Ferri and Maber, 2013; Correa and Lel, 2016).

If common owners aim to soften competition by reducing RPE, I hypothesize that they will use say-on-pay to voice their support for 
compensation packages that do not contain relative pay elements. Formally: 
Hypothesis 2. If common owners discourage competition, firms that share a higher degree of common ownership with their competitors will 
receive a higher percentage of votes in favor of compensation in say-on-pay referendums when they incorporate less explicit RPE-based incentive 
pay into executive compensation contracts.

To test Hypothesis 2, I obtain firm-level say-on-pay vote tallies from ISS Voting Analytics and define Votes as the percentage of votes 
cast in favor of say-on-pay.22 As Table 2 reports, the average say-on-pay proposal receives 90 % support. Although most votes receive 
high support as evidenced by the 25th percentile of 89 %, there is significant variation in the left tail as the standard deviation is over 
12 %age points.

3.3. Peer selection

My third set of tests investigate the possibility that the common owners circumvent RPE’s competitive effects by altering firms’ RPE 
benchmark groups. Each RPE goal lists the underlying benchmark group used as the basis of comparison when evaluating relative 
performance. Using Incentive Lab, I classify these groups into three categories: a peer group (e.g. panel C of Table 1), an industry- 
specific index (e.g. the Dow Jones U.S. Banks Index), or a broad market index (e.g. the Russell 1000). If the common owners wish 
to discourage competition while using RPE in compensation, they may choose to benchmark against a market index that is too broad to 
capture gains from industry-level collusion or against a custom peer group that is designed to exclude commonly held competitors. 
That is, they will be less inclined to benchmark against an industry-specific index because that would incentivize managers to compete 
more fiercely against firms in their own industries.

Hypothesis 3a. If common owners discourage competition, firms that share a higher degree of common ownership with their competitors will

I estimate Eq. (1) with these three measures as dependent variables. If common owners use RPE in a manner that softens 
competition, then the β-coefficients on ownership and common ownership measures will be positive for the Peer Group or Market Index 
outcomes, and negative for the Industry Index outcome. However, I find no evidence of this.

Second, I study whether the common owners tailor firms’ custom RPE peer groups to avoid pitting their portfolio constituents 
against each other. The idea is that even if common owners encourage the use of RPE, they might pressure firms to remove commonly 
owned rivals from their peer groups to limit competition. Formally: 
Hypothesis 3b. If common owners discourage competition, firms will be less likely to include rivals in their RPE peer groups when they share 
a common owner.

To test Hypothesis 3b, I construct a dataset of firm-peer pairs and define the binary variable RPE Peer as equal to 1 if the bench
marking firm names the other in its RPE peer group that year and 0 if the firm is not named. For practicality and tractability, I restrict 
the counterfactual peers to those in the same 1-digit SIC code as the benchmarking firm since firms predominately select RPE peers 
from their industry (e.g. Gong et al., 2011).

Table 3 reports summary statistics at the firm-peer-year level. The average peer group contains 11.274 firms. While peer groups are 
fairly consistent from year to year, they are not static. On average, a firm adds 1.238 new peer firms to the group each year and removes

% (=0.854/11.274). This dynamic suggests that adjustments to RPE peer groups, though modest, occur regularly and could provide a 
strategic mechanism for influential stakeholders to drive change. An influential common owner might exert pressure on firms to drop a 
commonly held firm from the RPE peer group, particularly if the two firms are fierce competitors. For instance, as Feichter et al. (2022)
note, the Big Three may influence changes in their portfolio firms through “hidden power” via private engagements with management.

Moreover, narrowly adjusting the peer group is arguably more feasible than attempting a full redesign of a compensation program 
to exclude RPE. Compensation programs typically consist of finely tuned short-term and long-term goals, require board ratification, 
and are subject to an advisory shareholder vote. The fixed costs associated with a comprehensive redesign can be substantial, including 
consulting fees, legal and compliance-related expenses, and the costs of stakeholder engagement. Thus, excluding a commonly held 
competitor from the RPE group is potentially a cost-effective path of least resistance to discourage competition.

4.3. Peer selection

Table 7 presents results from regressing Relative to Peer Group, Relative to Industry Index, and Relative to Market Index on the 
measures of institutions’ direct and common ownership, similar to Eq. (1). The effects observed in this analysis are quite muted. The 
coefficients on Big 3 Direct Ownership, Big 3 Common Ownership, and Other Common Ownership are insignificant in all columns. Other 
Direct Ownership is weakly negatively related to Industry Index. Overall, the results suggest that these institutions are unlikely to alter 
RPE reference group choice at the firms they own, and there is no evidence that common ownership has any effect.

Table 8 investigates the possibility that investors alter peer groups to exclude commonly held competitors as in Eq. (3). Column (1) 
reports a small yet positive effect at the 10 % level of significance of Big 3 Common Block on RPE peer selection. This implies that firms 
are, if anything, more likely to select peers with whom they share a Big Three blockholder, which is contrary to the idea that the Big 
Three avoid pitting their portfolio firms against each other. Also in Column (1), the direct effects of the two competitiveness measures, 
Product Similarity and Return Correlation (in Panels A and B, respectively), are positive, meaning that firms benchmark against peers 
that are closer rivals in terms of product market space and economic exposure.

The remaining columns reveal that the effects attributed to the competitiveness measures are largely due to the Big Three’s 
presence. Introducing the interactions of the direct and common ownership with Product Similarity and Return Correlation produces 
large effects on their interactions with Big 3 Direct Ownership and causes their direct effects to shrink to near zero. In Columns (2) and 
(6), the interaction terms involving Big 3 Direct Ownership are statistically positive, while Product Similarity and Return Correlation have 
negligible effects. These results suggest that the Big Three influence pairwise peer selection by encouraging the firms they own to 
benchmark performance-contingent pay against closely related rivals.

Furthermore, the coefficients on the interaction terms involving Big 3 Common Block and Other Common Block are statistically 
indifferent from zero. For example, the point estimate on Big 3 Common Block × Product Similarity in Column (2) of Panel A is 0.031 and 
its standard error is 0.022. The one-sided lower 95 % bound on this estimate is − 0.004 (=0.031–1.645 × 0.022), which means that a 
one-sigma increase in Product Similarity produces a small 0.15 percentage point reduction in peer selection probability (= − 0.004 ×
0.331) at firms with a Big 3 Common Block, or about 2.3 % of the sample mean (=0.0015/0.065). Alternatively, the same lower bound 
on Big 3 Common Block × Return Correlation is − 0.003 (=0.000–1.645 × 0.002), which predicts an even smaller effect in conjunction 
with Return Correlation’s smaller standard deviation. Interpreting the effect of Other Common Block × Product Similarity in Column (4) 
of Panel A, the lower 95 % confidence bound is − 0.017 (=0.029–1.645 × 0.028), where a one-sigma increase in Other Common Block 
corresponds to a 0.3 % (= − 0.017 × 0.192) reduction in peer selection probability, and the effect in Panel B is even smaller.

I conclude that common ownership does not materially affect pairwise peer selection. This statistically insignificant non-result is 
inconsistent with the hypothesis that institutional investors discourage competition among their portfolio companies.

4.4. Robustness tests

This section outlines several sets of robustness tests that corroborate the paper’s main results and, for brevity, are reported in the 
Internet Appendix. Please see the Internet Appendix for complete discussions of these tests. First, I adopt the modified Herfindahl- 
Hirschman Index developed by O’Brien and Salop (2000) and use ΔMHHI – which captures common ownership by all institutional 
owners – as the measure of common ownership. Second, I use alternative industry specifications based on product descriptions by 
Hoberg and Phillips (2010, 2016). Third, I use an alternative definition of the common ownership based on relative portfolio weights 
as in Harford et al. (2011). Last, I estimate nonlinear alternatives to the paper’s linear regression models. In all cases, the results 
support the main paper. Altogether, I find no evidence that common owners reduce RPE in executive compensation.

5. Conclusion

The Big Three asset management firms have significant economic power in U.S. capital markets, accounting for over 20 % of 
holdings at most firms in recent years (Fig. 1). These institutions have been at the center of a debate on antitrust regulations, as they 
regularly command stakes in natural product market competitors and could gain from collusion. Scholars including Elhauge (2016, 
2021) and Posner et al. (2016) have recommended antitrust enforcement actions to limit common ownership such as divestitures or 
prohibiting funds from owning multiple stocks within concentrated industries.

My paper looks for evidence of anticompetitive behavior in executive compensation design and tests whether the Big Three reduce 
incentive programs that reward executives for outperforming their rivals. In contrast, I find that the Big Three encourage RPE at the 
firms they own to align pay with performance despite its known competitive effects. Their common ownership does not diminish this 
propensity, and my statistical estimates of these effects are tightly within the neighborhood of zero. My results do not support the 
hypothesis that the Big Three have anti-competitive effects and suggest that antitrust intervention may be imprudent.

Appendix A. Variable definitions

Relative Performance Evaluation
RPE: A dummy variable that indicates if the firm uses relative performance evaluation as a component of CEO compensation [Incentive Lab]
RPE%: The dollar-weighted fraction of the CEO’s pay that is linked to relative performance awards [Incentive Lab]

Ownership and Instruments
Institutional Direct Ownership: The fraction of the firm’s stock owned by institutional investors [Thomson Reuters]
Institutional Common Ownership: The fraction of same-industry firms that share at least one institutional investors as a blockholder with the firm [Thomson 
Reuters]
Big 3 Direct Ownership: The fraction of the firm’s stock owned by BlackRock, State Street, and Vanguard [Thomson Reuters]
Big 3 Common Ownership: The fraction of same-industry firms that share at least one of the Big Three as a blockholder with the firm [Thomson Reuters]
Other Direct Ownership: The fraction of the firm’s stock owned by institutional investors, excluding BlackRock, State Street, and Vanguard [Thomson Reuters]
Other Common Ownership: The fraction of same-industry firms that share at least one non-Big Three institutional investor as a blockholder with the firm 
[Thomson Reuters]
Firm in S&P 500: A dummy variable that indicates that the firm is in the S&P 500 [CRSP]
Industry in S&P 500: The fraction of same-industry firms that are in the S&P 500 [CRSP]
Industry ΔMHHI: The incremental increase in industry concentration attributable to common ownership as in O’Brien and Salop (2000) [CRSP]
Industry HHI: Herfindahl-Hirschman Index of industry concentration [CRSP]

Firm Characteristics
Industry Exposure: The correlation of the firm’s stock returns with the industry’s return over the prior year [CRSP]
Stock Return: The firm’s total stockholder return including dividends [CRSP]
Return-on-Assets: Net income divided by total assets [Compustat]
Book-to-Market: Book value of equity divided by market value of equity [Compustat]
Leverage: Total debt divided by assets [Compustat]
log MCAP: Natural log of market capitalization [Compustat]
Multi-segment: A dummy if the firm is a multi-segment conglomerate [Compustat]

Executive Characteristics
CEO/Chair Duality: A dummy variable if the CEO is chairperson of the board [BoardEx, Incentive Lab]
General Ability Index: The extent to which a CEO has general managerial skills, in contrast to specialist skills, as in Custódio et al. (2013) [BoardEx]
Executive Age: The CEO’s age [BoardEx, Incentive Lab]
Executive Tenure: The number of years the CEO has been in that position [BoardEx, Incentive Lab]

Say-on-Pay
Votes: The fraction of votes in support of the firm’s executive compensation program, defined as votes in favor divided by the prescribed base, which is either 
votes in favor plus votes against, votes in favor plus votes against plus abstentions, or the number of outstanding shares [ISS Voting Analytics]
log Total Compensation: The natural log of the total fair value of CEO compensation [Incentive Lab]
Pct Stock-based Pay: The dollar-weighted fraction of the CEO’s pay that is delivered in stock-based or option-based pay [Incentive Lab]

Benchmark Group
Relative to Peer Group: A dummy variable that indicates if the firm benchmarks RPE relative to a peer group [Incentive Lab]
Relative to Industry Index: A dummy variable that indicates if the firm benchmarks RPE relative to an industry index such as the S&P 500 Industrials Index 
[Incentive Lab]
Relative to Market Index: A dummy variable that indicates if the firm benchmarks RPE relative to an industry index such as the S&P 500 Index [Incentive Lab]

Peer Selection
Product Similarity: The Hoberg and Phillips (2010, 2016) pairwise text-based product market similarity score from 10 K product descriptions [https:// 
hobergphillips.tuck.dartmouth.edu/]
Return Correlation: Pairwise stock return correlation between the firm and the potential peer [CRSP]
Similar Variable: A dummy variable that indicates that the firm and the peer are within one sample standard deviation of each other for a variety of metrics 
(MCAP, Leverage, etc.) [Compustat]

Appendix B. Excerpt of voting guidelines

This appendix presents an excerpt from Vanguard’s 2024 U.S. Proxy Voting Policy as it pertains to 
advisory votes on executive compensation. The excerpt shows that Vanguard’s policy exhibits a 
preference for the use of RPE in executive incentives. Source: https://corporate.vanguard.com/ 
content/dam/corp/advocate/investment-stewardship/pdf/policies-and-reports/us_proxy_voting_ 
policy_2024.pdf

Advisory votes on executive compensation (Say on Pay) 
Because norms and expectations vary by industry type, company size, company age, and 
geographic location, the following guidelines illustrate elements of effective executive 
compensation plans and are not a “one-size-fits-all” tool. 
A fund’s considerations when evaluating executive pay fall into three broad categories:   

Alignment of pay and performance. The funds look for evidence of clear alignment between pay 
outcomes and company performance. This is mainly assessed through alignment of incentive targets 
with corporate strategy and analysis of three-year total shareholder return and realized pay over the 
same period vs. a relevant set of peer companies. If there are concerns that pay and performance are not 
aligned, a fund may vote against a pay-related proposal.
Compensation plan structure. Plan structures should be aligned with the company’s stated long-term 
strategy and should support pay-for-performance alignment. Where a plan includes structural issues 
which the funds determine have led to, or could in the future lead to, pay-for-performance 
misalignment, a fund may vote against a pay-related proposal. For compensation structures which are 
not typical of a market, the Vanguard-advised funds look for specific disclosure demonstrating how the 
structure supports long-term value creation for shareholders.
Governance of compensation plans. The funds look for boards to have a clear strategy and philosophy on 
executive pay, utilize robust processes to evaluate and evolve executive pay plans, and implement 
executive pay plans responsive to shareholder feedback over time. The funds also look for boards to 
explain these matters to shareholders via company disclosures. Where pay-related proposals consis
tently receive low support, the funds look for boards to demonstrate responsiveness to shareholder 
concerns.
A fund will vote case by case on executive compensation proposals (including Say on Pay, 
compensation reports, and compensation policies) and in general will support those that enhance long- 
term shareholder value. It may also vote for compensation proposals that reflect improvements in 
compensation practices in the interests of long-term shareholder value creation, even if the proposals 
are not perfectly aligned with all these guidelines.
While a fund will not be prescriptive as to exact structure of a compensation plan, it will seek structures 
and processes that can reasonably be expected to align pay and performance over time. Such structures 
may include a meaningful portion of equity vesting on performance criteria, strategically aligned 
performance metrics set to rigorous goals, and clear disclosure of the program and outcomes enabling 
shareholders to understand the connection to long-term shareholder value, among other factors. A 
fund does not look for nonfinancial metrics (such as environmental, social, and governance [ESG] 
metrics) to be a standard component of all compensation plans. When compensation committees 
choose to include nonfinancial metrics, the funds look for the same qualities the funds do with more 
traditional metrics, such as rigor, disclosure, and alignment with key strategic goals and/or material 
risks.
The following situations are among those that raise a higher level of concern related to a compensation 
plan: 
• Pay outcomes are significantly higher than those of peers but total shareholder return is well below 

that of peers.
• The long-term plan makes up less than 50 % of total pay.
• The long-term plan has a performance period of less than three years.
• Plan targets are reset or retested or are not rigorous.
• The target for total pay is set above the peer-group median.
The following situations are among those that raise warning signs, or a moderate level of concern:   

• The peer group used to benchmark pay is not comparably aligned with the company in size or 
strategy.

• The plan uses absolute metrics only.
• The plan allows for positive discretion only.
• The company uses one-time (e.g., retention) awards.
• The disclosure related to plan structure or payout is limited.

Where these warning signs exist, elements of strong compensation governance, such as board 
responsiveness and disclosure that includes data, rationale, and alternatives considered, can 
sometimes serve to mitigate these concerns.
