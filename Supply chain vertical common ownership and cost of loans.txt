1. Introduction

Supplier-customer relations are pivotal in the real sector, especially when transactions involve incomplete contracts and
relationship-specific investments, leading to the hold-up problem. This issue, as noted by Grossman and Hart (1986), results in po-
tential exploitation and underinvestment. The problem intensifies under the current complex global scenario, characterized by dis-
rupted supply chains and rising protectionism. An illustrative case is the U.S. port crisis, where container pile-ups due to unclaimed
cargo have spiked cargo costs, negatively impacting suppliers’ investments. Vertical integration could resolve this, but its feasibility is
limited by merger risks, high costs, and anti-monopoly laws. Therefore, it is crucial to explore alternative mechanisms that enhance
cooperation and reduce opportunism and hold-up risks in supply chains.

Traditional economic theory posits that firms are self-interested, aiming to maximize their value (Smith, 1937). This assumes that
major shareholders do not have significant stakes in competing firms. However, recent studies suggest that institutional investors, who
aim to maximize their entire portfolio’s value, can coordinate behaviors among commonly owned firms. Harford et al. (2011) found
that most institutional investors in S&P 500 companies prioritize overall portfolio value over individual firm value. Similarly, Park

In this study, we delve into the effects of vertical common ownership on supply chain hold-up issues and its subsequent influence on
stakeholder perceptions, with a specific emphasis on resolving agency conflicts between companies and their creditors. Corporate
loans are a predominant source of financing, comprising a significant portion of U.S. corporate debts. By considering creditors as
primary stakeholders, we aim to understand how vertical common ownership influences the dynamics in a crucially important creditor
relationship, offering a clearer understanding of the broader financial implications of these ownership structures in corporate finance.
Our core argument is that, vertical common ownership can i) coordinate the supply chain partners, to maximize their total investment
portfolio value; and ii) effectively monitor corporate behaviors, and lower adverse selection and moral hazard issues. Such an influence
can affect not only the supplier or customer firm itself, but also the attitude and decisions of outside corporate stakeholders. Thus, as an
important outside stakeholder, creditors will perceive vertical common ownership as a positive signal, and further lower their cost of
loans.

Noting that we study the role of vertical common ownership on the supplier side. This is mainly because we develop the hypothesis
based on hold-up problems and relationship-specific investments. Generally, the relationship-specific investment is provided by the
supplier firm to a specific customer firm, after which the supplier is more vulnerable to the customer’s rent extraction (Grossman and
Hart, 1986). Therefore, we argue that vertical common ownership is more beneficial for supplier firms considering their weaker
position in the relationship, rather than customers. In addition, we test the main hypotheses based on the supplier-customer dyad level
in the robustness check section. However, the negative relationship may not be held according to the collusion hypothesis and private
information sharing hypothesis, which remains an empirical issue.

Using a sample of 7005 facility observations from the year 1986 to 2017, we empirically test the impact of vertical common
ownership and the cost of loans. In line with our prediction, there is a negative association between the common ownership of a
supplier with its customers and the cost of loans. In robustness analysis, we find similar results in other loan pricing variables (i.e.,
longer loan maturities, greater loan size, number of lenders, and lower total cost of borrowing). Further cross-sectional tests are in
accordance with the coordination and monitoring channels. The results are robust under a battery of robustness checks. We also argue
that the fraction of customer sales and the institutional investors’ share ownership are likely to influence the likelihood of vertical
common ownership. Our results consistently hold under the Heckman analysis.

This study makes the following potential contributions. First, from the supply chain perspective, this study provides new evidence
on common ownership literature. As the primary role of common ownership is to foster collaboration, the supply chain is an ideal
venue to examine such a topic, though less attention has been paid to this area. Extant studies mostly focus on the common institutional
ownership of industry peers (i.e., horizontal common ownership) and find mixed evidence on whether such ownership improves peer
cooperation. Most of the prior literature has found a positive relationship between common ownership and cooperation among in-
dustry peers. In contrast, Lewellen and Lowry (2021) document that this type of relationship is mainly driven by other factors, such as
the 2008 financial crisis. There are also controversies such as the anti-competitiveness effect, limited attention hypothesis, collusion
hypothesis, etc. The coordination role of vertical common ownership is essentially different from that of horizontal one; whereas the
former improves total utility, the latter is welfare-reducing due to its anti-competitiveness. In this paper, we examine the creditor’s
perceptions of common ownership between supply chain partners (rather than competing peers within an industry). Empirical results
validate the positive role of vertical common ownership in coordinating and monitoring supply chain partners. Results still hold when
controlling for horizontal common ownership and institutional shareholdings, which indicates the incremental effect of vertical
common ownership and complements prior studies.

Second, we extend the literature on how supply chain relations affect creditors’ decision-making. Existing studies document that
corporate internal governance and external monitoring can affect the financial decisions of creditors (e.g., Álvarez-Botas and González,
2021; Campello et al., 2011; Francis et al., 2017; Valta, 2012).1 However, only few of these studies have ever considered the supply
chain relationship, which find that durable supply chain relationships (Cen et al., 2016; Croci et al., 2021) and lower customer
concentration (Campello and Gao, 2017) lead to lower cost of loans. As we know, this study is the first to connect supply chain vertical
common ownership to the cost of loans. Through the coordination and monitoring mechanisms, vertical common ownership reduces
creditors’ perceptions of supply chain risk significantly, which adds to the existing literature on how supply chain relations affect
financial decisions made by creditors.

Lastly, the results of this study potentially mitigate the apprehensions of regulators and policymakers about common ownership.

challenges these assertions (Azar et al., 2018; Dennis et al., 2022; Lewellen and Lowry, 2021) or highlights the detrimental effects of

2. Literature and hypothesis

2.1. Prior studies on common ownership

Common ownership occurs when an institutional investor holds shares in multiple firms concurrently. This ownership structure is
characterized by the investors’ aim to maximize the overall value of their combined holdings, rather than focusing on individual firm
values (Hansen and Lott, 1996; Gilje et al., 2020). Consequently, common investors tend to coordinate the behavior of the commonly
owned firms. Most studies on common ownership focus on commonly owned industry peers and find mixed evidence. He and Huang
(2017) observe that firms with commonly owned industry peers have better operating performance, consistent with the coordination
argument. Azar et al. (2018) document that common ownership reduces price competition and allows airlines to charge higher prices.
Ni and Yin (2023) find that common ownership decreases a firm’s cost of capital by lowering product market competition. Brooks et al.
(2018) present evidence that common ownership not only increases the likelihood of mergers and acquisitions but also improves post-
deal performance and decreases the probability of unsuccessful deals. Park et al. (2019) argue that commonly owned firms face lower
proprietary costs and are more inclined to disclose information voluntarily, including management earnings forecasts. Conversely,
Harford et al. (2011) find that common ownership matters little in firm acquisition decisions, which is difficult to reconcile with the
point that common ownership accounts for the lack of shareholder resistance to bad firm behaviors. Lewellen and Lowry (2021)
contest the link between common ownership and firm coordination, suggesting that this relationship is not robust and could be
influenced by other factors.

Furthermore, previous studies have highlighted the role of common ownership in monitoring and knowledge sharing, attributed to
their informational advantage. Chen et al. (2021) find that cross-owners in multiple same-industry firms possess informational ben-
efits, such as broader access to information and industry expertise. Gao et al. (2019) document that this informational advantage under
common ownership fosters knowledge exchange among firms, thus bolstering corporate innovation. He et al. (2020) and Ram-
alingegowda et al. (2021) observed that common ownership reduces firms’ earnings management through improved monitoring.
Edmans et al. (2019) discover that common ownership enhances corporate governance across different industries through both active
engagement and disinvestment strategies, supporting the monitoring hypothesis. Additionally, He et al. (2024) provide evidence that
the common ownership between borrower firms and financial banks can alleviate information frictions and decrease the firm’s loan
spreads, which arise from the information dissemination within the portfolios.

According to existing literature, less attention has been paid to firms with commonly owned vertical partners, where cooperation
between suppliers and their customers is a major issue. Some notable exceptions are Freeman (2023) and Chen (2021), both of which
find that supplier firms are more willing to undertake innovative activities when they have commonly owned customer firms, which is
consistent with institutional investors’ ability to play a coordinating role and thus reduce supplier firms’ perception of hold-up risk.
Based on social network theory, Cheung et al. (2020) document that vertical common ownership increases suppliers’ operating and
market performance.

2.2. Hypotheses development

Prior financial literature argues that default risk is the key lending risk faced by creditors and is one of the fundamental factors of
loan pricing (e.g., Merton, 1974; Demiroglu and James, 2010). Higher default risk may induce higher cost of loans and stricter loan
contracts in order to alleviate agency conflicts between the borrowers and lenders. This relationship is underscored by agency theory,
which posits that loan covenants can mitigate agency problems between creditors and shareholders (Jensen and Meckling, 2019;
Myers, 1977).2 In this article, we argue that common ownership along the supply chain can lower creditors’ perception of corporate
default risk and thus reduce loan spreads of supplier companies because of the coordination and monitoring channels.

First, vertical common ownership can coordinate the behaviors of supply chain partners, lower hold-up problems, and promote a
stable supply-customer relationship. When institutional investors hold multiple firms, investors aim to maximize their investments’
total portfolio value, rather than the value of individual firms (He and Huang, 2017). In this sense, vertical common investors are
incentivized to coordinate the behaviors of their portfolio firms (i.e., supplier and customer). Cen et al. (2016) show that long-term
supply chain relationships can act as a reputation signal, which helps diminish informational disparities between enterprises and
creditors, and lowers corporate loan costs. Campello and Gao (2017) document that companies with fewer hold-up problems may lead
to lower default risk and lower cost of loans. Similar findings are shown in Dhaliwal et al. (2016). Building on these studies, we argue

that due to the strengthened supply chain relationships created by vertical common ownership, customer firms are less likely to be
opportunistic, and suppliers will seldom be held up, which lowers the hold-up risk and default risk perceived by creditors. Thus, we
expect a negative relation between vertical common ownership and suppliers’ cost of loans.

Second, in addition to their coordination role, vertical common shareholders have the motivation and ability to perform effective
monitoring. Institutional investors can effectively monitor corporate behaviors, thus improving the provision of corporate information
and quality of financial reporting and reducing information asymmetry (Appel et al., 2016). Such monitoring ability is intensified
when institutional investors are block-holders of multiple firms simultaneously. In addition, for institutional investors who are
shareholders of suppliers and their customers simultaneously, the externalities of one side’s behaviors are internalized by the other
partner they hold, affecting the total portfolio values. Consequently, common owners have stronger motivation in monitoring
corporate misbehaviors. And the enhanced monitoring may increase corporate information transparency and promote better corporate
governance (He et al., 2019). To the extent that the vertical common institutional investors’ monitoring increases, creditors perceive
companies with lower operation and information risk, and their concerns about adverse selection and moral hazard in loan contracts
are alleviated. Therefore, creditors will provide a reduction in loan pricing.

In brief, vertical common ownership provides new information to creditors, helps improve their confidence in supply chain re-
lationships, and mitigates their concerns about managers’ opportunistic behaviors, which further lowers the cost of loans.

Nevertheless, the negative influence on the cost of loans may not be significantly observed. First, based on the collusion hypothesis,
supply chain partners may collude for their own benefit but harm the external stakeholders. In such a situation, the monitoring effect of
common investors may be undermined due to their resource extraction incentives (Raman and Shahrur, 2008). The common investors
are given both opportunities and abilities to extract resources for their own interests (Matvos and Ostrovsky, 2008) and expropriate the
wealth from creditors. In this sense, creditors may view vertical common ownership negatively and increase loan pricing. Second,
according to the private information sharing hypothesis, customers will reduce the demand for the suppliers’ public information when
they have better access to suppliers’ private information (Crawford et al., 2020). Since common institutional investors can promote
information sharing (Freeman, 2023), customers know more information through their common owners. In this regard, considering
the proprietary costs and disclosing costs, suppliers do not need to disclose such information, and the public information on which the
creditors can rely becomes less. Thus, creditors may compensate for the higher cost of loans. The last reason is due to the possible
incapability of creditors. It is unclear whether creditors are able to observe and process the vertical common ownership information
effectively and whether or not they regard it as a pricing factor.

Therefore, the baseline hypothesis can be stated as a null hypothesis:
Hypothesis: Others being equal, there is no relationship between vertical common ownership with major customers and a supplier’s cost of

loans.

3. Research design

3.1. Measure of vertical common ownership

We construct four variables to capture vertical common ownership (He and Huang, 2017; He et al., 2019). The first independent
variable, CO_Firms, is the natural log of one plus the number of the firm’s customers that are co-owned with the supplier firm. The
second independent variable, CO_Firms_MV, is the natural log of one plus the market value of the firm’s customers that are co-owned
with the supplier firm. The third independent variable is COwn_#, which equals the natural log of one plus the number of institutional
investors who are shareholders of both the supplier firm and its major customers. The fourth independent variable is Avg_Investors,
which is the number of vertical common institutional investors scaled by the number of total institutional investors in the firm. We set
5 % as the shares’ cutoff for common institutional owners, in order that the investors can exert enough influence on firm decisions. The
institutional shareholding data from Thomson Reuters 13F are provided at the quarter level. Since stakeholders may evaluate the
company over a period of time, we average the quarter common ownership measurements into the most adjacent fiscal year to which
the loan facility belongs.

3.2. Measure of cost of loans

As bank loans remain a predominant source of corporate financing, comprising approximately two-thirds of U.S. corporate debts (e.
g., Carey and Nini, 2007; Houston and James, 1996),3 we use loan spread (Spread) as the main measurement of the cost of loans in our
empirical analysis. Spread is the natural log value of the all-in spread drawn, charged by the bank over LIBOR for the drawn portion of
the loan facility, as reported in the LPC DealScan’s facility-level data. In our robustness analysis, we also investigate the effects on loan
maturity (Maturity), loan amount (LoanSize), and the number of lenders in a loan facility (NoLenders). Additionally, we use total cost of
borrowing for complementary analysis (Berg et al., 2016).

3.3. Regression model

Following Campello and Gao (2017) and Cohen et al. (2014), regression model is shown in eq. (1).

Spreadi,k,t = α+ β×COi,t + γʹFirmControlsi,t + δʹLoanControlsk,t + Loan Purpose FE+ Loan Type FE+Year FE+ Firm FE+ εi,k,t (1)

Here, the subscripts i, k, and t denote firm i, loan facility k, and year t, respectively. The dependent variable, Spread, represents the
loan spread, which is the most common measurement of the cost of loans.4 CO captures firms’ common ownership status. We use four
common ownership variables, namely CO_Firms, CO_Firms_MV, COwn_#, and Avg_Investor.

For control variables, firm-level controls include firm size (SIZE), leverage (LEV), market to book (MTB), ROA, Tangibility, cash flow
volatility (CFO_VOL), Altman (1968) Z-Score (Z_Score), and bond rating dummy (Rating). In addition, loan characteristics controls
include loan maturity (Maturity), loan amount (LoanSize), and performance pricing dummy (Performance). Detailed variable defini-
tions are seen in Appendix A.

Moreover, throughout the empirical analysis, we use loan purpose and loan type fixed effects, year fixed effects, and firm fixed
effects to alleviate potential omitted variable concerns. Loan purposes include corporate purposes, working capital, debt repayment,
acquisition, backup line for commercial paper, and others. Loan types include term loans, revolvers, and 364-day facilities. The year
fixed effects represent the period a loan facility belongs to. Continuous variables are winsorized at 1 % and 99 %.

3.4. Data and sample

Data used in this study are from various sources. We obtain supplier chain data from the Compustat Segment file in WRDS, which
provides comprehensive coverage of major buyer-supplier relationships and is widely used in academic research (e.g., Gu et al., 2017;
Radhakrishnan et al., 2014). According to the mandate by the Statement of Financial Accounting Standards No. 14 and Statement No. 131,
public firms are required to disclose their major customers who account for at least 10 % of total sales.5,6 Common ownership measures
between suppliers and customers are constructed using the Thomson Reuters 13F database. Loan cost data are determined by Thomson
Reuters loan pricing corporation (LPC) Dealscan, which comprises a variety of loan features. The financial data of control variables are
from Compustat.

As for dataset matching, following Cen et al. (2017) and Cohen and Frazzini (2008), we utilize the WRDS Analytics Tool, which is
built on the historical customer piece from Compustat Segment data. This tool employs a fuzzy name-matching algorithm to link
customer identifiers across historical CRSP and Compustat company fields, ensuring accurate integration of datasets. Each match is
manually verified to enhance the reliability of our common ownership measures. Additionally, the records are calibrated and com-
plemented with publicly available data and contributions from researchers.7

We start constructing the sample from supply-customer relationship data, which includes 30,356 pairs in a total of 115,877 ob-
servations ranging from 1976 to 2017. Then we drop pairs without disclosing the date or sales, and match the remaining data with the
13F database. The institutional shareholding data in 13F is provided at the quarter level, and we obtain 86,494 quarter observations for
24,244 pairs. We further aggregate the total quarterly supplier-customer pairs into supplier-level data, and average them within a fiscal
year.

Later on, we calculate the cost of loans through Dealscan, and merge the loan facility with Compustat, CRSP, and common
ownership data using the linktable from Dealscan. For financial variables, book value of total assets (or equity) should be greater than
zero, and all the financial and loan characteristics variables are not missing. Finally, the baseline sample comprises 7005 facility (or
tranche) observations from the year 1986 to 2017.

4.3. The influence of hold-up problems

We then investigate the elements that may mitigate the negative effect of vertical common ownership on suppliers’ cost of loans.
First, considering the coordination channel, suppliers with significant relationship-specific investments are more vulnerable to hold-
ups, especially if their customers switch to other suppliers, rendering these investments less valuable elsewhere. Previous research
indicates that such firms are more likely to resort to accounting strategies to sustain their supply chain relationships (Raman and
Shahrur, 2008). We argue that vertical common ownership could be particularly effective in reducing perceived supply chain risks
among creditors, especially in cases where hold-up issues are pronounced. Therefore, the baseline association is anticipated to be
stronger for businesses with higher relationship-specific investment. We use asset redeployability (Redeploy) measured by the BEA
capital flow table (Kim and Kung, 2017) to proxy for relationship-specific investment. The subsample regression results based on asset
redeployability are shown in Table 3. Firms are assigned to the low (high) group if the values of Redeploy are lower (higher) than the
median.

In Table 3, the baseline results remain significantly negative when Redeploy = Low, but become insignificant when Redeploy =
High. According to the P-values reported at the bottom of the panel, the differences between the two subsamples are significant at 1 %
or 5 %. Overall, this cross-sectional test suggests that the baseline relationship is more significant for firms with greater relationship-
specific investment.

4.4. The influence of bargaining power

Moreover, in line with the coordination role, we consider the baseline relationship to be more significant for suppliers with weaker
bargaining power. Transactional agreements and their terms are largely influenced by the bargaining power of each party involved.
Typically, the party with greater bargaining power secures more favorable contract terms (Hui et al., 2012). Suppliers with weaker
bargaining power are more vulnerable to opportunistic actions from their customers. Therefore, vertical common ownership’s role in
coordination is likely more critical in these situations, potentially strengthening the baseline relationship. In this test, we use two
variables to proxy for the bargaining power of the supplier firm and report the results in Table 4. The first one, industry entry rate
(Entry), is calculated as the proportion of new firms to total firms within an industry, where the new firm is just established for one year
or two years (Klapper et al., 2006). Higher industry entry rate means more suppliers are available for customers to choose from, where
the bargaining power of suppliers is lower. Therefore, we argue that the baseline relationship is more pronounced for subsamples of
Entry = High. This measurement is measured at the industry level. Besides, we also use a firm-level trade measurement to proxy for
bargaining power. The trade credit provision (AR) is the accounts receivable of the supplier divided by lagged total assets. Suppliers
with greater bargaining power tend to offer more credit financing to their customers (Fabbri and Klapper, 2016). Therefore, the
baseline relationship is expected to be more pronounced when AR = High.

The subsample results are shown in Table 4, Panel A and Panel B. Firms are assigned to the low (high) group if the values of
conditional variables are lower (higher) than the median. The negative baseline relationships remain significant when Entry = High
and AR = High, but insignificant in the low groups. All of the within-group P-values are lower than 0.001 in Panel A, and lower than
0.10 in Panel B, which suggests that the negative baseline relationship is more pronounced for supplier firms with weaker bargaining
power.

4.6. The influence of the information environment

In addition, to test the monitoring hypothesis, we investigate the effect of information asymmetry. According to Jin and Myers
(2006) and Jensen and Meckling (2019), agency conflicts between borrowing firms and creditors become more severe when the
company is operating in an opaque environment. Since we contend that vertical common ownership can effectively monitor supply
chain partners’ misconduct and lower agency costs, thereby reducing the cost of loans, we hypothesize that the role of vertical common
ownership may be more prominent for firms with higher information asymmetry.

We use the information opacity measure to proxy for the information environment. Opacity is calculated as the prior three years’
moving sum of the absolute value of discretionary accruals (Hutton et al., 2009). The subsample regressions are reported in Table 6.
The negative baseline relationships remain significant when Opacity = High, but become insignificant when Opacity = Low. All of the
P-values within groups are lower than 5 %, indicating that the baseline relationship is more pronounced for firms with higher in-
formation asymmetry.

4.7. The influence of financial position

We further test the mediation effect of corporate financial conditions. A firm’s financial condition is a vital determinant of its
solvency or default risk. External supervision becomes more important for a firm with poorer financial conditions (Fields et al., 2012).
Thus, creditors will commend vertical common ownership more in terms of lower cost of loans. Moreover, we argue that supplier firms
with weaker financial positions are more vulnerable to customers’ hold-ups, and thus have stronger incentives to engage in accounting
manipulation, where the coordination role of common institutional investors may be more important. According to the argument
above, the influence of financial condition provides evidence for the overall effect of vertical common ownership on the cost of loans,
so we do not distinguish between coordination and monitoring channels in this test. To sum up, we expect the relation of common
ownership with reduced cost of loans to be stronger for firms with weaker financial situations.

5.2. Vertical common ownership versus horizontal common ownership

Further, there may be a concern that vertical common ownership acts in the same role as horizontal one, which means the firm has
common institutional investors with its competing peers. To differentiate the effect of vertical common ownership from horizontal one,
we conduct two robustness tests in this section.

First, referring to Park et al. (2019), we construct dummy variables VCO (and HCO), which equal one if a firm has common
ownership with its customers (industry peers) for more than three quarters within the fiscal year, otherwise zero. In this section, the
industry competing peers are identified by the 4-digit SIC industry classification code. Results remain similar if we use a 2 or 3-digit SIC
code. As in the baseline regression, we also set 5 % as the shares’ cutoff for common ownership measurements construction. Statistics
show that the mean value of VCO is 0.12, and the mean value of HCO is 0.45, which is more prevalent. Then, based on the values of
VCO andHCO, we conduct a 2 × 2 matrix to compare the intergroup differences of loan spread (Spread). The results are shown in Panel
A of Table 9. Panel A lists the average value of Spread in the four groups (VCO = 0 and HCO = 0, VCO = 1 and HCO = 0, VCO = 0 and
HCO = 1, VCO = 1 and HCO = 1) and the intergroup differences among every two groups. For any value of HCO, the differences
between VCO = 1 and VCO = 0 are significantly negative at 5 % level. However, the differences between HCO = 1 and HCO = 0 are
always insignificantly positive. To sum up, horizontal common ownership has no effect in lowering the cost of loans in supply chain
settings, while the role of vertical common ownership is still significant, even ruling out the influence of horizontal one.

Second, we set a series of horizontal common ownership measures similar to the vertical ones in baseline tests and add them
together in the regressions. Specifically, HCO_Firms is the natural log of one plus the number of the firm’s industry peers that are co-
owned with the firm. HCO_Firms_MV is the natural log of one plus the market value of the firm’s industry peers that are co-owned with
the firm. HCOwn_# is the natural log of one plus the number of institutional investors who are blockholders of both the firm and its
industry peers. HAvg_Investors is the number of horizontal common institutional investors scaled by the number of total institutional
investors in the firm. We also add the percentage of shares held by institutional investors (INST) in the regression. Results of these two
kinds of common ownership measurements and institutional investor shareholdings are reported in Panel B of Table 9. It shows that the
coefficients of vertical common ownership measures are still significant at 5 % or 1 % level, while the horizontal ones are far from
significant; some are even positive. For the only negative coefficient of horizontal common ownership measurement in column (2), the
coefficient magnitude of vertical common ownership (− 0.009) is 9 times larger than that of horizontal common ownership (− 0.001).
Vertical common ownership definitely predominates. In addition, coefficients of INST are neither significant. Overall, it illustrates the
incremental effect of vertical common ownership compared to horizontal common ownership and institutional ownership.

5.4. Alternative vertical common ownership measurements

We further conduct robustness checks using several other vertical common ownership measurements. First, since our analysis is
conducted at the supplier level, it is crucial to understand how much of the supplier’s supply chain is exposed to common ownership.
Therefore, firm-based common ownership measurements, which reflect the number of a supplier’s customers that share a major
institutional investor with the supplier, should be more appropriate. Accordingly, we have adjusted our initial measurements in the
baseline regression model to be scaled by the total number of customers each firm has. Specifically, CO_AvgFirms represents the number
of the firm’s customers that are co-owned with the supplier, scaled by the total number of customers that the firm has. Similarly,
CO_AvgFirms_MV represents the market value of the firm’s customers that are co-owned with the supplier, scaled by the total market
value of all the firm’s customers. The regression results, presented in columns (1) and (2) of Table 11, remain consistent with previous
findings.

Additionally, following He and Huang (2017) and Freeman (2023), we consider a series of other vertical common ownership
measurements. The first one is COwn_D, which equals one if a firm has any common ownership with its customers, otherwise zero. The
second is COwn_Avg_EW, which is the arithmetic average of the supplier firm i’s shares and customer j’s shares held by common in-
vestors. It is aggregated at the firm level by using an equal-weighted average across all its customers. We also use the market value-
weighted average value (COwn_Avg_VW) and sales-weighted average value (COwn_Avg_SW) for robustness tests. In addition, we set the
variable COwn_Value_EW, which is the total market value held by all the common investors of the supplier firm i and its customer j,
scaled by the total market capitalization of supplier i and customer j. It is aggregated at the supplier firm level by using an equal-
weighted average across all of its customers. We also use the market value-weighted average value (COwn_Value_VW) and sales-
weighted average value (COwn_Value_SW) for robustness tests. Since institutional shareholdings are provided by quarter data in the
Thomson database, the above vertical common ownership measurements are averaged within a fiscal year. Regression results are
reported in the remaining columns of Table 11. The coefficients of all the vertical common ownership measures are significantly
negative at 1 % or 5 %, which further supports our arguments.

5.5. Alternative cost of loans measurements

In this section, we use other measures of cost of loans (Melnik and Plaut, 1986). The first is loan maturity (Maturity), is the natural
log value of one plus loan facility maturity counted in months. The second one is loan amount (LoanSize), equals the natural log value
of one plus the total loan amount, measured in millions of dollars.11 The third one is the number of lenders (No_Lenders), which equals
the natural log value of one plus the number of lenders in a loan facility. The regression results are reported in Panel A (Maturity), Panel
B (LoanSize), and Panel C (NoLenders) of Table 12, respectively. Most of the coefficients of vertical common ownership measurements
are significantly positive for loan maturity, loan amount, and the number of lenders (only slightly insignificant for Avg_Investors in
Panel B and Panel C), providing further evidence that besides loan spread, vertical common ownership can also decrease the other
dimensions of cost of loans.

Lastly, there may be concerns that relying solely on all-in spread drawn from drawn portion of loan facilities might be incomplete,
as the undrawn portion of a credit line also incurs spread/fees. Following the methodology outlined by Berg et al. (2016), we have
expanded our analysis to include the total cost of borrowing (TCB) as a measure of loan costs. TCB equals (Upfront Fee / Expected Loan
Maturity in Years) + (1-PDD) * (Facility Fee + Commitment Fee) + PDD * (Facility Fee + Spread) + PDD * Prob(Utilization > Uti-
lizationThreshold | Usage > 0) * Utilization Fee + Prob(Cancellation) * Cancellation Fee. In the equation, the Upfront Fee represents a one-
time payment made by the borrower at the loan’s closing date. The Expected LoanMaturity in Years is the duration between the facility’s
start and end dates. PDD stands for the probability of a drawdown, indicating the likelihood that the borrower will draw on the credit
line. The Facility Fee is an annual fee charged on the total committed amount, regardless of usage, while the Commitment Fee is paid on
the unused portion of the loan commitment. The Spread is the interest margin above the interbank loan rate for the drawn portion of
the loan. Prob(Utilization> UtilizationThreshold | Usage> 0) is the probability that the utilization rate will exceed a specified threshold,
given that there is usage, and the Utilization Fee is charged when this threshold is exceeded. Finally, Prob(Cancellation) represents the
probability that the loan will be canceled before maturity, and the Cancellation Fee is the cost associated with such an early termi-
nation.12 The regression results are presented in Table 12 Panel D, which are in line with our findings.

5.6. The drivers of vertical common ownership and Heckman analysis

Given that vertical common ownership is an endogenously determined variable and scant literature exists exploring its de-
terminants, concerns may arise regarding what drives variation in vertical common ownership. Our study aims to address this gap to
some extent. We propose that the primary drivers of vertical common ownership could stem from two sources: the fraction of customer
sales and the share ownership held by institutional investors. First, fraction of customer sales reflects a supplier’s dependency on each
customer. A higher proportion of sales to a particular customer not only highlights strategic importance of that relationship but also
encourages the alignment of objectives through common ownership. Such alignment is expected to smooth transactional flows,
enhance trust, and foster stable long-term relationships (Shi, 2016), thereby increasing the likelihood of vertical common ownership as
a strategic choice to secure and enhance these critical business engagements. Second, we suggest that companies with greater

6. Conclusions

This paper presents evidence linking vertical common ownership with cost of loans for supplier firms. We find that creditors view
suppliers with vertical common owners as less likely to default, as indicated by lower loan spreads. This negative correlation is more
distinct in suppliers with greater relationship-specific investments, weaker bargaining power, higher information asymmetry, long-
term common institutional owners, and weaker financial positions. The robustness of these results is confirmed through various
methods, including analyses of financial institution M&As, supplier-customer pair level assessments, and incorporating horizontal
common ownership and institutional ownership as control variables, along with other tests. Additionally, the study explores the impact
of vertical common ownership on other financial metrics, revealing an increase in subsequent financing and investment activities,
including capital and R&D expenditures. We also find that the fraction of customer sales and the institutional investors’ share
ownership are likely to influence the likelihood of vertical common ownership. Our results consistently hold under the Heckman
analysis.

These findings align with the argument that institutional investors with vertical common ownership enhance firm coordination and
monitoring, thereby reducing risks associated with hold-ups, opportunism, and adverse selection, ultimately leading to lower risk
premiums from creditors. This research illuminates the role of common ownership in supply chains and its effect on reducing the cost
of loans, offering valuable insights for corporate management, regulators, and external creditors. It underscores the significance of
