1. Introduction

Amid intensifying global economic competition and rising uncertainties, corporate innovation has become a core driver for 
enhancing national and enterprise competitiveness. Internationally, developed economies such as the United States and Germany have 
been actively fostering innovation ecosystems by optimizing capital markets and corporate governance systems (Bolor-Erdene et al., 
2023). Among these efforts, stable institutional investors provide crucial support for corporate innovation through sustained capital 
investment and alleviation of short-term performance pressures (Cella, 2020). Simultaneously, executive equity incentives, widely 
employed across firms in various countries, serve as an important tool for motivating management to pursue innovative endeavors. 
However, the instability in institutional investor behavior caused by increasing capital mobility, along with executives’ inclination 
toward short-term decision-making, has emerged as a critical challenge in global corporate innovation governance, particularly in 
emerging economies (García-Sánchez et al., 2024). In China, the rapid expansion of capital markets has led to a significant increase in 
the scale of institutional investors, whose investment behaviors have drawn considerable attention regarding their impact on corporate 
innovation. Compared to developed economies, China’s capital markets exhibit higher levels of uncertainty, with institutional in
vestors demonstrating a mix of short-term and long-term investment preferences. At the same time, the executive equity incentive 
mechanism is still in the process of gradual improvement, and how to release the potential for corporate innovation through effective 
incentives has become an urgent issue to be resolved.

The academic community has produced a wealth of research on the stability of institutional investors, equity incentives for ex
ecutives, and corporate innovation. From the perspective of institutional investor stability, Cella (2020) emphasizes the role of

digital financial development. Yang et al. (2024) examine the impact of administrative monopolies on corporate innovation, revealing 
that administrative monopolies significantly enhance innovation levels through resource access and market competition mechanisms. 
Yang et al. (2024) investigate the relationship between industry and regional peer effects and corporate innovation, concluding that 
firms’ innovation behaviors are significantly influenced by industry and regional peer dynamics.

A review and synthesis of the aforementioned literature reveal that while substantial research has been conducted on institutional 
investor stability, executive equity incentives, and corporate innovation individually, studies integrating these three themes remain 
relatively scarce. This paper addresses this gap by examining the impact of institutional investor stability on corporate innovation, with 
executive equity incentives as a mediating variable. The study also examines the role of agency costs in the relationship between 
institutional investor stability and corporate innovation. The results show that institutional investor stability significantly fosters 
corporate innovation, particularly in firms with strong internal controls and larger organizations. Mediation analysis reveals that 
institutional investor stability promotes corporate innovation by increasing executive equity incentives. Additionally, moderation 
analysis indicates that higher agency costs diminish the positive effect of institutional investor stability on corporate innovation.

This study theoretically enriches the research on internal and external governance mechanisms of corporate innovation and 
provides practical guidance for designing more effective incentive mechanisms and optimizing shareholder structures. It also offers 
valuable insights for policymakers to improve the governance framework of capital markets and enhance corporate innovation ca
pabilities. By uncovering the multifaceted roles of internal and external governance in corporate innovation, this study contributes to 
fostering long-term corporate competitiveness and promoting high-quality economic development.

2. Theoretical analysis and research hypotheses

2.1. Institutional investor stability and corporate innovation

Institutional investor stability, as a critical feature of external governance, is considered to play a significant role in promoting 
corporate innovation. According to agency theory, stable institutional investors, through long-term shareholding, provide clear in
centives and constraints for management, encouraging them to focus on the firm’s long-term development rather than short-term 
performance (Zhang et al., 2024). Innovation activities are often associated with high risks and uncertainties, and management 
may reduce R&D investments due to market pressures. Stable institutional investors, however, can alleviate this short-term orientation 
by offering sustained capital support and oversight of decision-making, thereby ensuring the continuous advancement of innovation 
activities. Moreover, long-term institutional investors develop a deep understanding of the firm’s operations, enabling them to provide 
high-quality informational support to management and reduce information asymmetry. This role not only improves decision-making 
efficiency but also enhances the firm’s credibility in capital markets, strengthening its financing capacity and laying a solid resource 
foundation for innovation activities (Kałdoński and Jewartowski, 2024). Additionally, behavioral finance theory highlights that stable 
institutional investors act as “stabilizers” during market fluctuations, helping firms maintain consistent R&D investments, which in 
turn enhances innovation efficiency and the conversion rate of innovation outcomes (Altaf and Jan 2023). Building on the previous 
findings, this study puts forward the following hypothesis:
Hypothesis 1. Institutional investor stability has a significant positive impact on corporate innovation.

2.2. Institutional investor stability, executive equity incentives, and corporate innovation

Corporate innovation is a crucial pathway for enhancing core competitiveness and achieving sustainable development. However, 
its characteristics of high investment, high risk, and high uncertainty make effective external governance and internal incentives 
particularly important (Belghitar and Clark, 2015). As a key factor in external governance, institutional investor stability provides 
firms with sustained capital support through long-term shareholding while establishing stable supervision and constraints on man
agement. Internally, executive equity incentives serve as a vital mechanism to stimulate innovation motivation by aligning man
agement interests with the firm’s long-term goals, thereby enhancing executives’ willingness to commit to innovation projects. Equity

technological upgrades, facilitating the realization of long-term corporate value (Huang et al., 2025; Lai et al., 2017). Additionally,

technology-oriented enterprises. Specifically, institutional investor stability offers external assurance for corporate innovation through 
sustained support and governance optimization. Meanwhile, executive equity incentives provide internal momentum for innovation 
through multi-level incentive mechanisms. Together, they complement each other, jointly enhancing corporate innovation capabilities 
and laying a solid foundation for long-term competitiveness. Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following hypothesis:
Hypothesis 2. Institutional investor stability enhances corporate innovation by increasing executive equity incentives.

2.3. Institutional investor stability, agency costs, and corporate innovation

Agency costs refer to the efficiency losses arising from information asymmetry, conflicting objectives, and other factors between 
corporate management and shareholders, which are particularly pronounced in the context of corporate innovation activities 
(Belghitar and Clark, 2015). High-stability institutional investors provide long-term capital support and foster greater focus on 
long-term development and innovation through effective supervision mechanisms. However, as agency costs increase, the positive 
effect of institutional investor stability on innovation tends to weaken. High agency costs signify more pronounced information 
asymmetry and conflicts of interest between management and shareholders. Under such conditions, management may be inclined to 
avoid high-risk innovation projects and favor conservative strategies with short-term returns (Zhang and Li, 2024). This weakens the 
supervisory and incentivizing effects of institutional investors, thereby diminishing their positive impact on corporate innovation. In 
light of the previous analysis, the following hypothesis is proposed in this study:
Hypothesis 3. The increase in agency costs weakens the positive effect of institutional investor stability on corporate innovation.

3. Research design

3.1. Data sources

This study analyzes A-share listed companies from 2013 to 2022, using data sourced from the CSMAR database. Companies 
categorized as ST and *ST, as well as those in the financial sector, were excluded from the sample. Furthermore, observations with 
incomplete data were omitted, and all continuous variables underwent winsorization. The final dataset includes 11,759 observations.

3.2. Variable definitions

Dependent variable: Corporate innovation (RD). Innovation is the primary driver of development, directly influencing a firm’s 
sustainable growth and its ability to gain a competitive edge in intense market competition, thereby enhancing overall competitiveness 
(Bai et al., 2024). This study uses firms’ R&D expenditure to measure their level of innovation. R&D expenditure forms the foundation 
of corporate innovation activities, encompassing direct financial investments in new products, new services, and technological im
provements. In the robustness tests, the number of annual patent applications is used as an alternative measure of corporate 
innovation..

Independent variable: Institutional investor stability (IO). Following the methodologies of Callen and Fang (2013) and Wang and 
Sun (2022), this study measures institutional investor stability based on the proportion of institutional shareholding, calculated from a 
temporal perspective. As shown in Eq. (1), institutional investor stability is defined as the ratio of the institutional shareholding 
proportion of firm i in year t to the standard deviation of institutional shareholding proportions for firm ii over the previous three years 
(from t − 1 to t − 3).

agency costs between shareholders and management.

4.3. Robustness tests

To further verify the reliability and validity of the research results, this study conducts robustness tests by replacing the dependent 
variable and excluding specific samples for regression analysis.

4.3.1. Replacing the dependent variable
To ensure that the research results are not dependent on a specific measurement indicator, this study uses alternative measures of 

corporate innovation. Specifically, the original dependent variable RD is replaced with PT (patent applications). R&D investment may 
be influenced by various factors, such as firm size, industry characteristics, and R&D efficiency, which could affect the relationship 
between R&D investment and corporate innovation. By using the number of patent applications as a proxy for innovation, this study 
mitigates potential biases caused by these factors and provides a complementary perspective for measuring innovation. Additionally, 
patent applications enable a better comparison of innovation levels across firms and industries, enhancing the generalizability and 
comparability of the results. The results, as shown in Table 4, indicate that after replacing the dependent variable, IO still has a 
significantly positive impact on PT at the 1 % level, further supporting Hypothesis 1.

4.3.2. Reducing the sample size
The 2015 stock market crash significantly disrupted the Chinese stock market, leading to a sharp decline in market value and a loss

level, thereby validating the conclusions drawn for Hypothesis 1.

4.4. Endogeneity test

Given the long cycle of corporate innovation activities and the delayed effect of institutional investors on innovation, this study 
incorporates lagged regression models for robustness testing. The results are presented in Table 5. In Column (1), when RD is lagged by 
one period, the coefficient of IO on RD is 0.0780, which remains significant at the 1 % level. In Column (2), when RD is lagged by two 
periods, IO continues to exhibit a significant positive effect at the 1 % level. Similarly, Columns (3) and (4) present the regression 
results when IO is lagged by one and two periods, respectively, where the impact of IO on RD remains significant at the 1 % level. The 
results indicate that as the lag period increases, the coefficient of IO on RD gradually decreases. This suggests that the impact of 
institutional investor stability on corporate innovation weakens over time. However, the long-term effect remains significant, 
demonstrating the robustness of the conclusions.

4.5. Mechanism analysis

The results of the main effect analysis indicate that institutional investor stability contributes to enhancing corporate innovation 
capability. Next, this study further explores how institutional investor stability affects corporate innovation.

4.5.1. Mediation effect
This study examines the mediating effect of MSR in the relationship between IO and RD. The results are presented in Table 6. 

Column (1) shows the baseline regression results, where IO has a significant positive impact on RD at the 1 % level. In Column (2), after 
introducing MSR, the results still indicate that IO has a significant positive impact on RD at the 1 % level. This demonstrates the 
presence of the mediating effect of MSR. In other words, IO promotes RD by enhancing MSR, thereby confirming Hypothesis 2.

4.5.2. Moderation effect
Table 7 introduces AC as a moderating variable and incorporates the interaction term IO × AC into Eq. (4). Column (1) presents the 

baseline regression results. In Column (2), the coefficient of AC is negative, indicating that AC is negatively correlated with RD. This 
suggests that an increase in AC suppresses RD. Moreover, the coefficient of the interaction term IO × AC is also negative and significant 
at the 1 % level. This finding implies that the increase in AC weakens the positive effect of IO on RD, indicating that AC negatively 
moderates the relationship between IO and RD. Thus, Hypothesis 3 is confirmed.

6. Conclusions and recommendations

Based on firm internal financial data, this study explores the relationship between institutional investor stability, executive in
centives, and corporate innovation. The findings are as follows: (1) Institutional investor stability enhances corporate innovation 
levels. (2) Institutional investor stability promotes corporate innovation by increasing executive equity ownership. (3) The positive 
effect of institutional investor stability on corporate innovation diminishes as agency costs increase. (4) The incentivizing effect of 
institutional investor stability varies across firms of different sizes and management characteristics, being more significant in large- 
scale firms and those with higher levels of internal management. Based on these findings, the following recommendations are 
proposed:

First, improve internal control mechanisms. Strengthen internal governance and enhance the quality of internal controls to ensure 
transparency in decision-making and reliability in financial information. High-quality internal controls not only reduce operational 
risks but also create a more stable investment environment for institutional investors, thereby increasing their willingness for long- 
term shareholding and providing stronger support for corporate innovation. Second, optimize executive equity incentive mecha
nism. Align executives’ personal interests with the company’s long-term innovation goals through equity incentives. Such mechanisms 
can enhance executives’ sense of responsibility and belonging, motivating them to focus more on the firm’s long-term development 
and innovation activities, ultimately boosting the company’s overall innovation capacity. Third, reduce agency costs. Implement 
effective measures to lower agency costs by optimizing corporate governance structures, increasing information transparency, and 
strengthening internal audits. By reducing information asymmetry and conflicts of interest between management and shareholders, 
the stability of institutional investors can be better leveraged to drive innovation. Fourth, adopt differentiated innovation strategies. 
The scale of a firm determines its resource endowment and strategic choices. Large firms should fully leverage their capital and 
resource advantages to increase R&D investment and attract stable institutional investors to strengthen long-term innovation capacity. 
Meanwhile, SMEs should enhance internal controls and actively integrate external resources to alleviate financial constraints through 
institutional investor support, thereby improving innovation efficiency. Fifth, optimize the capital market policy environment. Poli
cymakers should improve regulatory mechanisms in the capital market and encourage institutional investors to adopt long-term 
shareholding strategies. Measures such as optimizing tax policies and enhancing information disclosure transparency can increase

Although this study reveals the positive effect of institutional investor stability on corporate innovation, there are still some 
limitations: First, sample data limitation. The sample data is confined to listed companies, without adequately considering differences 
in industries, regions, or ownership structures, which restricts the generalizability of the conclusions. Second, lack of dynamic analysis. 
The study lacks an analysis of the dynamic evolution of the relationship between institutional investor stability and corporate inno
vation, failing to capture its long-term effects. Future research could expand the sample data range and employ methods such as 
dynamic panel data models to enhance the generalizability and representativeness of the research conclusions.
