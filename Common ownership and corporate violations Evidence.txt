1. Introduction

Common ownership of public firms occurs when an investor cross-holds equity stakes in peer firms within the same industry 
(Ramalingegowda et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023a). Common ownership has become a prevalent phenomenon, raising widespread 
concern in both developed and emerging markets (Azar et al., 2018; Athira and Lukose, 2023). In China, the number of common 
owners and the proportion of firms with common owners are increasing year on year (Fig. 1). Although some literature suggests that 
common ownership can result in governance synergies among co-owned firms (e.g., Kang et al., 2018; He et al., 2019), others 
document that it can cause anti-competitive effects across an industry and weaken oversight over management (e.g., He and Huang, 
2017; Lu et al., 2022; Antón et al., 2023). Given the persistent controversy over the governance implications of common ownership, we 
focus on corporate violations, which are a direct manifestation of governance failures, to assess the impact of common ownership.

Corporate violations are an important consideration for investors, as they can affect shareholder wealth, firm value, and the overall 
health of the capital market (Fich and Shivdasani, 2007; Chen et al., 2023b). Prior research documents that external law environment 
(La Porta et al., 2002; Kedia and Rajgopal, 2011), industry and geographic regulations (Dyck et al., 2010; Kedia and Rajgopal, 2011; 
Wang, 2013), auditors (Dyck et al., 2010), and the media (Miller, 2006; Dyck et al., 2008, 2010; Heese et al., 2022), play central roles 
in monitoring corporate violations. Internally, firm-specific characteristics, such as performance pressure and managers’ self- 
interested behaviors (Bell and Carcello, 2000; Agrawal and Chadha, 2005; Khanna et al., 2015), as well as board and CEO charac
teristics (Chen et al., 2006; Efendi et al., 2007; Khanna et al., 2015), can also influence corporate violations. Yet, despite an extensive 
body of research examining various determinants of corporate violations, the potential impact of common ownership on firms’

We predict that common ownership will be a proactive force curbing corporate violations. This prediction is grounded in the 
theoretical frameworks of the positive effect of common ownership on corporate governance and information transparency. First, 
common owners serve as effective monitors. They can directly intervene in corporate governance through voice, voting, and exit 
threats to prevent managers from pursuing private benefits (Kang et al., 2018; Edmans et al., 2019; He et al., 2019). They also have 
access to more knowledge, expertise, and experience across their portfolio, which enhances their monitoring capabilities (Kang et al., 
2018). Additionally, they benefit from economies of scale, particularly in terms of information acquisition and monitoring multiple 
firms in the same industry, which further enhances their monitoring efficiency (Chen et al., 2023a). These advantages work to reduce 
managerial opportunism, thus curbing the occurrence of corporate violations. Second, common ownership tends to enhance a firm’s 
transparency. Co-owned firms are not only motivated to voluntarily disclose more information (Park et al., 2019; Pawliczek et al., 
2022), but they are also subject to greater external oversight (Edmans et al., 2019; Jang et al., 2022). This enhanced transparency 
increases the likelihood of managers’ wrongdoings being detected, which helps to suppress corporate violations.

To investigate whether common ownership affects corporate violations, we empirically examine the relationship using a sample of 
Chinese A-share listed firms over the period 2007 to 2022. Our baseline regression results suggest a negative relationship between 
common ownership and corporate violations. The effect is not only statistically significant but also economically relevant. To address 
potential endogeneity issues, we employ a series of identification strategies, including propensity score matching (PSM), Heckman 
two-stage, instrumental variable, placebo, and DiD tests. The consistency of results across these different methods confirms the reli
ability of our baseline findings and strengthens the validity of our causal inference. Our results remain robust after adopting alternative 
independent variables, sample period, model, and fixed effects. Moreover, we identify improvements in corporate governance and 
information transparency as potential mechanisms through which common ownership affects corporate violations. In cross-sectional 
analyses, we find that the negative relationship between common ownership and corporate violations is more pronounced for firms 
with a greater extent of external media monitoring and those with a relatively lower proportion of state-owned shares. Additional 
analyses indicate that, among various types of common ownership, common state ownership and common foreign ownership exhibit 
the most significant negative relationship with corporate violations.

Our study makes three main contributions. First, this paper adds to the literature on the effect of common ownership. While some 
research suggests that common ownership creates governance synergies across portfolio firms, referred to as the “synergistic gover
nance” effect (e.g., Kang et al., 2018; Ramalingegowda et al., 2021), other studies suggest that common ownership decreases 
competition and fosters coordination or tacit collusion among firms in the industry, referred to as the “collusion” effect (e.g., He and 
Huang, 2017; Azar et al., 2018). There is also a third strand of literature that suggests common ownership has only a limited impact (e. 
g., Lewellen and Lowry, 2021). Our study contributes to this literature by documenting a tendency toward synergistic governance as a 
result of common ownership. Unlike prior studies on governance synergies that examine the role of common ownership in specific 
aspects of managerial misconduct, such as curbing tax avoidance (Athira and Lukose, 2023), constraining insider trading (Dai et al., 
2024), mitigating earnings management (Ramalingegowda et al., 2021), and deterring bad news hoarding (Chen et al., 2023a), we 
focus on corporate violations, a direct and comprehensive manifestation of governance failure, to assess the impact of common 
ownership. Furthermore, we provide evidence that improved corporate governance and increased information transparency serve as 
mechanisms through which common ownership acts as an effective form of “synergistic governance”.

Second, we enrich the literature on the determinants that influence corporate violations. An existing body of literature documents 
some of these factors, including external forces such as the legal and regulatory environment, the media, and auditors (La Porta et al.,

Third, our paper provides nuanced insights into the differential impact of various types of common ownership on corporate vio

to researchers, regulators, and corporations, as it provides evidence that the governance efficiency of common ownership differs 
between different types of common ownership.

2. Literature review and hypothesis development

2.1. Prior research on common ownership

Common owners simultaneously hold shares in competing firms within the same industry, shifting their goals from maximizing the 
value of individual firms to maximizing returns across an entire portfolio (e.g., Azar et al., 2018; He et al., 2019). This can generate 
countervailing effects: efficiency gains from coordinated governance (the “synergistic governance” effect) versus anti-competitive and 
collusion effects through reduced market discipline (the “collusion” effect). One strand of the literature suggests that common 
ownership creates governance synergies due to more information advantages and greater corporate governance (e.g., Kang et al., 
2018; Edmans et al., 2019; He et al., 2019). However, an opposing strand of literature points out that common ownership can facilitate 
market coordination or tacit collusion and decrease competition among firms in the common owner’s portfolio (e.g., He and Huang, 
2017; Lu et al., 2022; Antón et al., 2023), which creates a hidden social cost for the entire industry. There is also a third strand of 
literature suggesting that common ownership only has a modest impact on competition (Lewellen and Lowry, 2021; Lewellen and 
Lewellen, 2022). In fact, Lewellen and Lowry (2021) document that any anti-competitive effects observed likely stem from other 
factors, such as firms’ or industries’ differential responses to the 2008 financial crisis. They find little robust evidence that common 
ownership affects firm behavior when using modified empirical approaches that are less sensitive to these issues.

2.2. Hypothesis development

Corporate violations refer to actions taken by firms that violate the law or deviate from social norms, resulting in a loss of value for 
the firms and harming the interests of investors (e.g., Holzman et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023b). Violations are typically driven by 
managers’ opportunistic motives to maximize their own interests, which often arise from inadequate internal governance systems 
(Chen et al., 2006; Donelson et al., 2017). Building on the literature that documents the governance synergies effect of common 
ownership, we propose that common ownership will reduce corporate violations due to improved corporate governance and increased 
information transparency.

The first channel through which common ownership reduces corporate violations is improved corporate governance. Common 
ownership can improve corporate governance for three reasons. First, common owners can directly intervene in corporate governance 
via mechanisms such as voice and exit, thereby preventing managers from pursuing their own private benefits. For example, they are 
more likely to vote against management on shareholder-sponsored governance proposals, and their exit is effective because a reduction 
in their holdings in a specific firm signals a pessimistic outlook to the market (He et al., 2019; Edmans et al., 2019). Additionally, they 
can force poorly performing executives to turnover and vote on director candidates to monitor managerial behavior and curb 
opportunistic insider trading (Kang et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2024). Such interventions can prompt managers to take actions that better 
align with the interests of shareholders.

Second, owning multiple blockholdings within a portfolio not only strengthens the incentives for common owners to monitor the 
firms in their portfolio but also improves their governance capabilities. Common owners have a strong incentive to maximize the value 
of their portfolio, which drives them to leverage their advantages to enhance monitoring efficiency (He et al., 2019; Ramalingegowda 
et al., 2021). They can also spread knowledge and governance expertise among co-owned peer firms, acting as an information bridge 
among portfolio firms and thereby reducing monitoring costs (Kang et al., 2018; Ramalingegowda et al., 2021; Athira and Lukose, 
2023).

Third, common owners benefit from economies of scale, which also helps to strengthen their monitoring efficiency. For example, 
when an industry is influenced by unfortunate macroeconomic factors, a common owner does not need to develop a different coping 
strategy for each firm in their portfolio. Rather, one good strategy should suffice for all firms. Moreover, common owners generally 
have access to overarching information and expertise, making them better equipped to identify misconduct and bad news 
(Ramalingegowda et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023a; Liu and He, 2024).

Given that common ownership can constrain managerial opportunistic behaviors and mitigate agency conflicts, managers will be 
more cautious and compliant in making decisions. Several studies provide evidence that stronger external monitoring and internal 
governance result in fewer violations. For example, Dyck et al. (2010), Karpoff and Lou (2010), and Wang et al. (2010) find that 
auditors, media, industry regulators, employees, short sellers, and underwriters can play a monitoring role that prevents firms from 
engaging in misconduct and violations. Therefore, we expect that the monitoring role of common ownership can suppress the 
opportunistic behaviors of managers, which in turn inhibits corporate violations.

The second channel through which common ownership reduces corporate violations is enhanced information transparency. 
Common owners can improve the transparency of portfolio firms for at least two reasons. First, co-owned firms are more motivated to 
disclose information. Common owners aim to promote cooperation and resource sharing. Firms with common owners can have greater 
voluntary disclosure because common ownership reduces proprietary cost concerns of disclosure and internalizes the externality 
benefits of disclosure (Park et al., 2019). The sharing of information among portfolio firms, coupled with the enhanced monitoring 
capabilities of common owners, also contributes to greater transparency in the information disclosed by these firms.

Second, co-owned firms can attract greater public attention and are subject to stricter oversight. Under external pressure, these 
firms become more transparent, which deters them from manipulating earnings and concealing bad news (Ramalingegowda et al., 
2021; Chen et al., 2023a). Additionally, common owners can systematically compare financial reporting and other information among 
industry peers, enabling them to identify anomalies and potential misconduct more effectively than isolated investors. A firm’s 
violation behaviors have negative externalities (He et al., 2019; Park et al., 2019). When a violation occurs, false disclosures and 
misleading statements may distort the investment decisions of peer firms. Therefore, common ownership can reduce the prevalence of 
false information in financial reports and help avoid losses for portfolio firms (He et al., 2019).

Common ownership can improve information transparency, which increases the probability that misconduct will be detected and 
thereby deters such behaviors. Jin and Myers (2006) and Bleck and Liu (2007) find that managers opt to support bad projects to 
maximize their benefits in markets with significant information asymmetry. Once violations are discovered, the perpetrators must bear 
the consequences, which include returning illegally obtained profits and facing significant penalties. Given that common ownership 
can increase a firm’s information transparency, we expect it to help suppress corporate violations.

Based on the above discussion, we propose that common ownership can improve corporate governance and enhance information 
transparency, thereby leading to a decrease in corporate violations. Therefore, we propose our hypothesis as follows: 
Hypothesis. Common ownership is associated with lower corporate violations.

While our primary hypothesis predicts a negative relationship between common ownership and corporate violations, we 
acknowledge compelling theoretical arguments supporting an alternative perspective. The “collusion” effect of common ownership 
may increase corporate violations. First, common ownership may diminish product market competition (Hansen and Lott, 1996; He 
and Huang, 2017; Lu et al., 2022), thereby weakening an important external governance mechanism (Hart, 1983; Chhaochharia et al., 
2017). In less competitive environments, managers may have greater latitude to engage in opportunistic behaviors that serve their own 
private interests rather than shareholders’ interests. Second, common ownership might facilitate information sharing that enables 
sophisticated violations across portfolio firms through complex related-party transactions (Brooks et al., 2018). These countervailing 
effects create a tension that ultimately renders the relationship between common ownership and regulatory violations an empirical 
question worthy of rigorous investigation.

3. Research design

3.1. Data and sample

Our initial sample consists of Chinese A-share listed firms over the period of 2007–2021. The data are primarily collected from the 
China Stock Market & Accounting Research (CSMAR) database and supplemented by the Chinese Research Data Services (CNRDS) 
dataset and the DIB database. We extract common ownership holdings information from the CSMAR database and define a holding as a 
block if it exceeds 5 % of the firm’s outstanding shares. Given the revision of the Chinese Accounting Standards in 2007, this study 
selects 2007 as the initial research year. We exclude the following observations from the initial sample: (1) financial firms; (2) ST and 
*ST firms; (3) firms with leverage ratios exceeding 1; and (4) firms with missing data for key variables. After these exclusions, we 
obtain 37,957 firm-year observations. To mitigate the impact of extreme values, all continuous variables are winsorized at the 1st and 
99th percentiles.

3.2. Variable construction

3.2.1. Common ownership
To measure the impact of common ownership in a given fiscal year, following He and Huang (2017) and Athira and Lukose (2023), 

we focus on blockholders owning at least 5 % of a firm’s outstanding shares and construct two measures: (1) Cross5_Dum is a dummy 
variable that equals 1 if the focal firm and at least one of its industry peers are simultaneously held by at least one blockholder in any 
quarter of the fiscal year, and 0 otherwise. (2) Cross5_Num is measured as the natural logarithm of 1 plus the average number of 
common blockholders that hold the focal firm and its industry peers across the four quarters of a fiscal year. We use the 3-digit industry 
code to identify industry peers based on the Guidelines for the Industry Classification of Listed Companies issued by the China Se
curities Regulatory Commission in 2012.

3.2.2. Corporate violations
Drawing on the methods of Zhang (2018) and Chen et al. (2023b), the corporate violations are proxied by VioDummy and VioNum. 

VioDummy is a dummy variable that equals 1 when a firm is sanctioned for violations by the regulatory authorities, and 0 otherwise. 
VioNum is calculated as the sum of the number of violations committed by the firm.

3.2.3. Control variables
Following Zhang (2018), Jiang et al. (2022), and Chen et al. (2023b), we include a series of firm-level financial characteristics and 

external government variables to control for the potential drivers of corporate violations. These control variables include firm size 
(Size), leverage (Lev), profitability (ROA), operating cash flow (OCF), sales growth (Growth), capital expenditure (Invest), the largest 
shareholding (Top1), independent directors (Indirect), CEO duality (Dual), state ownership (SOE), fixed assets (Fix), firm age (Age), and 
whether the firm is audited by a Big Four accounting firm (BIG4). For detailed definitions of the variables, please refer to 
Appendix Table 1.

3.3. Regression specifications

To examine the effect of common ownership on corporate violations, we construct the following baseline regression model: 
Vioi,t+1 = β0 + β1Crossi,t + βnControlsi,t + Firm FE+Year FE+ εi,t (1) 

where i represents the firm, and t represents the year. The dependent variable is corporate violations (Vio) as proxied by VioDummy and 
VioNum. The independent variable is common ownership (Cross), proxied by Cross5_Dum and Cross5_Num. In our regression specifi
cation, we include firm fixed effects to absorb time-invariant firm characteristics. We also include year fixed effects to mitigate 
concerns that our results might be driven by time trends. Standard errors are clustered at the firm level, and εi,t represents the residual 
term. The coefficient for Cross, β1, captures the relationship between common ownership and corporate violations. Specifically, we 
employ ordinary least squares (OLS) to estimate the coefficients in Eq. (1).

4.2. Endogeneity tests

Our baseline regression results may have potential endogeneity issues in terms of identifying the relationship between common 
ownership and corporate violations. To address concerns associated with self-selection bias, omitted variables, and reverse causality, 
we conduct a series of tests using the following approaches: propensity score matching, Heckman analysis, instrumental variable 
approach, placebo test, and DiD analysis.

4.2.2. Heckman analysis
There is another concern regarding self-selection caused by omitted variables that could result in a biased coefficient—namely, that 

investors may prefer investing in certain types of firms. Firms in the same industry may share the same characteristics, such as similar 
market trends or technological levels. These common characteristics may lead to common ownership linkages. In other words, our 
results may be driven by a firm’s industry-specific factors rather than by common ownership. Following the prior procedure (Heckman, 
1979), we conduct a Heckman analysis to mitigate concerns about investors’ self-selection. Lennox et al. (2012) propose the use of 
restriction variables to estimate the omitted variables in the regression analyses. Our paper imposes exclusion restrictions to imple
ment the Heckman procedure. In the first stage, we use probit modeling to estimate the inverse Mills ratios (IMR). Following the 
literature (Yao et al., 2024), we use CSI300in as the exclusion restriction variable. This is because firms newly included in the Shanghai 
Shenzhen 300 Stock Market Index (CSI 300 index) tend to receive increased attention from investors. CSI300in is a dummy variable, 
which equals 1 if a firm is newly added to the CSI 300 index in that year, and 0 otherwise. In Panel B of Table 3, we report the first-stage 
estimation results, which show that CSI300in is positively and significantly associated with common ownership. In the second stage, 
we re-estimate the regression of the effect of common ownership on corporate violations, adding the IMR variable as a control variable. 
In Columns (2)–(5), the coefficients of IMR are insignificant, suggesting that the potential endogeneity from self-selection is weak. The 
coefficients of Cross5_Dum and Cross5_Num are negative and significant. So, our results are robust after controlling for unobserved 
factors.

4.2.3. Instrumental variable analysis
Our findings might be attributed to the fact that firms with fewer corporate violations attract common owners to invest. That is, our 

findings may be affected by the issue of reverse causality. Thus, we adopt an instrumental variable approach to further mitigate 
potential endogeneity concerns. Previous literature has found that investors generally have specific stock preferences, such as those 
paying high dividends, stocks in firms listed on indexes, and stocks in large firms (Ferreira and Matos, 2008; Aggarwal et al., 2011). 
Since corporate dividends are directly related to investment returns for shareholders, most will expect the firm to maintain consistent

common ownership variable. To increase the identification power, we repeat this exercise 1000 times. We then re-estimate the baseline 
model Eq. (1) using the “false” common ownership. The distribution of estimated coefficients of the placebo test is provided in Fig. 2.

4.4. Mechanism analyses

4.4.1. Corporate governance
According to our hypothesis, common ownership plays a role in corporate governance by leveraging its monitoring capabilities 

(Kang et al., 2018; Edmans et al., 2019; Ramalingegowda et al., 2021; Athira and Lukose, 2023). As corporate governance improves, 
management’s financial decisions become more cautious and compliant, which in turn reduces corporate violations. To test whether 
corporate governance serves as a channel through which common ownership curbs corporate violations, we examine the effect of 
common ownership on corporate governance.

Specifically, we construct four measures to capture corporate governance following prior literature (Dai et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 
2024; Singh and Davidson III, 2003; Jiang et al., 2010). The first measure, common ownership directors (CODirector), is defined as the 
ratio of directors appointed by common owners to total number of directors. The second measure, the quality of internal control (ICQ), 
is defined as the internal control index constructed by the DIB database. The third measure, SG&A expense (SGA), is defined as the 
amount of selling, general, and administrative expenses scaled by total sales. The fourth measure, other receivables (ORR), is defined as 
the amount of other receivables scaled by total assets.

The regression results are presented in Panel A of Table 4. In Columns (1) and (5), where the dependent variable is CODirector, the 
coefficients of Cross5_Dum and Cross5_Num are positive and significant. These results indicate that common owners gain board rep
resentation in their portfolio firms, allowing them to actively participate in corporate governance. In Columns (2) and (6), where the 
dependent variable is ICQ, the coefficients of Cross5_Dum and Cross5_Num are also positive and significant, suggesting that common 
ownership enhances the quality of internal controls for firms. In Columns (3) and (7), with SGA as the dependent variable, and in 
Columns (4) and (8), where the dependent variable is ORR, the coefficients of Cross5_Dum and Cross5_Num are both negative and 
significant, suggesting that common ownership can reduce corporate agency problems.

Collectively, these results provide evidence that common ownership improves corporate governance. Given that an effective in
ternal governance system plays a crucial role in detecting and deterring corporate fraud and misconduct (e.g., Chen et al., 2006; Shan, 
2013), our findings suggest that improved corporate governance serves as a mediator in the effect of common ownership on curbing 
corporate violations.

in information acquisition (Chen et al., 2023a). They can also share their knowledge and experiences among co-owned peer firms,

translates to a greater likelihood of detecting and preventing corporate violations. To test whether information transparency serves as a 
channel through which common ownership curbs corporate violations, we examine the effect of common ownership on information 
transparency.

Specifically, we measure information transparency using two proxies. The first is real earnings management (REM), which is

(CrossSOE_Num) and CrossFOR_Dum (CrossFOR_Num) are significantly negative, while the coefficient of CrossNSOE_Dum (Cross
NSOE_Num) is insignificant. These results suggest that common state ownership and common foreign ownership are likely to act as 
more active monitors and play a stronger governance role. These findings are consistent with previous studies, which show that state 
ownership enhances firm performance through effective monitoring (Chen et al., 2009), and that foreign investors have superior 
experience in information management than domestic investors (Gul et al., 2010).

5. Conclusion

In this paper, we empirically examine whether and how common ownership impacts corporate violations using a sample of Chinese 
A-share listed firms from 2007 to 2022. Consistent with the view of the “synergistic governance” effect, we find significant evidence of 
a negative relationship between common ownership and firms’ violation behaviors. This negative relationship remains robust after 
addressing endogeneity concerns through various methodologies. We also conduct additional robustness tests, including alternative 
independent variables, shortened sample periods, alternative models, and alternative fixed effects. We find that the potential mech
anisms are improved corporate governance and enhanced information transparency. The negative relationship between common 
ownership and corporate violations is more pronounced among firms with a higher external monitoring and those with a lower 
proportion of state-owned shares. By distinguishing between different types of common ownership, we identify that common state 
ownership and common foreign ownership serve as key forces in curbing corporate violations. Overall, we shed light on the role of 
common ownership in deterring a firm’s violation behaviors. Thus, this study contributes to the literature on the determinants of 
corporate violations and also enriches the existing literature related to common ownership.

These findings provide policy implications for governments and corporations. In terms of the public interest, the Chinese gov
ernment should take advantage of the positive role of common ownership to stabilize the capital market. This stabilization can be 
achieved by encouraging practices that enhance transparency and accountability among firms. In terms of business, commercial 
enterprises should proactively introduce and actively manage common ownership to ensure their “synergistic governance”, which 
helps mitigate the risk of corporate violations and enhances long-term value. In addition, many countries are strengthening the 
regulatory requirements surrounding common ownership; however, they face significant debate about whether common ownership is 
beneficial or detrimental. Hence, the insights from this study may be applicable to countries far beyond China, helping to inform policy 
and regulatory frameworks in broader international contexts.
