1 INTRODUCTION

Supply chain relationships constitute one of the most important linkages in the economy. Suppliers not only serve as
production input providers to their customers, but they are increasingly engaging inR&Dand innovation activities that
are beneficial to the customers (Cen &Dasgupta, 2021). However, supplier–customer relationships are generally gov-
ernedby contracts and the challenge ofwriting complete contracts exposes suppliers to postcontractual opportunistic
behaviors by their customers; for example, customers may threaten to walk away from the deal, leaving suppliers’
specialized investments much less valuable (Klein et al., 1978). Existing literature has explored solutions to hold-up
problems and shown that complete vertical integration between the two parties (Frésard et al., 2020; Seru, 2014),
partial equity ownership in the supplier firm by the customer (Fee et al., 2006), and social connections between direc-
tors and senior managers of supplier and customer firms (Dasgupta et al., 2021) can mitigate hold-ups and promote
relationship-specific investments (RSIs) between suppliers and customers. While previous studies focus on strategic
actions taken by the two parties of the bilateral business relationship, little is known about whether a third party out-
side the supply chain can help overcome this hold-up problem.We fill this gap by drawing insights from a type of third
parties that are becoming increasingly prominent among US public firms—common institutional investors.

The hold-up problem arises when a supplier makes RSIs for a customer, including customized production technol-
ogy, tailored inventory management systems, collaborative R&D projects, dedicated infrastructures, and employee
training programs (Crawford, 1990). Inmany cases, supply chains stand to gain fromdeeper relationships throughRSIs
and the exchange of information, owing to the potential for generating relationship rents. However, strong buyersmay
take advantage of weak suppliers (Goldberg, 1976; Klein et al., 1978), as the value of these investments is much lower
outside the relationship, and the relationship could terminate after suppliers havemade substantial RSIs (Williamson,
1979). Moreover, there is considerable uncertainty regarding the outcomes of RSIs, making them difficult to describe
ex ante and therefore not easily contractible (Tirole, 1999). This uncertainty exacerbates the risk of opportunistic
behaviors that favor one party but harm the other, thus undermining the bond between suppliers and customers.
Consequently, a supplier facing higher opportunistic risk may strategically underinvest in customer-specific projects,
resulting in suboptimal outcomes (Grossman &Hart, 1986).

We hypothesize that common institutional ownership (CIO) can help align interests and reduce opportunistic risks
along the supply chain. An institutional investor aims tomaximize the risk-adjusted returns of its portfolio rather than
individual firm profits. As a result, portfolio diversification can mitigate market frictions (Admati et al., 1994; Clay-
ton & Jorgensen, 2005; Hansen & Lott, 1996; Rubin, 2006). Hansen and Lott’s (1996) model suggests that individual
firms may impose various types of externalities—pecuniary or nonpecuniary, vertical or horizontal—on one another,
and diversification helps internalize these externalities to maximize portfolio returns. Therefore, in supply chain rela-
tionships, while a customer can strategically choose to act opportunistically at the expense of its supplier by imposing
negative externalities, a customer whose owners also hold stakes in its suppliers is more likely to consider the impact
of the externalities on its supplier. As a result, holding stakes in both customers and suppliers could align their incen-
tives and reduce the risk of opportunistic behavior, resulting in an improved coordinationwithmore efficient RSIs and
higher overall returns.

One empirical challenge in studying hold-up problems is to reliably measure and quantify investment specificity
and the direction toward whom the specialized investments are tailored. In this paper, we use patent cross-citations
between supplier and customer firms to construct measures of RSIs, as the citing and cited patents can be linked to
individual filing firms, which allows us to use them as a proxy for the citing firm’s direction of innovation investments.
Our measure is more direct compared to other metrics used in the literature such as patent technology class overlap
(Dasgupta et al., 2021) and computational textual analysis of patents (Bena et al., 2021), as these measures cannot
identify the direction of innovation investments between suppliers and customers. In addition, innovation investment
is particularly relevant to hold-up problems, as innovation investment is a long-term and risky process by nature,
rendering it impossible to specify all the contingencies in a contract. More broadly, as a special type of corporate
investment, innovation is critical for a firm’s long-term competitive advantages and sustainable growth (Porter, 1992).

To empirically test our hypotheses on CIO as a potential solution to hold-up problems, we extract a large sample
of supplier–customer pairs from the Compustat Segment Files and obtain institutional holdings data from Thomson’s
CDA/Spectrum database (form 13F). Using a pairwise fixed-effect model, we first exploit within supplier–customer
pair variation in CIO and show that suppliers conduct more R&Dwhen they and their major customers are commonly
held by institutional investors. Next, we show that higher R&D expenditures translate into more innovation outputs,
as measured by number of patents filed and eventually granted to suppliers. We then use patents’ cross-citations
between supplier and customer firms to evaluate the specificity of innovation investments. Our results indicate that
suppliers produce more innovation outputs that are specific to their major customers when they share common own-
ership linkageswith their customers. The above findings are robust to controlling for suppliers’ and customers’ overall
institutional investors’ holdings levels, in addition to an array of firm and firm-pair level characteristics as well as year
fixed effects.

Nevertheless, institutional investors conduct dedicated research and analysis to pick their investment targets. The
nonrandom nature of the investment decision process leads to an endogeneity concern that investors may choose
to hold supplier and customer firms with certain innovation prospects. Moreover, unobservable firm characteristics
may also correlate with both our explanatory and outcome variables, thus making the ordinary least square (OLS)
results spurious. To address these endogeneity concerns, we exploit a quasi-natural experiment of financial institu-
tion mergers using a difference-in-differences (DiD) framework (He & Huang, 2017; Lewellen & Lowry, 2021). When
two financial institutions merge, a firm held by onemerging institution is likely to experience an increase in CIOwhen
one of its major customers is held by the other merging institution prior to the merger.1 Thus, the treatment sam-
ple consists of firms whose ownership linkages with customer firms are likely to increase just because of the merger,
while the control sample consists of other firms in the same institution’s portfolio that are unlikely to experience such
changes. We find evidence that treatment firms, relative to control firms, make more relationship-specific innovation
investments in the year after the institution mergers, which suggests a causal impact of CIO on suppliers’ specialized
innovation investments made toward their customers.

Our DiD analysis reveals that treatment firms, compared to control firms, exhibit a higher propensity for making
specialized innovation investments targeted toward their commonly held customers in the year following institu-
tional mergers. This finding corroborates our OLS results and helps us establish a causal relation between CIO and
suppliers’ innovation specificity toward their customers. Notably, these results remain robust across various model
specifications, including the incorporation of merger-time pair-fixed effects as well as merger- and pair-fixed effects.
The results are also robust when we look at innovation investments in the post-3-year window and when accounting
for heterogeneous treatment effects in DiD settings.

Building upon this DiD framework, we further investigate heterogeneous effects to provide additional evidence
supporting the mitigation of hold-up concerns as the underlying mechanism driving the increased innovation speci-
ficity. If CIO indeed fosters supplier innovation specificity by alleviating hold-up concerns,weexpect toobserve amore
pronounced increase in investment specificity among suppliers facing more severe hold-up concerns ex ante. We use
two measures to capture the severity of the hold-up problems. The first measure is asset redeployability, developed
by Kim and Kung (2017), which accounts for the usability of assets within and across industries. More redeployable
assets exhibit higher recovery rates and are traded more actively in secondary markets. For suppliers with higher
asset redeployability, they should be less concerned about being held up by their customers, since it is relatively easier
for these suppliers to find other buyers in case of a hold-up. Our second measure is the Herfindahl–Hirschman Index
(HHI), which serves as a proxy for suppliers’ and customers’ relative bargaining powers. For suppliers operating in
industries with higher HHI, an indicator of less competitionwithin the industry, they should possess higher bargaining
power relative to their customers, and thus have less hold-up concerns. On the other hand, if customers hold more
bargaining power, suppliers should be more concerned about being held up in the future. We find that the increase
in suppliers’ innovation specificity is lower after institution mergers for suppliers with higher asset redeployability or

Next, we investigate and rule out some alternative explanations for our main results. In particular, we exam-
ine whether knowledge spillovers and innovation collaboration between suppliers and customers are the channels
leading to increased supplier innovation specificity. If knowledge spillovers were indeed the primary mechanism,
we would anticipate observing effects on customers’ innovation specificity. In this scenario, customers’ innovation
endeavors would becomemore tailored toward their suppliers, indicating a convergence of innovation efforts toward
a common direction. This alignment would result from the exchange of ideas and know-how, facilitating a shared
knowledge reservoir between suppliers and customers.However, our results show that customers’ innovations exhibit
reduced specificity toward their suppliers following institutionalmergers. This discrepancy contradicts the notion that
knowledge spillovers are the driving force behind the increase in supplier innovation specificity.

Then, we investigate whether firm-pairs initiate or terminate joint ventures and strategic alliances after becom-
ing commonly owned, as these are significant organizational mechanisms for fostering innovation collaboration
(Robinson, 2008; Seru, 2014). Our analysis shows that treated firm-pairs are less inclined to establish an innovation
collaboration entity after the institutionmerger.

He and Huang (2017) also examine joint ventures and strategic alliances using the institution merger setting. They
find that cross-ownership improves firms’ tendency to engage in joint ventures, strategic alliances, or acquisitions.
While it may seem that we provide an opposite finding using the same setting, it is important to note that their study
focuses on the cross-ownership of same-industry firms, whereas our study examines the cross-ownership of sup-
ply chain partners. Johnson and Houston (2000) find that horizontal joint ventures create synergistic gains for both
partners, while vertical joint ventures generate gains only for suppliers. Supplier–customer pairs only choose joint
ventures when hold-up hazards are high due to a governance motive, as firms become mutually reliant on a venture
framework to reduce potential hold-up hazards. Given that our baseline results suggest that CIO among supply chain
partners mitigates hold-up problems, our findings corroborate this line of research by demonstrating the substitutive
effect of CIO on collaboration in terms of joint ventures and strategic alliances.

As the last step of our analyses, we examine the welfare implications of CIO on the value of the suppliers and
customers. We investigate this effect at both the firm and firm-pair levels, specifically on the market valuations of
suppliers, customers, and joint parties. Our findings reveal that the combined market value of supplier and customer
pairs increases following CIO involvement. At the firm level, we find that the substantial increase in market value
is concentrated among customers rather than suppliers. In addition, we show that the effect of our main findings is
stronger for active investors rather than passive investors. These results are consistentwith our predictions and eluci-
date investors’ incentives for promotingRSIsmade fromsuppliers toward their commonly held customers, as suppliers
stand to benefit from such coordination while customers can benefit from the value effects. Our findings suggest that
CIO involvement can lead toPareto improvements in such scenarios, resulting in advantageousoutcomes for the three
parties involved.

Our paper is related to several strands of literature. First, we add to the literature on the interaction between
transaction cost economics and supply chain management by showing that CIO along the supply chain can potentially
mitigate hold-up problems. Williamson (1979) and Grossman and Hart (1986) develop the transaction costs theory
and property rights theory, respectively, to tackle the questions of whether a firm’s transactions with other firms
should remain at arm’s length or internalized via vertical integration, whenRSIs are needed, but facedwith incomplete
contracts and hold-up problems. Klein et al. (1978) argue that complete vertical integration canmitigate hold-up prob-
lems in the supply chain, so thatmergers along the supply chain can lead to increased efficiency (see also Frésard et al.,
2020; Seru, 2014). Fee et al. (2006) show that customer’s equity stake in its supplier firm also encourages efficient
actions when firms are concerned with expropriation or hold-up problems arising from RSIs. A more recent study by
Dasgupta et al. (2021) finds social connections between directors and seniormanagers of supplier and customer firms
help mitigate hold-up concerns. Prior studies focus on strategic actions taken by the firms themselves to solve the

Our paper also contributes to the burgeoning literature of CIO by looking at the cross-held firms along the supply
chain. The literature has documented that as common owners are incentivized to internalize each firm’s actions to
maximize the portfolio benefit (Hansen & Lott, 1996; Rubin, 2006), CIOs could have real impacts on firms’ strategic
choices, including product market competition (Azar et al., 2018; Dennis et al., 2022; He & Huang, 2017; Koch et al.,
2021; Lewellen & Lowry, 2021; López & Vives, 2019), managerial incentives and compensations (Antón et al., 2023;
Gilje et al., 2020; Liang, 2016), corporate governance (He et al., 2019), executive, and financial disclosure and account-
ing accruals (He et al., 2020; Jung, 2013; Park et al., 2019; Pawliczek et al., 2022). The literature began by focusing on
horizontal CIOs (i.e., commonly owned firms in the same industry) (Azar et al., 2018;He&Huang, 2017;Heet al., 2019;
Koch et al., 2021; López&Vives, 2019; Park et al., 2019), andmore recently, moved on to examineCIOs between firms
from different industries that are economically linked in different contexts, such as CIOs between acquirers and tar-
get firms (Brooks et al., 2018; Harford et al., 2011; Matvos & Ostrovsky, 2008), banks and industrial firms (Azar et al.,
2022; He et al., 2023; Ojeda, 2019;Wang&Wang, 2022), media and industrial firms (Chen et al., 2022; He et al., 2022,
Ruet al., 2020).While growing fast, the literature has paid little attention to the effects ofCIOalong supply chain firms.
Our paper is among the first to extend this strand of literature.

While Freeman (2023) and Cheung et al. (2020) also examine CIO effects on supply chain issues, our study distin-
guishes itself in severalways. First, Freeman (2023) focuses on the supply chain partnership duration andCheung et al.
(2020) focus on suppliers’ operating performance. In contrast, our focus lies on the specificity of supplier innovation
investments tailored to their customers. Second, we employ institution mergers as a quasi-natural experiment to pro-
vide causal inference, while Cheung et al. (2020) primarily demonstrate correlations. While Freeman (2023) employs
a similar DiD approach, our methodology involves supplier–customer pairwise fixed effects rather than supplier- and
customer-fixed effects. This enables us to account for changes in supplier innovation activities directed at specific
customers within a given relationship, thus addressing supply chain hold-up concerns more directly. Third, our sam-
ple spans from 1980 till 2019 while Freeman’s stops in 2010 and Cheung et al.’s in 2011, making the results and
implications of our study applicable to a wider andmore recent time period.

Our paper also adds to the literature on corporate innovation. A vast body of literature investigates various factors
that foster or hinder innovation, including financial market developments (Hsu et al., 2014; Moshirian et al., 2021),
shareholder characteristics (Aghion et al., 2013; Brav et al., 2018; Chemmanur et al., 2014), managerial incentives
(Manso, 2011), CEO traits (Hirshleifer et al., 2012), financial analysts (He & Tian, 2013), firm boundaries (Seru, 2014),
and product market competition (Aghion et al., 2005).2 Fewer studies have looked at innovation activities through
the lens of institutional cross-ownership. He and Huang (2017) find that cross-ownership enhances firms’ innova-
tion productivity. Kostovetsky and Manconi (2020) find that CIO facilitates the diffusion of innovation across firms.
Using project-level data, Li et al. (2023) find that CIOs prompt venture capitalists to terminate underperforming drug
projects and withhold funding from struggling startups. Antón et al. (2024) show that common ownership decreases
innovations since commonowners are incentivized todiscouragebusiness stealingbetweencross-held firms that com-
pete in one product market, while common ownership can only increase innovation when technological spillovers are
sufficiently large relative to product market spillovers. Compared to prior studies that focus on knowledge spillovers
and innovation outcomes in general, we are interested in hold-up mitigation and we show that CIO can help reduce
the supply chain hold-up costs. Furthermore, we provide suggestive evidence that RSIs induced by CIO can substitute
for formal collaborations, such as joint ventures and strategic alliances, between suppliers and customers.

2 DATA, SAMPLE, AND VARIABLES

2.1 Data sources

Our supplier–customer relationship sample is fromCompustat Segment Files, covering firm-years from1978 to 2019.
We stop at the end of 2019 to avoid the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on firms’ activities. According to the State-
ment of Financial Accounting Standards (SFAS) No. 131, firms are required to disclose the names of customers that
account for more than 10% of their total sales, while some firms voluntarily report customers even below this thresh-
old.Weexclude government buyers or generic customers such as “Foreign Sales,” “MajorCustomer,” “Vendor,” or “Not
Reported” in our sample. Since the Compustat Segment database only discloses customer names, and in many cases,
only name abbreviations, it requires manual matching of each disclosed name to a Compustat identifier. We use the
supplier–customer links provided by Barrot and Sauvagnat (2016), who use a string-matching algorithm tomatch cus-
tomer names to their corresponding firms in Compustat from 1978 to 2013, and augmented the data coverage to
2019 with a fuzzy name-matching algorithm between company names from Compustat Segment Files and names in
Compustat/CRSP.

Data on institutional ownership is from the Thomson (13F) database. This database includes institutional investors
with over $100million in assets undermanagement, including pension funds, endowments, insurance companies, bank
trusts, mutual funds, hedge funds, and independent advisors. The ownership data report institutions’ holdings at the
end of each calendar quarter. Institutional investors’ stock holding information from the Thomson (13F) database is
available from 1980 to the present. We then merge Thomson (13F) data with CRSP’s quarterly database to obtain
data on firms’ shares outstanding and share prices.

Finally, we require both supplier and customer firms to have nonmissing relevant financial information to be
included in our final sample. We also require firm-year observations to have nonnegative values for total assets
and sales. Firm-level information is obtained from the Compustat/CRSP merged database. Following prior supply
chain studies (Banerjee et al., 2008, 2021), we exclude financial firms (Standard Industrial Classification [SIC] code
6000−6999) and utility firms (SIC codes 4900−4999) as they are subject to different sets of regulatory require-
ments. The final sample covers the periods from 1980 to 2019, consisting of 22,847 supplier–customer pair by year
observations with 2715 unique suppliers, 983 unique customers, and 4805 unique supplier–customer pairs.

2.2 Investment specificity measures

We are interested in how CIO influences suppliers’ specialized investments made toward customers. We investigate
three dimensions of investments on innovation activities: innovation input, innovation output, and innovation speci-
ficity, withmost attention devoted to innovation specificity. R&D expenditures have beenwidely used in the literature
to proxy for innovation input (Allen & Phillips, 2000). Specifically, we scale R&D expenses by the book value of total
assets and replace them with zero if R&D is missing. For innovation output, we count the number of patents filed and
eventually granted in each application year (Fang et al., 2014).We retrieve patent data fromKogan et al. (2017), which
complies the entire universe of US patents from 1926 to 2020.3 The primary interest of this paper is innovation speci-
ficity, as RSI is the focal issue in hold-up problems. Kelly et al. (2021) further provide patent pairwise citations for all
United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) patents from 1836 to 2022.4 We construct two pairwise mea-
sures based on their cross-citations data to capture whether suppliers’ innovative activities are more tailored to the

more supplier patents cite their major customer’s patents, and cite a more diverse range of customer patents, we can
interpret this as the supplier conductingmore relationship-specific innovation investment toward its customer. These
cross-citation measures can deliver more direct information about investment specificity compared with other mea-
sures employed by the literature, such as patent technology classes overlap (Dasgupta et al., 2021) and computational
textual analysis of patents (Bena et al., 2021). Both these two measures aim to capture the similarity of the patents’
technology, which should naturally be high for firms along the supply chain that mostly operate in the same industry.
However, similarity alone provides no information about direction, which is our main focus in the paper. By employing
cross-citation information, we can directly quantify whether suppliers tailor their innovation investment toward their
customers.

2.3 CIO measures

FollowingHe andHuang (2017), we construct twomeasures of CIO,NumCross and TotalCrossOwn.NumCross is defined
as the number of unique common investors holding both the supplier and customer (with holdings larger than 1% of
total shares outstanding). This measure is discrete, capturing the width of common investor networks. TotalCrossOwn
is calculated as the sum of supplier shares held across common investors, divided by the supplier’s total share out-
standing (also considering only holdings larger than 1% of total shares outstanding). This measure captures the level
of influence that common investors can exert on suppliers, sincewe are interested inwhether common ownership can
incentivize suppliers to conductmore relationship-specific R&D.We calculate the CIOmeasures for each quarter and
then average to construct an annual figure. In all specifications, CIO is measured with a 1-year lag.

2.4 Control variables

Following prior literature (Banerjee et al., 2008; Fee et al., 2006), we construct and control for an array of supplier,
customer, and firm-pair-level characteristics in the regression analyses. We control for the same set of characteris-
tics for both suppliers and customers, including institutional ownership (sumof total percentage institutional holdings
reported on 13F), firm size (natural logarithmic of the book value of total assets), leverage (total debt divided by the
book valueof total assets),market-to-book ratio (market value of total assets dividedby thebook valueof total assets),
asset tangibility (net property, plant, and equipment [PPE] divided by the book value of total assets), return on assets
(operating income divided by the book value of total assets), and capital expenditures (capital expenditures divided
by the book value of total assets). To take into consideration pairwise strength and relative importance of business
relationship, we control for pairwise sales fraction (supplier’s sales to eachmajor customer divided by total sales).

3 EMPIRICAL METHODOLOGY AND RESULTS

3.1 OLS analysis: Innovation quantity

We begin our analyses by investigating whether CIO promotes suppliers to make more innovation investments in
general. We regress supplier innovative activities on the pairwise common ownership measures with the following
regression specification:

Sup_Innovationi,t+1 = 𝛼CIOi,j,t + 𝛽Xi,t + 𝛾Yj,t + 𝛿Zi,j,t + 𝜇i,j + 𝜐t + 𝜀i,t+1, (1)

where i, j, and t denote supplier, customer, and year, respectively. Sup_Innovationi,t+1 represents supplier-level inno-
vative activities defined in Section 2.2 (including innovative input, i.e., R&D expenditures, and innovative output, i.e.,
patenting outcomes).Wemeasure the dependent variable in year t+1 since it takes time for innovative ideas tomate-
rialize. CIOi,j,t is the main variable of interest (including two different measures) that captures the common investor
networks, defined in Section 2.3. In addition, we control for a series of supplier-level (Xi,t), customer-level (Yj,t), and
supplier–customer pair-level (Zi,j,t) characteristics that might affect supplier innovation. Finally, we include year fixed
effects (υt) to capture the time trend of innovation activities and possible fluctuation of overall economic conditions,
and supplier–customer pair fixed effects (µi,j) to control for any unobserved time-invariant heterogeneity at the rela-
tionship level (e.g., unobserved technology synergy). Supplier–customer-pair fixed effects ensure that our results
capture the effect of the within-relationship variations in pairwise CIO on supplier innovation. In other words, any
changes in supplier innovation activities can be seen as reactions targeting the customer in the specific relationship
pair. We cluster standard errors at the supplier–customer pair level to account for pairwise serial correlation of the
residuals.

Table 2 reports the regression results on supplier R&D expenditures and patenting outcomes. In columns (1) and
(2), we consistently observe a positive effect of CIO on supplier R&D (significant at the 1% level). For example, column
(1) suggests that, when they become commonly held (by one additional common investor) with its major customer,
holding all else equal, suppliers’ R&D expenditures increase by 0.2% of total assets, a 2.50% increase relative to the
sample mean. Column (2) shows that a 10% increase in TotalCrossOwn, that is, holding 10% more of the total market
values of the supplier–customer pair, increases suppliers’ R&D expenditures by 0.5% of their total assets, equivalent
to a 6.1% increase relative to the samplemean. Columns (3) and (4) report the regression results on supplier patenting
outcomes. Consistent with the results in R&D expenditures, we find a strong correlation between common ownership
and supplier innovation output. Column (3) shows that the number of supplier patents increases by 2.2% if a sup-
plier becomes commonly held (by one additional common investor) with itsmajor customer; while column (4) suggests
that a one-standard-deviation increase in TotalCrossOwn translates into a 0.84% increase in the number of supplier
patents, though not statistically significant. Results in Table 2 lend support to our hypothesis that CIO along the supply
chain mitigates the underinvestment (in specialized assets) problems, promoting both supplier innovation inputs and
outputs.

3.2 OLS analysis: Innovation specificity

Having shown that CIO is associated with suppliers conducting more innovation activities, we proceed to investigate
whether these innovations aremore specialized toward their commonly held customers, which is the focal issue of the
hold-up problem.We take advantage of the patent pairwise cross-citations data to gauge suppliers’ innovation speci-
ficity and estimate Equation (1) by replacing Sup_Innovationwith Innovation_Specificity, which ismeasured by TotalCiter,
the number of supplier patents that cite their customer’s patents in an application year, and TotalCited, the number of
customer patents that cited by their supplier in an application year. We tabulate the regression results in Table 3. For
the TotalCiter measure, NumCross and TotalCrossOwn have positive and significant coefficients (at the 1% level). Col-
umn (1) suggests that, on average, being commonly held by one additional common investor with its major customer is
associatedwith the supplier citing 0.388more of its commonly held customers’ patents, which is equivalent to a 17.6%
increase relative to the sample mean. Similarly, in column (4) we show that a 10% increase in the holding of the total
market values of supplier–customer pairs will bolster suppliers cite 0.747 more of their commonly held customers’
patents, a 25.7% increase relative to the samplemean. Results in this section further support ourmain hypothesis that
common ownership incentivizes suppliers to conduct more relationship-specific innovation toward their commonly
held customers by alleviating suppliers’ concerns of being held up.

3.3 Identification

As discussed earlier, an endogeneity concern of our OLS results is that omitted variables correlated with both a sup-
plier being commonly held with its major customers and its innovation prospects could bias the results. Another
concern is that supply chains with better innovation synergies or technological cooperation prospects may attract
more common institutional investors, resulting in reverse causality. In this section, we address these concerns using
an identification strategy that is widely exploited in the CIO literature: a DiD approach based on the quasi-natural
experiment of financial institution mergers that generates plausibly exogenous variation of a firm’s common owner-
ship levels.5 Financial institutions usually merge for reasons unrelated to the fundamentals of their portfolio holdings.
He and Huang (2017) show that the majority of financial institution mergers happen either due to deregulation or
strategic considerations of the investors. In addition, the acquiring investor usually maintains the holdings of the tar-
get investor for a period of time following a merger, possibly due to liquidity or transaction cost concerns. Therefore,
if one party of the supply chain is held by one of the merging institutions before the merger, while the other party of
the supply chain is held by the other merging institution, then both firms would be held (commonly held) by the same
institution right after the merger. This event provides us with a nice quasi-natural experiment to test how common

Recent research demonstrates that the traditional staggered DID approach, which uses already-treated firms,

treatment effects (Baker et al., 2022). To mitigate this concern, we follow the recent econometrics literature and

4.2 Hold-up problems mitigation channel: Bargaining power

The second dimension of firm heterogeneitywe explore is suppliers’ bargaining power. Following prior studies, we use
industryHHI to proxy for the suppliers’ and customers’ relative bargaining power. For suppliers operating in industries
with higher HHI, an indicator for less competition within the industry, they should have higher bargaining power rela-
tive to their customers, thus be less concernedwith being held up. If customers have relatively large bargaining power,
on the other hand, suppliers should be more concerned with being held up in the future. Similar to previous analysis,
we incorporate supplier’s and customer’s HHI 1 year before themerger event into the DiD regression. The results are
reported in Table 7. The first four columns study how suppliers’ bargaining power interacts with the effects of com-
mon ownership. The coefficients on Treat × Post × Sup_HHI suggest that for suppliers with higher bargaining power,
the increase in innovation specificity after being commonly held with their major customers is lower. Economically,
compared to suppliers with lower HHI, suppliers with higher HHI see a decrease of 3.000 (= 0.226*13.278) TotalCiter
for each standard deviation increase in suppliers’ HHI, and a decrease of 2.900 (= 0.226*12.831) TotalCited for each
standard deviation increase in suppliers’ HHI. Columns (5)–(8) study how customers’ bargaining power interacts with
the effects of common ownership. The results are just the opposite, with the main variable of interest, Treat × Post ×
Cust_HHI being positive and significant.

4.3 Alternative explanations: Knowledge spillover

Our next test considers an alternative explanation—knowledge spillover. If our main results are driven by knowledge
spillover, thenwe should observe not only that suppliers conductmore specialized innovation toward their customers,
but customers’ innovation being more specialized to their suppliers. In other words, the effect of knowledge spillover
should be two-way. We might observe both suppliers’ and customers’ innovations becoming more aligned with each
other during the exchanges of ideas and know-hows. We test this explanation by looking at customers’ innovation
specificity. Specifically, we define Cust_TotalCiter as the number of customer patents that cite its suppliers’ patents in
an application year, and Cust_TotalCited as the number of supplier patents that are cited by the customer in an appli-
cation year. The results are reported in Table 8. Our main variable of interest, Treat × Post, is statistically insignificant
across columns (1)–(3), while it is significantly negative in columns (4)–(6). This suggests that customers cite less of
their suppliers’ patents after being commonly held due to institution mergers, which is against the prediction of the
knowledge spillover channel.

5 WELFARE IMPLICATIONS

Our results so far have provided evidence that CIO along the supply chain promotes relationship-specific innovation
investments of suppliers toward their commonly held customers bymitigating the hold-up problem. In this section, we
explore the welfare implications on the investors from such effect.

Institutional investors aim to maximize the risk-adjusted returns of its portfolio. A substantial body of literature
has argued that shareholders with diversified portfolios aim to maximize portfolio profits rather than individual firm
profits, and as a result, portfolio diversification can mitigate market frictions (Admati et al., 1994; Clayton & Jor-
gensen, 2005; Hansen & Lott, 1996; Rubin, 2006). Hansen and Lott’s (1996) model suggests that individual firms may
impose various types of externalities—pecuniary or nonpecuniary, vertical or horizontal—on one another, and diver-
sification helps internalize these externalities to maximize portfolio returns. Therefore, we predict that an institution
holding stakes in both customers and suppliers could align incentives between the two parties and reduce the risk of
opportunistic behaviors, resulting in an improved coordination with higher overall returns.

To empirically test this prediction, we examine whether CIO involvement leads to higher valuations (measured by
Tobin’s Q) of suppliers, customers, and supplier–customer pairs using a similar DiD framework. Specifically, the joint
valuation of a supplier–customer pair at a given year is defined as the combined book value of assets minus the book
value of common equity plus the market value of common equity of both supplier and customer, then scaled by the
combinedbookvalueof assets of both supplier and customer. Table10 reports the results.We find that the coefficients
of Treat × Post across columns (1)–(3) are positive and statistically significant. In column (2), the coefficient of Treat ×
Post is 0.209, indicating a 10.6% (= 0.209/1.965) increase in Tobin’sQ for treated customers compared to the control
group. In column (3), the coefficient of Treat× Post is 0.195, indicating a 9.9% (= 0.195/1.972) increase in the combined
value of the treated pairs, relative to the control pairs.

6 CONCLUSION

In this paper, we analyze supplier investment specificity toward their commonly held customers via patent cross-
citations, and show that vertical CIO can mitigate hold-up problems along the supply chain. We find that when
commonly held with their major customers, suppliers conduct more specialized R&D and produce more specialized
patents, more so when the common ownership networks are wider and deeper. We establish causality using a DiD
approach based on a quasi-natural experiment of financial institution mergers. Our results are robust to a battery
of robustness tests. Furthermore, we investigate the differential effects of vertical CIO by suppliers’ ex ante hold-
up concern severity and provide evidence on hold-up concerns mitigation as the underlying mechanism driving the
increase in suppliers’ innovation specificity. We also provide evidence that our main findings are stronger in active
institutions rather than passive institutions. As the last step of our analysis, we show that common ownership of a
supplier–customer pair creates value for investors, particularly benefiting customer valuations.

Ourpapernotonly tries to tacklehold-upproblemsbyoffering anewsolutionwhich is independentof actions taken
by the two parties of the business relationship, but is also among the first to extend the common ownership literature
to study its vertical effects along the supply chain.Our paper sheds new light on the hold-up concernsmitigation effect
of common ownership on suppliers’ relationship-specific innovation investments toward customers. In view of this
positive effect, our studyhas important policy implications given the recent debates on imposing tighter regulations on
financial institutions’ cross-holdings due to the widely documented anticompetition effect (on firms competing in the
same industries).9 Our study provides new evidence on the efficiency implications of common ownership that future
regulatory discussions should take into consideration.
