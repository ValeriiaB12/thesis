1. Introduction


    In the financial economics field, many practitioners, investors, and researchers shed light on common ownership among
competing firms. For example, in Financial Times, it is stated as ‘‘Global regulators are starting to home in on an academic theory
on the drawbacks of overlapping corporate ownership by investors, a trend that could pose a threat to the $80tn asset management
industry’’.1 This is one of the typical explanations of common ownership and one can recognize the attention to common ownership
by stakeholders and regulators. Additionally, according to Azar et al. (2018) and Dennis et al. (2022), one can confirm the importance
of common ownership for the firm value and social performance by the stakeholders and researchers. In particular, the relationship
between product market competition and common ownership is widely investigated in the financial economics field. This is because
while common ownership is one of the related issues in the finance field, from the definition of common ownership, product market
competition plays an important role to discuss the effect of it.
    In this study, we also focus on common ownership among competing firms in the product market. In particular, we shed light on
the asymmetric competition among supply chain parties. When shareholders become increasingly powerful, supply chain participants
are often mutually owned by the same institutional owners who share common ownership. For example, Vanguard Group holds

a designer and manufacturer of emission control products, shares common ownership with customers (e.g., Caterpillar Inc. and

    IT developments have made it easier for manufacturers to open a direct channel, leading to manufacturer encroachment. For
example, Hewlett-Packard Co. has a direct channel for selling its products to consumers without using retail stores. Additionally,
reputed companies such as Fast Retailing Co., Ltd., Procter and Gamble, and Apple Inc. use direct channels to sell their products.
With manufacturer encroachment, the manufacturer becomes a retailer’s competitor rather than a supply chain partner. Specifically,
it becomes challenging to consider the ownership of supply chain parties if the owner is a partner or a competitor. For example, the
Vanguard Group holds an 11.50% share of Best Buy4 and 7.4% of General Electric Co..5 This example represents the case in which
the retailer (Best Buy) is encroached by the manufacturer (General Electric) through common ownership. How do manufacturers’
and retailers’ decision-making change in the threat of manufacturer encroachment? This study considered the following research
questions: (i) What is the effect of manufacturer encroachment and common ownership on supply chain parties’ decision-making?
(ii) What is the effect of manufacturer encroachment and common ownership on retailer profits? (iii) What is the surplus effect of
common ownership?
    This study constructs an analytical model based on game theory to explore the impact of common ownership on decision-making
processes and surplus management. In our model, the supply chain comprises one retailer and one manufacturer. The manufacturer
produces goods and sells them to the retailer at wholesale price. The retailer supplies the product to the final goods market by
determining its quantity. The manufacturer can also decide whether to encroach on the final goods market. When a manufacturer
encroaches quantity competition among the retailer and manufacturer occurs in the retail market. Our model assumes common
ownership among firms to emphasize the other parties’ profit. The timeline of these events is as follows: First, the manufacturer
chooses whether to encroach. Second, the manufacturer decides on the wholesale price. Finally, in the non-encroachment scenario,
only the retailer determines the quantity, whereas, in the encroachment scenario, the retailer and manufacturer determine the
quantity. We describe our basic model in Section 3.6
    From our analysis, we obtained the following results. In the non-encroachment case, the degree of channel substitution does
not affect the retailer’s profit, because the competition does not occur between the participants in the supply chain. In the non-
encroachment case, the wholesale price charged by the manufacturer increases as the degree of common ownership increases. Under
large common ownership, while the retailer raises the supplied quantity to enhance the manufacturer’s profit, anticipating retailer
behavior, the manufacturer raises the wholesale price to avoid excessive supply in the retail market. In the case of encroachment,
the degree of channel substitution plays an important role in considering our result. Under a large degree of channel substitution,
it is not profitable for the manufacturer and retailer to supply large quantities in the retail market, because intense competition
leads to cannibalization. Therefore, the manufacturer manages the retail market to enhance its profit and the retailer’s profit under
common ownership. From the discussion presented above, the following two effects are important when considering the effect of
common ownership on wholesale prices: (i) Increasing wholesale prices because the retailer raises quantity by emphasizing the
manufacturer’s profit, and (ii) decreasing wholesale prices to provide the market share to retailers. In the case of a small degree
of channel substitution, the wholesale price increases and the retailer increases the quantity as the degree of common ownership
increases. Therefore, each firm improves its profit under common ownership by increasing its profit. In this case, one can infer that
consumer surplus may decrease with increasing common ownership, because large common ownership leads to tacit collusion in
the product market by supply chain parties. However, this is not always true in our model. In the case of a small degree of channel
substitution, when the wholesale price increases, although the quantity supplied by the manufacturer decreases with increasing
common ownership, that supplied by the retailer increases. The retailer radically increases the quantity in the retail market by
reducing the quantity supplied by the manufacturer. Consequently, the total quantity supplied to the retail market increases as
common ownership increases. In our additional analysis, we check the robustness of this result by assuming a direct selling cost
for the manufacturer in Section 5.4. In addition, by using a numerical example, we confirm that our main results are presented in
Section 5.2.
    These results represent important contributions to supply chain management practices and earlier studies from the perspective of
decision-making and policy-making. First, our results on retailer profits suggest that common ownership improves a retailer’s profit.
This also indirectly enhances the profit in the encroachment case. These findings imply that retailers should aggressively join the

2. Related literature

    Our contributions can be clarified through the earlier supply chain and common-ownership studies. Additionally, using the
game theoretical approach, many supply chain management studies elucidate the effects of opening a direct channel by a
manufacturer (e.g., Chiang et al., 2003; Hamamura & Zennyo, 2021; Hsiao & Chen, 2013; Matsui & Tsunoda, 2022; Matsushima &
Mizuno, 2018; Mizuno, 2012; Tsay & Agrawal, 2004). In this context, Chiang et al. (2003) presents a traditional economic analysis of
a dual-channel supply chain. Additionally, Hamamura and Zennyo (2021), Pei and Yan (2015), Yoon (2016), and Zhang et al. (2020)
use non-cooperative game theory to demonstrate the positive effects of spillover investments. From a managerial perspective, Matsui
and Tsunoda (2022) considers the effects of exclusive territories on supply chain management. Additionally, Hua et al. (2010)
includes the assumption of a delivery lead-time for a manufacturer in a direct channel. Few studies claim that manufacturer
encroachment positively affects a retailer’s profit. Only a few studies have examined the effects of common ownership on supply
chain management. We focus on the study that is closest to our model.
    The effects of the dual-channel on the retailer’s profit and surplus were also investigated in previous studies (Arya et al., 2007; Sun
et al., 2019; Yoon, 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). Arya et al. (2007) demonstrates the bright side of manufacturer encroachment, which
enhances retailers’ profit. Additionally, Yoon (2016) describes that a manufacturer’s cost-reducing investment improves the retailer’s
profits through the spillover effects of its investment. Furthermore, Zhang et al. (2020) considers demand-enhancing investments. Pei
and Yan (2015) conducts supportive retailers’ services that coordinate the dual-channel distribution as an effective incentive. They
assume that additional retail services positively impact demand. Hamamura and Zennyo (2021) describes the effects of a retailer’s
voluntary investment in cost reduction on manufacturer encroachment. In addition, Hsiao and Chen (2013) explores an asymmetric
channel structure despite the ex-ante symmetry between manufacturers.
    In the supply chain study, Aviv and Shamir (2021) is closest to our research. They also explore the impact of common ownership
on supply chain parties using non-cooperative game theory. However, while we assume common ownership between a manufacturer
and retailer, Aviv and Shamir (2021) considers common ownership between horizontal retailers. In other words, while we shed light
on the impact of common ownership on the strategies of supply chain parties with supplier encroachment, Aviv and Shamir (2021)
focus only on indirect channels through retailers, assuming that one manufacturer does not open the direct channel. Additionally,
while Aviv and Shamir (2021) assumes asymmetric information between supply chain parties, we do not assume this to clarify the
impact of common ownership on strategies and surplus. Moreover, assuming a two-echelon supply chain comprised of one assembler
and multiple upstream suppliers, Xiao et al. (2021) considers backward shareholding among supply chain parties. However, they
do not examine the direct channels of the manufacturer.
    Additionally, in the economics field, based on non-cooperative game theory, several findings in the study have described
economic analyses of common (overlapping) ownership (e.g., Li & Zhang, 2021; López & Vives, 2019; Sato & Matsumura, 2020)
because common ownership is an important issue from the perspective of antitrust policy. Li and Zhang (2021) demonstrates the
adverse effects of common ownership on the consumer and total surplus in a duopoly, whereas Sato and Matsumura (2020) shows
that increasing common ownership engenders decreased entry and may or may not improve total surplus. Therefore, common
ownership helps improve the total surplus under a particular economic environment. Additionally, López and Vives (2019) shows
that considering R&D spillover, common ownership enhances consumer surplus and total surplus. Among these reports, few have
described studies demonstrating the positive aspects of common ownership on consumer surplus and total surplus with product
market competition. Our study also assesses the positive effects of common ownership.
    Empirical studies have indicated the importance of common ownership in supply chain management (Chen, 2021; Freeman,
2021).7 In the recent study, Chen (2021) claims that common ownership facilitates innovation by improving relationships between
supply chain parties. Additionally, Freeman (2021) demonstrates that common ownership enhances innovative collaboration
by increasing R&D, patenting, and patent cross-citations. These empirical results indicate that common ownership has positive
management. While common ownership among supply chain parties influences decision-making in supply chain studies, Aviv and
Shamir (2021) only examines its effects. Therefore, one can infer that some light must be shed on common ownership among supply
chain parties.8

   We summarize the key differences between our study and previous studies as follows. (i) While prior studies do not consider
common ownership among vertical supply chain parties with manufacturer encroachment, we consider common ownership by a
shareholder between a manufacturer and retailer. (ii) We demonstrate that manufacturer encroachment positively impacts retailer
profit. This result has been corroborated by several studies (e.g., Arya et al., 2007; Yoon, 2016). (iii) Assuming a dual-channel
supply chain, we demonstrate the positive effects of common ownership on total surplus. While several prior studies demonstrate
the positive impact of common ownership on total surplus (López & Vives, 2019; Sato & Matsumura, 2020), they do not assume
a supply chain. Our study finds that common ownership supports supply chain participants and contributes to the total surplus.
While common ownership is profitable, it harms the total anti-competition surplus. Therefore, the impact of common ownership
on supply chain parties influences decisions regarding supply chain management and anti-competitive policies. Additionally, our
results provide a new explanation for the impact of common ownership on supply chain management. Thus, our results explain joint
investments by shareholders among supply chains, indicating that a firm’s profits improve with common ownership. Production is
commonly owned in many cases (e.g., Rollins Inc. and Dick’s Sporting Goods, Caterpillar Inc., Harley Davidson Inc., and Best Buy and
General Electric). Our findings can explain the common ownership practices of manufacturers. Our results suggest that manufacturers
must determine their levels of common ownership and strategies after observing the degree of channel differentiation. Therefore, this
study not only contributes to supply chain practice from the economic analysis perspective, but also suggests important implications
from the perspective of antitrust policy.

concept in this study and analyze the equilibrium by backward induction.

6. Conclusion

    This study examines the effects of common ownership on supply chain management with the manufacturer’s encroachment
decision. The results of our analyses demonstrate that given a large share of ownership by the manufacturer, the retailer’s profit is
improved by manufacturer encroachment. Additionally, we demonstrate that increasing ownership share improves the total surplus
in a specific case. In our additional analysis, we confirm the robustness and extensibility of our model. In particular, an asymmetric
ownership share and the manufacturer’s direct selling cost have an important role in practice.

                                                                        15
J. Hamamura                                                                                         International Review of Economics and Finance 96 (2024) 103596


   These results constitute important contributions to supply chain management practice and financial economics studies. First,
the result obtained for the retailer’s profit suggests that common ownership directly improves the retailer’s profit and indirectly
enhances the profit in the encroachment case. This result implies that the retailer should aggressively engage in common ownership
between the retailer and manufacturer. Second, we can ascertain some positive aspects of common ownership regarding the total
surplus. Therefore, for supply chain management, society should accept common ownership by manufacturers and retailers.
   We can consider the following future research based on our model. First, for our main model, we assume a monopoly case
by using one supply chain. Therefore, future research should include analyses incorporating the assumptions of a competitor in a
product market. Second, while this study considers only quantity competition, future research can consider cases that include price
competition. Third, recently, to explore optimal decision-making by supply chain parties, several studies assume that other kinds of
agents have a specific utility. In particular, risk-averse agents are supposed to analyze the supply chain coordination (e.g., Matsui,
2019). Therefore, in future research, one can consider the effect of risk-averse agents under common ownership among supply chain
parties. While one can consider the above future studies based on our model, there are beyond our scope, and we propose a basic
analysis of the impact of common ownership on the dual-channel supply chain.
