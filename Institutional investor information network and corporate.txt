1. Introduction

In recent years, China’s economy has been in a critical transition period from rapid growth to high-quality development. As the 
micro-foundation of the economy and society, enterprises not only contribute to the rapid development of the economy, but also 
contribute to the high-quality development of the economy. Enhancing the ability of enterprises to cope with future uncertainties and 
improving risk-taking levels play an important role in the high-quality development of the economy. The risk-taking level reflects the 
price a company willing to pay to obtain potentially high returns, which is reflected in the company’s choice of investment projects that 
have a positive future net present value but are full of risks (Acharya et al., 2011; Boubakri et al., 2013). A higher level of risk-taking 
can enhance the long-term competitive advantage of enterprise and corporate value (Koirala et al., 2020; Cucculelli and Ermini, 2012), 
and also accelerate the accumulation of social capital as well as promoting long-term economic growth (John et al., 2008). Therefore, if 
an enterprise chooses investment projects with lower risks in the long term, both the enterprise’s own production efficiency and the 
development of social productivity will be hindered. The level of risk-taking often depends on the management’s risk propensity in 
operating activities and the company’s ability to obtain resources. Based on the existing literature, the factors affecting corporate 
risk-taking mainly involve social environment and policy systems (Gopalan et al., 2021; Raithatha and Popli, 2022), corporate

micro-governance (Lin et al., 2019) and manager characteristics (Do et al., 2022). But what is more subjective and proactive is closely 
related to the management of risk decision-making entities. In the situations of principal-agent and information asymmetry in en
terprises, management often avoids high-risk projects for the sake of their own compensation and professional reputation, resulting in 
some high-risk investment projects with positive future net present values being abandoned by management, which damages

Professional institutional investors have the dual advantages of information acquisition and capital scale. They can conduct 
detailed collection and analysis of corporate information (Jiang and Yuan, 2018), and participate in corporate activities by soliciting 
voting proxies from small shareholders and submitting shareholder proposals (Matvos and Ostrovsky, 2010). So institutional investors 
are given high expectations in terms of corporate governance. However, market survey reports as of 2022 show that the participation 
of institutional investors and shareholders in corporate governance in China is still not ideal. Faced with many restrictive factors such 
as the excessive voice of corporate major shareholders and information asymmetry, institutional investors have higher cost in 
corporate governance. Therefore, individual institutional investor plays relatively limited role in corporate governance (Shleifer and 
Vishny, 1986). However, the joint participation of institutional investors in corporate governance may make up for the weak 
governance effectiveness of individual institutional investors and strengthen the governance effect on the company. Social embedd
edness theory points out that any individual in economic society is in a certain relationship, and their behavioral decisions will be 
affected by the network. The same is true when it comes to asset selection. As peers, institutional investors frequently exchange in
formation, and their decision-making behaviors will influence each other (Pool et al., 2015). The consistency of institutional investors’ 
decision-making behavior will promote the performance of their functions at the group level (Cvijanović et al., 2022), which may make 
up for the shortcomings of individual institutional investors and improve the governance effect of enterprises (He et al., 2019).

Different institutional investors will allocate assets to the same listed company, resulting in the phenomenon of common share
holding. Based on this common shareholding behavior, an institutional investor network is formed among institutional investors, and 
there is information exchange and sharing in the network (Krishnamurti et al., 2021; Gong and Liu, 2023; Li et al., 2023). Therefore, 
this paper uses the joint shareholding behavior of institutional investors to construct an institutional investor network and studies the 
impact of information sharing in the network on corporate risk-taking. Currently, some scholars have studied the impact of different 
social networks on corporate risk-taking levels based on the perspective of social networks (Fan et al., 2021; Ferris et al., 2019), but 
there is no literature that directly studies the impact of institutional investors on corporate risk-taking levels from the network level.

The contributions of this paper mainly include the following aspects: (1) This paper enriches the literature on the factors affecting 
corporate risk-taking. Based on social network theory, this paper constructs an institutional investor network and finds that infor
mation sharing within the institutional investor network has external supervision and resource advantage effects, which helps to 
improve the level of corporate risk-taking, and expands and deepens the relevant research on corporate risk-taking from the social 
network level. (2) This paper enriches the research literature on the economic consequences of institutional investors. Based on the 
perspective of corporate risk-taking, we reveal the impact of the relationship network that is prevalent in my country’s traditional 
relationship-based society on corporate governance. (3) This paper explores the differences in the role of institutional investor in
formation sharing on corporate risk-taking behavior under different scenarios of financialization degree, financing constraints, po
litical connections, agency costs, internal control quality, regional legal environment and life cycle.

2. Literature review and research hypothesis

2.1. Institutional investor network and corporate risk-taking

Risk selection is an important decision in the corporate investment decision-making process and is related to the company’s risk- 
taking level. Tobin’s theoretical analysis shows that in a perfect market, the real investment level can be predicted by marginal Q, and 
the company’s investment behavior depends only on the investment opportunities represented by marginal Q. That is, managers 
should choose all investment projects with positive expected net present values so that marginal Q equals 1. However, in reality, 
managers’ choice of investment projects is affected by many factors. According to the agency theory, since managers’ human capital is 
a non-diversified investment and managers have no other investments to disperse risks, managers tend to abandon high-risk invest
ment projects to avoid the personal wealth loss, unemployment risk and reputation loss. Hence, in the face of mismatch between 
income and required risk responsibilities, avoiding investment projects of higher risks but higher income becomes the logical choice. 
Especially when the contract between owners and managers is incomplete and information asymmetry occurs, management lacks 
corresponding constraints and often chooses to live a "quiet life" (Bertrand and Mullainathan, 2003), instead of actively choosing 
high-risk and high-yield investments project. In addition, enterprise risk-taking is a resource-consuming activity, and has strong 
resource dependence (Almeida and Campello, 2007). The investment projects and funds required for corporate investment require 
high information costs and transaction costs. This means that even if corporate managers are willing to engage in high-risk and 
high-return investment activities, they may lack the corresponding resources, resulting in insufficient investment and low investment 
efficiency (Faccio et al., 2016). Therefore, if a company cannot obtain sufficient resources, it is often difficult to make risky strategic 
investments, which in turn affects the company’s risk-taking level.

Existing research shows that there is information sharing within institutional investor information networks (Krishnamurti et al., 
2021; Gong and Liu, 2023; Li et al., 2023). Information sharing among institutional investors can produce two effects: one is the 
external supervision effect, the other is the resource advantage effect. First of all, information sharing among institutional investors can 
broaden the channels for institutional investors to obtain information, make up for the lack of information, and improve the

Information sharing among investors on the network enables individual institutional investors to obtain more information on

abreast of industry dynamics and development trends, and to discover more favorable investment opportunities more accurately and 
timely, thereby reducing the risk of underinvestment caused by management’s lack of key decision-making information. At the same 
time, the resource allocation effect of the institutional investor network can more conveniently help companies obtain more resources 
needed for investment at a lower cost (Dahl and Pedersen, 2005). Based on the above analysis, this paper proposes hypothesis H1: 
H1. Information sharing among institutional investors can improve corporate risk-taking levels.

2.2. Institutional investor network, information asymmetry and corporate risk-taking

The risk-taking behavior of the enterprise is highly dependent on resources, and the resources required include investment projects, 
funds, technology, etc. If there are not enough investment projects and cannot obtain enough funds, enterprises will face greater 
resource constraints when investing, resulting in lower corporate risk-taking levels. Investors’ investment decisions will be affected by 
the information they obtain (Travis, 2021), Biddle et al. (2009) pointed out that information asymmetry is an important reason for 
corporate underinvestment and low investment efficiency. On the one hand, when the degree of information asymmetry increases, 
investors will reduce investment demand, which can reduce the investment projects available to the enterprise. On the other hand, 
higher information asymmetry will also increase corporate financing constraints. Bank loans are the main source for enterprises to 
obtain long-term development funds. Higher information asymmetry will increase banks’ risk aversion motivation, banks will require 
higher loan interest rates and formulate more stringent loan treaties from enterprises based on the consideration of preventing loan 
risks, making it more difficult for companies to obtain external financing (Bai et al., 2006).

The external monitoring effect generated by information sharing by institutional investors can prompt managers to adopt better 
accounting policies, and institutional investors can also disclose the earnings management methods of their holding companies, 
thereby reducing the degree of information asymmetry between the outside world and enterprises (Chung et al., 2002). Supervision by 
institutional investors can also improve the quality of corporate information disclosure (Boone and White, 2015) and reduce the degree 
of information asymmetry between companies and external stakeholders. When institutional investors discover that corporate man
agers are hiding information or engaging in improper behavior, they can sue the managers, prompting them to proactively improve the 
quality of information disclosure (Cheng et al., 2010; Pukthuanthong et al., 2017). With reduced information asymmetry, external 
investors such as banks can obtain more corporate information, reduce investment risks, and make it easier for companies to obtain 
funds and investment projects. Based on the above analysis, this paper proposes hypothesis H2: 
H2. Institutional investor information sharing improves corporate risk-taking by reducing information asymmetry.

2.3. Institutional investor network, herding behavior of analysts and corporate risk-taking

Securities analysts are important information intermediaries in the capital market, and investors can obtain relevant information 
about listed companies through research reports issued by securities analysts. However, there is obvious convergence in the behavior 
of analysts, and they will issue similar forecast ratings to other analysts, that is, there is analyst herding behavior (Welch, 2003). 
Analyst herding behavior will reduce corporate risk-taking levels. On the one hand, analysts’ herding behavior reduces private in
formation in research reports, resulting in less corporate information available in the market, which makes it more difficult for in
vestors to understand the true situation of the company and evaluate the future returns of the company’s high-risk projects. So, in order 
to avoid risks, investors will reduce the supply of funds, thereby increasing the financing constraints faced by enterprises and reducing 
their risk-taking levels. On the other hand, analyst herding behavior shows that many analysts simply imitate other people’s research 
reports to obtain information about the company, and do not pay too much attention to the company’s operating conditions, thereby 
reducing the external supervision pressure on the company’s management, which induces opportunistic behavior of management, that 
is, the management invests in more low-risk projects for its own interests.

Existing researches have shown that there is a correlation between analysts’ forecasting behavior and institutional investors, which 
proves that there is close exchange of information between institutional investors and analysts (Liu and Chen, 2024). Close 
communication between institutional investors and analysts will increase analysts’ private information, which will reduce analyst 
herding behavior. The more frequent the exchange of information in the institutional investor network and the higher the degree of 
information sharing, the more private information analysts obtain, thereby reducing analyst herding behavior. Based on the above 
analysis, this paper proposes hypothesis H3:

3. Research design

3.1. Institutional investor network

It is found that institutional investor networks are generally constructed through three methods. The first type is a network built 
based on the social relationships of institutional investors, such as alumni relationships (Galariotis et al., 2015). Fund managers in the 
same social network have similar shareholding behaviors and trading styles (Cici et al., 2017). And the second kind is a network based 
on geographical location, for example, fund managers living in the same city exhibit consistent trading behavior (Hong et al., 2005). 
The third kind is a network built based on asset allocation. Crane et al. (2019) studied the situation of the U.S. capital market, con
structed an institutional investor network based on common ownership relationships, and then analyzed the corporate governance 
effect of the institutional investor network. The network built based on the shareholdings of institutional investors supports infor
mation exchange through the consistency of transactions and positions within the network, so the consistency of trading behavior 
among funds supports the existence of the network. Following the third method, this paper constructs an institutional investor network 
based on institutional investors’ heavy holdings of stocks of the same listed company.

If institutional investors hold a large number of the same stocks, it is assumed that there is a connection between the two insti
tutional investors. The institutional investor network is defined as a collection of funds that directly and jointly hold a heavy position in 
the stock (In the network, each institutional investor is a node, and two institutional investors are connected by a straight line.). The 
standard for large holdings is that the proportion of institutional investors’ holdings accounts for 5 % or more of the fund’s net value. 
As shown in Fig. 1, Muyuan shares are taken as an example. The blue squares in the picture represent funds, and the orange squares 
represent stocks. Muyuan shares were heavily held by Qianhai Kaiyuan Refinancing Theme Select Fund in mid-2021. At the same time, 
the fund also held heavy positions in four stocks including TCL Zhonghuan, Wen’s Shares, Gemdale Group and Poly Development.

3.2. Variable definition and measurement

Network density is an indicator that describes the structural characteristics of a network, and can effectively measure the rate of 
information flow within the network. The greater the network density, the faster the information flow (Pareek, 2011). Network 
centrality better describes the differences in the positions of different individuals in the network, and can effectively capture an in
dividual’s ability to obtain information and influence the behavior of other individuals (El-Khatib et al., 2015). Individuals with 
stronger network centrality have stronger abilities to obtain and transmit information through the network (Bajo et al., 2016). 
Therefore, this paper uses network density and network centrality to characterize the network characteristics of institutional investors, 
and measures the efficiency of information sharing from two dimensions: the speed of information dissemination in the network and 
the degree of control of information in the network by institutional investors.

institutional investor is a node, and two institutional investors are connected by a straight line. Network density is defined as the ratio

3.2.4. Information asymmetry
Referring to the method of Ascioglu et al. (2005), the KV measurement method is used to measure the degree of information 

asymmetry of listed companies. The KV measurement method measures the information disclosure quality through the slope of the 
regression of stock return on trading volume. The basic principle is that if the information disclosure quality of listed companies is 
poor, investors mainly obtain information based on the trading volume of listed companies’ stocks, and have a low degree of 
dependence on information disclosure. At this time, changes in trading volume will cause large changes in stock prices, so the rate of 
change is an inverse indicator of information disclosure quality, that is, the smaller the KV value, the better the information disclosure 
quality. And the better the quality of information disclosure, the lower the degree of information asymmetry.

5. Mechanism test

paper further attempts to explore the mechanism by which information sharing of institutional investor affects corporate risk-taking 
level. We use the following empirical model to test the mediating role of information asymmetry and analyst herding behavior:

and network centrality, and the remaining variables are consistent with the baseline model. If the signs of α1 and γ1 are significantly 
negative, it means that information asymmetry and analyst herding behavior play the mediating role in the impact of institutional 
investors’ information sharing on corporate risk-taking level.

6. Heterogeneity analysis

The previous analysis and empirical results show that information sharing in institutional investor networks can produce external 
supervision effect and resource advantage effect, which prompt managers to carry out high-risk investment activities that are more 
beneficial to enterprise development, and provide enterprises with corresponding resources support, thereby improving the level of 
corporate risk-taking. However, a company’s operating activities and investment strategies will also be affected by the company’s 
internal governance mechanism and the external environment. For companies with different characteristics, the impact of institutional 
investor networks on corporate risk-taking levels may be different. In consequence, based on the previous analysis, this paper further 
studies the heterogeneity of institutional investor information sharing affecting corporate risk-taking levels from the perspectives of 
corporate internal governance and external environment.

6.3. Perspective of internal corporate governance

6.3.1. Agency costs
Under the system of separation of ownership and management rights, principal-agent problems are common in enterprises. 

Corporate owners with ownership rights and managers with management rights have inconsistent utility functions, resulting in 
managers not necessarily making investment decisions based on shareholders’ demands and wealth maximization. Companies with 
more serious agency problems have managers with more decision-making autonomy and more room for opportunistic behavior, which 
may affect the level of corporate risk-taking. Based on the above analysis, this paper uses the management expense ratio as the variable 
of corporate agency cost according to the method of Ang et al. (2000), divides the sample into enterprises with low agency cost and 
high agency cost according to the median level, and examines the differences in the impact of institutional investor information sharing 
on corporate risk-taking levels in corporate samples with different agency costs.

The regression results are shown in Table 14. The results in columns (1) and (2) of the table show that in enterprises with higher 
agency costs, the coefficients of network density and network centrality are significantly positive on the level at 5 % and 1 % 
respectively. The results in columns (3) and (4) of the table show that in enterprises with low agency costs, the coefficients of network 
density and network centrality are not significant. The above results show that the more severe the agency costs, the higher the 
possibility of corporate management to engage in opportunistic behavior, so the supervisory effect of institutional investors’ infor
mation sharing can play a more significant role. That is, the higher the agency costs within a company, the more the institutional 
investor network can improve the level of corporate risk-taking.

7. Conclusion

Based on the social network theory, this paper constructs an institutional investor network by connecting institutional investors 
with heavy holdings of shares of the same listed company, and studies the impact of information sharing among institutional investors 
within the network on corporate risk-taking levels. The study found that information sharing among institutional investors can 
improve corporate risk-taking levels. The higher the efficiency of information sharing, the higher the risk-taking level of listed 
companies. In particular, this paper finds that institutional investor information sharing affects corporate risk-taking levels by reducing 
the degree of information asymmetry and reducing analyst herding behavior. In order to verify the robustness of the conclusion, we 
successively changed the calculation method of corporate risk-taking level, the measurement method of information sharing effi
ciency, the sample selection time range, and used the first-order lag test and instrumental variable method to control endogeneity. In 
addition, based on the internal and external perspectives of the enterprise, the difference in the network governance role of institu
tional investors under different circumstances was examined. The analysis found that in samples with high degree of financialization, 
high financing constraints, no political connections, high agency costs, good information disclosure quality, good legal environment, 
and relatively developed regional economies, The analysis found that in samples with a high degree of financialization, high financing 
constraints, no political connections, high agency costs, good internal control quality, good legal environment, and growth stage 
company, institutional investor information sharing can more significantly improve the enterprises risk-taking level.

Based on the above research conclusions and combined with the governance situation of listed companies in China, this paper puts 
forward the following suggestions: (1) Listed companies should actively introduce institutional investors, increase the shareholding 
ratio of institutional investors, so as to form a multi-dimensional institutional investor network and improve corporate risk-taking 
level. (2) Institutional investors should make full use of resources in the network to enhance their voice in participating in corpo
rate governance. So institutional investors need to strengthen their connections with other institutional investors or market partici
pants, and make up for the disadvantages caused by information asymmetry, as well as improving the ability to participate in the 
governance of listed companies. (3) Policymakers should further introduce relevant policies and measures to continuously improve the 
market environment, and provide a better market environment and institutional guarantee for the long-term development of
