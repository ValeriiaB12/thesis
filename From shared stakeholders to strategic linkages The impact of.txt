1. Introduction

The modern economy is characterized by two critical features. First, the production organization is increasingly reliant on 
complex and interlocking supply chains (Carvalho et al., 2021). Second, the ownership of corporate equity has been dominated 
by large institutional investors, leading to the rise of common ownership—where shares of multiple firms are held by the same 
investor (Azar, 2020; Ben-David et al., 2021; Galeotti and Ghiglino, 2021). Together, firms are not only linked through physical 
flows of goods and services in supply chains but also through shared ownership structures that potentially align their incentives 
and strategies (Xu et al., 2023). However, existing literature primarily focuses on each network in isolation, leaving the interaction 
between supply chains and common ownership networks as an important but underexplored area of study.

To bridge this gap, we investigate how common ownership shapes a firm’s supply chain diversification and conduct an in-
depth analysis of its effects on both the intensive and extensive margins of supply-chain linkages. Our results indicate that a firm’s 
ownership overlap with others reduces its reliance on a few key suppliers and diversifies the firm’s supply chain. Furthermore, we 
perform higher-dimensional regressions at the supplier–buyer–year level, finding that the ownership overlap of two firms encourages 
them to form new supply-chain linkages and strengthen the linkages between them, especially for those newly formed. Additionally, 
we explore the mechanisms behind this. Firms with shared ownership prefer to construct tight supply-chain linkages due to lower 
transaction costs and higher information transparency.

oped legal and financial systems lead to weak contract enforcement and heavy reliance on relational governance (Allen et al., 2005; 
Gu et al., 2008), bringing additional costs and barriers for Chinese firms to diversify their supply chains (Du et al., 2012; Fan et al.,

Using institutional shareholdings from the China Stock Market & Accounting Research (CSMAR) database, we follow Ederer and 
Pellegrino (2022) to measure each two firms’ ownership overlap in tradable A-shares and calculate each firm’s aggregate ownership 
overlap with others. Combining it with top 5 supplier information of listed firms, we begin our analysis by investigating the effects of 
common ownership on supply chain diversification. After accounting for control variables, firm fixed effects, year fixed effects, and 
industry- and province-specific linear time trends, we find that a 1% increase in the degree of ownership overlap is associated with 
a 0.47 percentage point reduction in the purchase share from the top 5 suppliers and a 0.034% reduction in purchase concentration 
across them. This indicates that common ownership reduces a firm’s reliance on key suppliers and helps diversify its supply chain.

Furthermore, we carefully address potential endogeneity concerns that may bias our baseline results. One standout issue is 
self-disclosure bias: supplier information is typically limited to a firm’s top 5 suppliers and is not mandatorily reported. This may 
lead to incomplete or selective observations of supply chain structures. To address the limited disclosure, we predict undisclosed 
supply-chain linkages through various graph neural networks to enrich the observed networks. Predicted linkages are combined with 
observed ones to construct a more complete measure for supply chain concentration. To correct for selection bias from voluntary 
disclosure, we additionally implement Heckman two-stage regressions, machine learning imputation, and inverse probability 
weighting.

Our results also suffer from concerns about reverse causality and omitted variable bias. To mitigate these, we exploit the launch 
of the Shanghai-Hong Kong (2014) and Shenzhen-Hong Kong (2016) Stock Connect programs as plausibly exogenous instrumental 
variables (IVs) for institutional ownership. Firms initially included in these programs saw significant increases in common ownership, 
which in turn led to more diversified supply chains. We also perform an event study and find no pre-treatment differences, supporting 
the causal interpretation of our IV estimates. Additionally, we construct an input-weighted peer CSI 300 index to capture exogenous 
variation in upstream institutional ownership. This measure averages the CSI 300 index weights of upstream industries based on 
input shares from the 2002 Input–Output Table, explicitly excluding the focal firm to mitigate endogeneity concerns related to firm 
characteristics. 

Beyond the IV strategies, we further address concerns about reverse causality—for example, firms with diversified supply chains 
are more attractive for institutional investors—through three complementary approaches: (i) lagging the explanatory variables, (ii) 
excluding common institutional investors that appear at or after the formation of supply-chain linkages, and (iii) directly controlling 
for the institutional ownership within firms. We also perform an additional set of robustness checks: (i) correcting the common 
ownership index to account for unobserved investors, (ii) using broader shareholding data (total shares rather than tradable A-
shares), (iii) applying alternative transformations, and (iv) excluding observations during three Black Swan events, namely the 
global financial crisis (2008–2009), the Chinese stock market crash (2015), and the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022).

For an in-depth analysis of how common ownership shapes a firm’s supply chain structure, we conduct regressions at the 
supplier–buyer–year level to examine the changes in the intensive and extensive margins of supply-chain linkages. Our findings 
reveal that ownership overlap strengthens supply-chain relationships, particularly by increasing purchase value and share in newly 
formed linkages, while its effect on incumbent relationships is negligible. Additionally, it increases the probability that two firms 
are connected through the supply chain, which is driven by the formation of new linkages rather than preventing the breakdown 
of existing linkages. These findings highlight the role of ownership overlap in fostering supply chain diversification by establishing 
and reinforcing new relationships.

We hypothesize two important mechanisms through which common ownership facilitates supply-chain linkages—reduced 
transaction costs and increased information transparency. First, common ownership aligns the incentives of firms, eases negotiation, 
and lowers coordination and enforcement costs (Liu and He, 2024; Tian et al., 2024), making it more economical for firms to establish 
new supply-chain relationships. Consistent with this, we find common ownership plays a more pronounced role in establishing 
and reinforcing new supply-chain linkages when the supplier or buyer has high transaction costs.1 Second, common ownership 
provides a better information environment by reducing asymmetries and improving the readability and comparability of financial 
statements (Park et al., 2019, 2024; Dai et al., 2024). This mechanism is also corroborated with our data. Specifically, the role of 
common ownership in encouraging and strengthening supply-chain linkages is more pronounced in less transparent environments, 
where the supplier or buyer hires non-Big 4 auditors.

et al., 2021; Lewellen and Lowry, 2021; Hou and Liu, 2023), productivity, prices, output, markups, market shares (Antón et al., 
2023), entry (Newham et al., 2018), and social responsibility (Dai and Qiu, 2020; Yin et al., 2024). Although some studies have 
explored common ownership along the supply chain (Cheung et al., 2020; Aviv and Shamir, 2021; Dai et al., 2021), they focus on

2. Literature review

The past several decades have witnessed a growing concentration of corporate equity ownership in the hands of large institutional 
investors (Davis, 2008; Fichtner et al., 2017; Azar et al., 2018), such as mutual funds, pension funds, and sovereign wealth funds. This 
growing concentration has given rise to common ownership, with studies highlighting its rapid expansion in both developed and 
emerging markets (Backus et al., 2021; Ederer and Pellegrino, 2022; Dai et al., 2024). This ownership structure can be broadly 
categorized into horizontal and vertical shareholdings. The former refers to a group of investors owning substantial stakes in 
competing corporations within the same product market (Elhauge, 2016). The latter is that common investors hold equity stakes of 
vertically related firms, i.e., firms engaged at different levels of the supply chain (Güth et al., 2007).

Prior studies have extensively examined the anti-competitive implications of horizontal shareholdings, particularly in con-
centrated markets (Elhauge, 2016; Azar et al., 2018; Koch et al., 2021; Lewellen and Lowry, 2021; Antón et al., 2023). Many 
theoretical models and empirical studies suggest that horizontal shareholdings can drive up prices, increase markups, and decrease 
market output. The rationale is very intuitive. Rather than solely striving for firm profit maximization, managers seek to maximize 
shareholder payoffs, which are determined by both the firm’s own profits and those of its competitors (Gramlich and Grundl, 2017; 
Ederer and Pellegrino, 2022). This creates incentives for firms under common ownership to internalize the profits of their horizontal 
competitors rather than engage in aggressive competition. Due to its anti-competitive effect, the rise of common ownership can lead 
to considerable welfare loss (Ederer and Pellegrino, 2022).

Beyond its direct role in shaping product market competition, common ownership has broader economic implications. Co-
owned firms seeking to maximize shareholder payoffs are expected to internalize any externalities they impose on other firms, 
regardless of whether these firms are horizontal competitors, vertically integrated partners, or connected in other ways (Schmalz, 
2018). For example, Edmans et al. (2019) find common investors can strengthen the governance for their portfolio firms through 
actively intervening and strategically selling securities, even when the co-owned firms operate in unrelated industries. According 
to Antón et al. (2024), common ownership can foster innovation through spillover internalization but may also hinder innovation 
by weakening competitive incentives. Moreover, Cheng et al. (2023) find that the degree of ownership overlap negatively affects a 
firm’s social responsibility.

Among these various externalities, some studies have demonstrated that ownership overlap motivates firms to internalize the 
impact of their information disclosure on other firms that share common investors (Park et al., 2019). According to Pawliczek 
and Nicole Skinner (2018), common ownership positively affects disclosure through two mechanisms. First, common ownership 
lessens competition and lowers proprietary costs of disclosure (Verrecchia, 1990, 2001). Second, common ownership may lead 
firms to seek anti-competitive outcomes (i.e., tacit collusion), and public disclosure can play a key communication role in facilitating 
tacit collusion (Levenstein and Suslow, 2006; Pawliczek et al., 2022). Consistent with these theories, existing studies find common 
ownership is positively related to information disclosure (Park et al., 2019; Pawliczek and Nicole Skinner, 2018; Mou et al., 2024). 
Besides this, common ownership has also been found to improve the disclosure quality. Park et al. (2024) emphasize common 
ownership among banks improves transparency, manifested as higher financial statement readability and comparability. Schiehll 
and Kolahgar (2025) find common ownership encourages firms to improve the comparability and financial materiality of their ESG 
information.

There is also a growing literature focusing on the ownership overlap along the supply chain and its impacts on the performance 
of firms within the supply chain (Cheung et al., 2020; Aviv and Shamir, 2021; Dai et al., 2021). However, these studies provide 
no direct evidence about how common ownership shapes supply chain structures. Previous studies have shown other ownership 
linkages, such as merger and corporate equity stakes, can mitigate vertical conflicts, align the goals of suppliers and buyers, and 
strengthen the supply-chain relationships (Fan and Wong., 2005; Fee et al., 2006; Lafontaine and Slade, 2007; Garfinkel and 
Hankins., 2011). Compared to these direct equity connections, there has been comparatively less research on how the indirect 
connection through shared stakeholders affects supply chain relationships. To our knowledge, the only exception is Freeman (2023), 
who finds that the ownership overlap between suppliers and buyers boosts their trust and extends the duration of their supply chain

3. Data, variables, and empirical specification

In this section, we describe our data sources and detail how to measure supply chain diversification and common ownership. 
Then, we present the empirical specification used to explore the relationship between common ownership and supply chain 
diversification.

3.1. Data

We rely on three firm-level datasets during the period 2006–2023 sourced from the CSMAR database to conduct our analysis. 
The first one records the top 5 suppliers of listed firms, including the purchase value and share attributable to each supplier. This 
information facilitates the measurement of supply chain diversification and allows us to conduct a detailed analysis of the changes 
in supply chain structures. The second one provides detailed records about institutional shareholdings, which are used to assess the 
degree of overlap in institutional ownership between listed firms. The third one is the basic information and financial performance 
of each firm, serving as the primary source for control variables. In the empirical analysis, we exclude firms from the financial 
industry, with abnormal listing status, and with missing values in key variables and obtain 24,299 firm-year observations.

Our sample period spans from 2006 to 2023, primarily determined by the availability of key data required for our analysis. 
Specifically, one of our IVs relies on the constituent stock information of the CSI 300 index, which was officially launched in April 
2005. To ensure consistency and reliability, we begin our analysis in 2006.

4. Main results

In this section, we first present our main empirical findings and then apply a variety of strategies to address the potential endo-
geneity concerns, including the self-disclosure bias, reverse causality, and omitted variable bias. Furthermore, we provide additional 
evidence for robustness by correcting the COI to account for unobserved investors, utilizing a broader scope of shareholdings to 
identify ownership overlap, excluding observations during major emergencies, and applying alternative transformations.

4.2. Endogeneity concerns

Our baseline regressions show that common ownership leads to a more diversified supply chain. However, this result may suffer 
from some endogeneity concerns. In this section, we discuss potential sources of endogeneity, such as sample selection bias, reverse 
causality, and omitted variables, and outline several strategies to address them.
4.2.1. Self-disclosure bias

The use of supplier information may give rise to two important concerns about self-disclosure bias. First, since firms typically 
report only their top 5 suppliers, this limited disclosure may cannot fully capture the extent of supply chain concentration. Second, 
supplier information is not mandatorily disclosed; firms can choose whether to disclose this information. As a result, the sample may 
be subject to self-selection, where firms that disclose their supplier data differ systematically from those that do not. This could bias 
our estimates if the disclosure decision is correlated with the degree of ownership overlap or other unobserved firm characteristics.
Limited disclosure of the top suppliers. To address the limitation that firm disclosures are restricted to top 5 suppliers, we apply graph 
neural networks (GNNs) to predict undisclosed supply-chain linkages and enrich the observed networks. For robustness, we use a 
variety of GNN models—including Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) (Kipf and Welling, 2016), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 
2017), Graph Attention Networks (GAT) (Veliǩović et al., 2017), Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018), and Graph 
Autoencoders (GAE) (Kipf and Welling, 2016b). See detailed introduction of these models and training process in Appendix A.1.1.

4.2.2. Reverse causality and omitted variable bias
Another main concern is the reverse causality. While we argue that common ownership influences supply chain diversification, it 

is also possible that firms with more diversified supply chains are more attractive to institutional investors, leading to higher levels of 
common ownership. In addition, unobserved factors—such as firm-level management strategies, industry policies, or macroeconomic 
conditions—may simultaneously affect both ownership structure and supply chain choices. If these factors are not fully accounted 
for, our estimates may suffer from omitted variable bias.
Instrument strategies. Following Dai et al. (2024), we first leverage the initial implementation of the Shanghai-Hong Kong (SH-HK) 
and Shenzhen-Hong Kong (SZ-HK) Stock Connect programs as plausibly exogenous shocks to institutional ownership. The SH-HK 
Connect, launched in November 2014, allows investors in Shanghai and Hong Kong to trade eligible stocks listed on each other’s 
exchanges through local securities firms or brokers. The SZ-HK Connect, launched in December 2016, extends this cross-boundary 
investment mechanism to include stocks listed on the Shenzhen Stock Exchange. Both programs generated exogenous variation in 
institutional ownership by enabling foreign investors to directly trade eligible mainland stocks through the Hong Kong exchange. 
Specifically, we identify firms included in the initial eligibility lists of SH-HK and SZ-HK Connects and use them as our treatment 
group. These initial inclusion lists were determined by regulatory authorities based on pre-specified criteria, plausibly exogenous to 
firms’ unobservable supply chain strategies.

Table 7 presents the 2SLS estimates using the shocks of SH-HK and SZ-HK Connect launches as IVs. In Column (1), we find 
that firms included in the initial eligibility lists for these Connect programs experience a significant increase in ownership overlap 
following their implementation. In Columns (2) and (3), an exogenous increase in common ownership, instrumented by the Connect 
launch shocks, leads to a statistically significant reduction in the purchase share from the top 5 suppliers and the purchase 
concentration among them, consistent with our baseline results.

5. Mechanism analysis

In this section, we first conduct a further analysis on why common ownership contributes to supply chain diversification. 
Specifically, we investigate how common ownership shapes supply chain structure along the intensive and extensive margins.

5.1. A closer look at the changes in supply chain structure

For an in-depth analysis of how common ownership shapes a firm’s supply chain structure, we employ a supplier-firm-level 
regression, which is specified as follows:

ownership overlap strengthens the supply-chain linkages between firms.

Furthermore, we differentiate all linkages into new and incumbent ones to better understand the changes in the supply chain.

incumbent linkages, the impact of common ownership on purchase share is statistically indistinguishable from 0 (Column (6)). We 
can conclude that ownership overlap reinforces the intensity of supply-chain linkages, with its impact being more pronounced on 
newly established relationships than on existing ones.

results reported in Table 13. With a logit model, Column (1) shows that shared institutional investors increase the probability 
that two firms are connected through the supply chain, statistically significant at the 1% level. Furthermore, the presence of 
common ownership fosters the creation of new supply-chain linkages between previously unconnected firms (Column (2)), while this 
ownership overlap has no significant effect on the disruption of existing relationships (Column (3)). Columns (4)–(6) utilize probit 
models to explore the changes in the extensive margin of supply-chain linkages and yield consistent findings. In summary, common 
ownership positively affects the extensive margin of supply-chain linkages, and this effect is driven by fostering the formation of 
new relationships, rather than preventing the breakdown of existing ones.

5.3. Common ownership and information transparency

Another important mechanism is that common ownership increases information transparency. Specifically, ownership overlap 
motivates firms to internalize the positive externalities of their information disclosure for co-owned firms (Park et al., 2019), improve 
the readability and comparability of their financial statements (Park et al., 2024), and inhibit opportunistic insider behaviors (Dai 
et al., 2024). Therefore, common ownership fosters an environment of trust and cooperation by reducing information asymmetries 
between firms, encouraging firms to construct supply-chain linkages. To test this mechanism, we demonstrate that ownership overlap 
plays a more prominent role in encouraging the construction and strengthening supply-chain linkages in an environment with more 
asymmetric information.

We use the engagement of Big 4 auditors (PwC, Deloitte, EY, and KPMG) as a proxy for information transparency. This practice 
is supported by a long-established literature. DeAngelo (1981) emphasizes that audit quality is positively associated with auditor 
size and reputation, with larger firms providing superior audit services. Consistently, there is a general consensus that compared 
to non-Big 4 auditors, Big 4 auditors are more credible and offer a more accurate reflection of firms’ financial conditions (Fan and 
Wong., 2005; Francis and Wang, 2008). Hiring Big 4 auditors serves as a strong signal of a firm’s commitment to high transparency.

First, we investigate the heterogeneous effects of common ownership on the intensive margin of supply-chain linkages in low 
and high information transparency environments, with the results reported in Columns (1)–(4) of Table 15. Column (1) yields a 
significant positive relationship between two firms’ ownership overlap and the intensity of their supply-chain linkages in terms of 
purchase value if the supplier hires non-Big 4 auditors, while Column (2) indicates this effect is statistically indistinguishable from 0 
if the supplier is audited by Big 4 auditors. Moving to the buyer’s information transparency in Columns (3) and (4), we continue to 
find evidence supporting our mechanism. For buyer firms that hire non-Big 4 auditors, their ownership overlap with their suppliers 
significantly increases their purchases from the suppliers, while this effect is statistically insignificant in the buyer group with Big 4 
auditors. These findings suggest that common ownership plays a more pronounced role in strengthening the supply-chain linkages 
in a less transparent environment.

Second, we turn to the different changes in the extensive margin of supply-chain linkages in low and high information 
transparency environments in Columns (5)–(8) of Table 15. Columns (5) and (7) show that ownership overlap increases the 
probability of a supply-chain linkage between two firms in a less transparent environment where the supplier or buyer hires non-Big 
4 auditors. However, this effect is insignificant in a more transparent environment, as the coefficients of the log COI in Columns (6) 
and (8) are statistically indistinguishable from 0 at the 5% level. Table A.19 corrects the index to account for unobserved investors 
and reports consistent results with Table 15.

In summary, common ownership plays a more pronounced role in encouraging the establishment of new supply-chain linkages 
and strengthening these linkages when information is more opaque. Improving information transparency is another important 
mechanism through which common ownership facilitates the diversification of supply chains.

This paper offers several implications. First, as diversified supply chains can effectively mitigate disruptions from specific

enhance supply chain resilience. Second, this paper highlights the role of information transparency and reduced transaction costs

There are several caveats to this paper, of which two points are emphasized here. First, due to data constraints, we can only access

common ownership’s effect on supply chain diversification, it does not directly assess the long-term performance implications of 
these diversified supply chains, such as operational efficiency, profitability, or firm growth. Further research could investigate how 
these changes in supply chain structure influence firm performance over time.

A.1.1. Link prediction through GNN models
We implement several GNN models, including GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT, GIN, and GAE, to estimate both the existence and intensity 

of inter-firm connections. These models differ mainly in how they aggregate and process neighborhood information. GCN aggregates 
information from all neighboring nodes simultaneously using a normalized adjacency matrix, applying a shared transformation 
across the entire neighborhood. It is simple yet effective in capturing local graph structure, serving as a baseline for link prediction. 
GraphSAGE samples a fixed-size subset of neighbors and aggregates their features through learnable functions, enabling better 
scalability and inductive learning on large or evolving graphs. GAT introduces attention mechanisms to assign varying importance 
to neighbors, allowing the model to focus on more relevant connections. GIN employs multilayer perceptrons to achieve stronger 
representational power and is theoretically capable of maximum discriminative performance among graph structures. In contrast, 
GAE is unsupervised and aims to reconstruct the adjacency matrix based on learned embeddings, making it particularly suitable 
when edge attributes are sparse. 

Taking the GCN model as an example, we reconstruct the measure of supply chain concentration in the following steps:
• For each year, we construct a bipartite directed graph where the two sets of nodes represent suppliers and listed customer 
firms. For suppliers without institutional identifiers, we use their customers’ stock codes and their purchase ranking to generate 
unique identifiers. All observed supplier–customer pairs in the dataset constitute the positive edges, whose weights represent 
the log purchase value.

• We train a three-layer GCN model. To train the model, we generate negative samples by randomly selecting unobserved 
supplier–customer pairs not present in the disclosure data. For each positive edge, two negative edges are sampled to balance 
the training data. When training the model, we optimize a combined loss function: binary cross-entropy for link existence 
and mean squared error for link weight, measured by the log purchase value. In this step, we adopt early stopping based on 
validation loss to prevent overfitting.

• After training, we apply the model to all firm pairs to estimate link probabilities and predicted weights. A link is assumed to 
exist if its probability exceeds 50%, yielding a dense, imputed supply chain network for each year.

• Finally, we compute the HHI across all supply chain relationships, combining both observed and predicted ones. For disclosed 
relationships, we use the reported purchase values; for predicted ones, we use GCN-estimated values. Note that each firm’s 
total purchase can be inferred from the reported values and shares purchased from its top 5 suppliers. If the combined value 
of observed and predicted purchases exceeds this total, the predicted values are proportionally scaled to ensure consistency.
