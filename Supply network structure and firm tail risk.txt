1. Introduction

The economy is susceptible to external shocks, which can lead to the diffusion of financial risks and potentially cause systematic 
disruptions. Asset tail risk, in particular, has far-reaching effects and can trigger large-scale financial crises (Aloui et al., 2022). Prior 
research has examined various determinants of tail risk, including political uncertainty, ESG disclosure (Reber et al., 2022), and asset 
crowdedness (Brown et al., 2022). However, the literature has largely overlooked the role of supply chains in influencing such risks. In 
today’s highly interconnected environment, firms are embedded in complex supply chain networks through their relationships with 
clients and suppliers. A key feature of these networks is their internal structure, which has been shown to significantly impact firm 
innovation (Bellamy et al., 2014) and investment efficiency (Shi et al., 2020). A more resilient supply chain network can also facilitate 
information transmission (Chuluun et al., 2014) and risk sharing (Fafchamps and Gubert, 2007; Larcker et al., 2013) among firms. This 
study enriches the related literature by investigating the impact of supply chain network structure on systemic tail risk.

Based on the literature in supply chain network structure, our main hypothesis is that firms with higher interconnectedness in the 
supply chain network incur lower systematic tail risks. There are two primary reasons. First, firms with a robust network structure tend 
to have a larger number of clients and suppliers, which facilitates the flow of information. This enhanced information transmission 
enables firms to obtain valuable insights more efficiently (Chuluun et al., 2014) and accelerates price discovery. During adverse events, 
firms with higher information efficiency are better able to incorporate private information (Jin and Myers, 2006), making them less 
vulnerable to investor sell-offs. Second, a well-connected supply chain network involves more partners, which reduces contracting

more likely to receive support from their network partners, thereby lowering their default risk. Overall, a more interconnected and

We utilize data from Chinese A-share listed companies on the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges from 2009 to 2022. Using 
disclosed information on the top five customers and suppliers from annual reports, we construct supply chain network structures to 
examine their impact on financial stability, with a particular focus on systemic tail risk. Our empirical evidence suggests that stronger 
supply chain network structures are associated with lower firm-level systemic tail risk. We further demonstrate that this effect operates 
primarily through two mechanisms: improved information efficiency and enhanced risk diversification. Heterogeneity analyses reveal 
that the mitigating effect of supply chain network structure on systemic tail risk is more pronounced in firms with greater analyst and 
media coverage, higher-quality accounting disclosures, closer geographic proximity to customers or suppliers, and lower financial 
constraints. Additionally, we perform a series of robustness checks, including the PSM-DID approach, the Heckman two-stage model, 
and alternative variable constructions. The main results remain consistent and robust across these tests.

Our study contributes to the literature on supply chain network structure. While prior research has extensively examined the 
conceptualization, characteristics, and determinants of supply chain networks (Ponis and Koronis, 2012; Chopra and Sodhi, 2014; 
Hosseini et al., 2019), relatively little attention has been paid to the implications of structural features within these networks. Bellamy 
et al. (2014) show that supply chain network structure influences corporate innovation, and Shi et al. (2020) find that firms located at 
the center of supply networks are better able to mitigate underinvestment. Wu and Birge (2014) examine how a firm’s centrality within 
the supply chain affects stock returns, particularly across industries. However, the impact of supply chain network structure on 
firm-level risk, especially systemic tail risk, and the mechanisms through which it operates remain underexplored. By focusing on 
Chinese listed companies, our study fills this gap by providing novel empirical evidence on how supply chain network structure in
fluences corporate tail risk. In doing so, we contribute to a deeper understanding of the intersection between supply chain dynamics 
and financial market stability.

This paper also offers a novel perspective on the determinants of firm-level tail risk. Prior studies have identified various drivers of 
tail risk, including political uncertainty, ESG disclosure (Reber et al., 2022), and asset crowdedness (Brown et al., 2022). In contrast, 
we focus on the role of supply chain network structure, recognizing that firms are embedded in interconnected supply networks and are 
closely linked to their peers. Our findings show that a more robust supply chain network structure significantly improves information 
efficiency and enhances risk diversification, thereby reducing systemic tail risk.

The structure of the paper is organized as follows: Part 2 presents the literature review and hypothesis formulation. Part 3 describes 
the data, variables, and research design. Part 4 provides the empirical results. Part 5 investigates the mechanisms and conducts 
heterogeneity tests. Part 6 concludes.

2. Literature review and hypothesis development

2.1. Supply chain network structure

In recent years, a growing body of literature has examined the roles of social networks in information dissemination, resource 
acquisition, risk sharing, behavior shaping, and the development of group trust (Larcker et al., 2013; Bianchi et al., 2022; Wu et al., 
2024). Scholars have constructed social networks involving directors, CEOs, analysts, auditors, and investors based on various types of 
cross-firm relationships, providing empirical evidence of their influence on corporate operations (Hochberg et al., 2007, 2010; Larcker 
et al., 2013; El-Khatib et al., 2015; Ahern, 2017; Bianchi, 2018; Rossi et al., 2018).

Within the supply chain network structure, suppliers, manufacturers, service providers, alliance partners, and customers are 
connected through activities related to the procurement, production, and delivery of goods and services (Kouvelis et al., 2006; 
Lamming et al., 2000). Prior research suggests that network participants benefit from both direct and indirect connections 
(Granovetter, 1973; Burt, 1995) as well as their positions within the network (Adler and Kwon, 2002; Borgatti and Foster, 2003). Firms 
with a stronger supply chain network structure possess greater social resources, enabling them to adopt risk-mitigating strategies such 
as enhancing supply chain collaboration (Pettit et al., 2013), making better supplier selections (Hosseini et al., 2019), eliminating 
redundancies (Ponis and Koronis, 2012), and actively fostering social capital and relationships (Johnson et al., 2013). Empirical 
studies have examined how the structure of supply chain networks affects firm innovation (Bellamy et al., 2014), opportunism (Dong 
et al., 2015), productive efficiency (Kao et al., 2017), profitability (Kim, 2017), and investment efficiency (Shi et al., 2020). However, 
the literature has largely overlooked the impact of supply chain network structure on systemic risk. Our paper addresses this gap by 
providing empirical evidence on how supply chain network structure influences systemic tail risk.

2.2. Systemic tail risk

Systemic tail risk refers to the risk of substantial asset losses during periods of extreme market downturns. Compared to general 
financial risks, systemic tail risk has broader economic implications and can trigger large-scale financial crises. Scholars have 
increasingly recognized systemic tail risk as an important factor in risk pricing (Kelly and Jiang, 2014; Van Oordt and Zhou, 2016; 
Chabi-Yo et al., 2018). Kelly and Jiang (2014) hypothesize that the left tail of the asset return distribution follows a dynamic power law 
process and find that stocks with higher systemic tail risk in the U.S. market tend to generate higher future returns. Van Oordt and Zhou 
(2016) develop a tail beta index to measure the sensitivity of asset prices to extreme market risks, demonstrating significant risk premia 
associated with tail beta. Chabi-Yo et al. (2018) employ a mixture of dynamic copula functions to assess individual stock sensitivity to

Other studies have explored the drivers of systemic tail risk, highlighting the impacts of political uncertainty, ESG disclosure (Reber

crowdedness significantly explain downside tail risk. Additionally, under the impact of Black Swan events, tail risk spillovers among 
RMB exchange rates exhibit significant dynamic and time-varying characteristics (Huang and Zhang, 2024). Building on the approach 
of Kelly and Jiang (2014), this study estimates systemic tail risk and addresses a gap in the literature by examining the determinants of 
systemic risk from the perspective of supply chain network structure.

2.3. The impact of supply chain network structure on systemic tail risks

This study posits that the supply chain network structure helps mitigate systemic tail risk through two primary mechanisms. First, 
networks serve as channels for aggregating and disseminating various types of information (Freeman, 2002). Firms with a robust 
network structure maintain numerous client and supplier relationships that facilitate information flow, allowing them to access 
valuable information more easily (Chuluun et al., 2014). Consequently, information is incorporated into stock prices more rapidly. 
During extreme market events, firms with higher information efficiency better reflect private information (Jin and Myers, 2006), 
making them less susceptible to large investor sell-offs and thus reducing the likelihood of systemic tail risk.

Second, from the perspective of risk diversification, a strong supply chain network structure includes many potential stakeholders, 
enabling more resource sharing through financial transactions with other companies. Studies such as Larcker et al. (2013) indicate that 
board networks provide risk insurance for firms, facilitating contract establishment and reducing contracting costs among firms. When 
facing liquidity constraints or inadequate credit allocation, firms with a stronger supply chain network structure are more likely to 
receive assistance from other network members, thereby dispersing individual default risks, thereby lowering systemic tail risks as the 
market experiences severe downturns. Hence, we propose the following hypothesis H1: 
H1. : Within supply chain networks, firms with stronger supply chain network structure exhibit lower systemic tail risks.

3. Data and variables

3.1. Data source and sample selection

To conduct empirical analyses, we begin with a sample of all A-share Chinese listed firms from 2009 to 2022. Supply chain network 
data comes from the China Research Data Services Platform (CNRDS). Monthly stock trading data and company financial statements 
are obtained from the China Stock Market & Accounting Research (CSMAR) database. We exclude financial and ST (Special Treatment) 
firms and remove firms with missing data in our sample. Our final sample consists of 9414 firm-year observations. To mitigate the 
influence of outliers, all continuous variables are winsorized at the 1st and 99th percentiles.

3.2.2. Independent variable: supply chain network structure
Common indicators used to characterize a firm’s position within networks include various measures of network centrality, such as 

degree centrality, closeness centrality, betweenness centrality, and eigenvector centrality. Among these, degree centrality is a primary 
measure of the importance of a node within a network, quantifying the number of connections a node has with other nodes. Firms with 
higher degree centrality are closer to the core of the network. Given large heterogeneity in connections between nodes, prior papers 
also measure weighted degree centrality, where weights are calculated based on the strength of connections between nodes. Drawing 
on approaches by Kim et al. (2011) and Allen et al. (2019), our study computes the weighted degree centrality in the supply chain 
network to gauge supply chain network structure.

Specifically, we compile two panel datasets from 2009 to 2022 for both the top five customer firms and top five supplier firms of 
listed companies, and construct directed weighted networks of customer-supplier relationships on an annual basis. Assuming the 
supply chain network is a directed weighted graph containing m firms, represented by elements 0≤ wi,j ≤ 1 of the m × m matrix, where 
wi,j＞0 denotes the total sales amount of products provided by firm i to enterprise j. We normalize the sales amount and use the 
NetworkX software package in Python to compute the weighted degree centrality. We then take the natural logarithm of these cen

4.3. Robustness checks

4.3.1. Endogeneity issues
To mitigate endogeneity issues, we first employ the Propensity Score Matching-Double Difference (PSM-DID) model, leveraging the 

"Chain Leader" policy shock.3 The "Chain Leader", proposed by Hunan Province in 2017 and implemented province-wide in Zhejiang 
Province in 2019, had been adopted by 29 provinces (autonomous regions, municipalities directly under the central government) in 
mainland China by December 2021. This policy aims to enhance industrial competitiveness and improve supply chain network 
structure through strategic initiatives.

The implementation of the “Chain Leader” policy is typically centrally and locally coordinated by the government. Its timing and 
scope are related to the supply chain network structure but are not endogenously linked to the risk characteristics of individual en
terprises. Thus, it can be considered an effective exogenous policy shock. Specifically, the Chain Leader policy is usually implemented 
in a top-down manner by local or central governments, and its policy motivation is unrelated to the micro characteristics of individual 
firms. Instead, it is more focused on macro-objectives such as regional industrial planning and supply chain security. Additionally, the 
timing of policy implementation is exogenous to firms, as they cannot predict or adjust their supply chain strategies in advance. The 
policy is typically implemented at the industry or regional level, rather than targeting specific companies, thus avoiding endogeneity 
issues that would arise if firms actively chose to enter the treatment group.

4.3.3. Alternative variable measurement
To ensure the robustness of our results, we first change the measurement of systemic tail risk. The selection of thresholds is crucial 

for estimating systemic tail risk. A threshold that is too high can result in a large number of non-extreme observations, while one that is 
too low may lead to a small number of observations. Therefore, we recalculate systemic tail risk using threshold levels of third and 
tenth percentile. Additionally, the choice of rolling regression windows can significantly affect the estimation of systemic tail risk due 
to variations in the number of valid observations. Hence, we reassess systemic tail risk using rolling windows of 48 months and 60 
months. Table 8 presents the corresponding regressions results. The findings consistently align with the main hypothesis.

Next, we change the measurement of supply chain network structure. In the baseline regression, supply chain network structure is 
measured using the natural logarithm of weighted degree centrality. For robustness checks, we employ alternative centrality measures, 
including the natural logarithm of node degree centrality (Lndegree, the natural logarithm of the number of directly connected nodes), 
standardized node degree centrality (Degree_centrality, the number of connected nodes divided by total nodes minus one), betweenness 
centrality (Betweenness), and closeness centrality (Closeness), all normalized (Z-scored) for comparability. Table 9 presents the cor
responding regression results. The findings are consistent with those of the baseline regression, indicating that the main conclusions 
remain robust with different measurements of the explanatory variable.

5. Underlying mechanisms and heterogeneity analysis

In the previous analyses, we demonstrate that enhancing supply chain network structure helps reduce future systemic tail risks for 
firms. However, the mechanism channels have not been explored. In this section, we investigate how supply chain network structure 
impacts systemic tail risk through channels such as information efficiency and risk diversification. Furthermore, we conduct het
erogeneity analysis to explore nuanced patterns in the relationship between supply chain network structure and systemic tail risk.

to lower systemic tail risks. In this study, we use stock synchronicity (Syn) as a measure of firm information efficiency, following the 
methodology of Durnev et al. (2003). Specifically, we obtain R-squared from regressions of weekly individual stock returns on market

inadequate credit allocation. The support from network members allows firms to better disperse individual default risks. Moreover, 
firms with lower individual risks are less susceptible to large investor sell-offs during extreme market events, resulting in lower sys
temic tail risks. To measure firm default risk,7 we use default distance (Dd), calculated following Bharath and Shumway’s (2008)

5.2. Heterogeneity analyses

5.2.1. Impact of capital market information intermediaries
Attention from analysts, news media, and other information intermediaries facilitates the dissemination of firm-specific infor

mation, which helps external investors understand corporate operational conditions in a timely manner. This study posits that 
heightened attention from information intermediaries enhances the role of supply chain network structure in improving corporate 
information efficiency, thereby exerting a stronger effect on reducing systemic tail risks. Specifically, we use analyst coverage 
(Ana_num, the natural logarithm of the number of analysts tracking the firm annually), analyst report coverage (Ana_report, the natural 
logarithm of the number of analyst reports published annually), and media coverage (News, the natural logarithm of the number of 
financial news articles annually sourced from CNRDS) to characterize attention from capital market information intermediaries. We 
divide the total sample into high and low groups based on the median value. Table 12 reports the findings and reveals that in groups 
with higher analyst coverage, analyst report coverage, and media coverage, the coefficient for Sns is significantly negative at the 1 % 
level. Conversely, in the low information intermediary attention groups, these coefficients are not significant. The significant differ
ences between the high and low groups verify our conjecture.

5.2.2. Impact of corporate accounting information quality
Accounting information reflects a company’s current financial and operating conditions, serving as a crucial source for investors to 

obtain firm-specific information. High-quality accounting information can convey more accurate firm-specific information to capital 
markets, thereby reducing stock price noise. This study posits that high-quality accounting information enhances the role of supply 
chain network structure in improving corporate information efficiency, thereby reinforcing its effect in reducing systemic tail risks. 
Specifically, we measure corporate accounting information quality based on earnings management (Em, manipulative accruals

5.2.3. Impact of customer or supplier geographic distance
When firms are located closer to their customers or suppliers, financial transactions and resource flow within the supply chain tend 

to be more rapid. This study posits that shorter geographic distances between customers or suppliers enhance supply chain network 
structure in mitigating default risks, thereby reinforcing its effect in reducing systemic tail risks. Specifically, we use the median of the 
natural logarithm of mean distances between firms and their customers or suppliers to divide the sample into high and low groups. 
Table 14 presents these findings, indicating that Sns coefficients are only significantly negative in the low customer or supplier distance 
group, supporting our assumption.

5.2.4. Impact of corporate financing constraints
Companies with lower degree of financing constraints face fewer liquidity crises within the supply chain. This study posits that 

fewer financing constraints enhance supply chain network structure in mitigating debt default risks and thus systemic tail risks. 
Specifically, we use the FC index and WW index to measure the extent of corporate financing constraints. The sample is divided into 
high and low groups based on the median values of these variables, where smaller FC and WW indices indicate lower financing 
constraints. Table 14 reports the findings, indicating that Sns coefficients are significantly negative at the 1 % level in the low financing 
constraints group, while they are not significant in the high financing constraints group. Moreover, there are significant differences of 
the coefficient between the high and low groups, verifying our conjecture.

5.2.5. Impact of supply chain transparency and extreme market conditions
Supply chain transparency reflects the degree to which external stakeholders understand a company’s supply chain network 

structure, which may influence our conclusions. Therefore, we conduct a heterogeneity analysis based on supply chain-related 
characteristics. Specifically, we measure enterprise supply chain transparency using the number of large suppliers and customers 
with disclosed names (Sct_Num) and the proportion of transactions from large suppliers and customers with disclosed names to the total 
transactions of the top five suppliers and customers (Sct_Ratio). Based on the median of the sample, we divide the full sample into high 
and low transparency groups. Table 15 shows that when a company’s supply chain transparency is higher, the centrality of the supply 
network has a more significant effect on mitigating systemic tail risk.

6. Conclusion

This study investigates how supply chain network structure influences financial stability in the capital market. We construct the 
supply chain network structure using data from the annual reports of Chinese A-share listed companies from 2009 to 2022, focusing on 
the top five customers and suppliers. By measuring the network structure, we examine its impact on financial stability. Our empirical 
results suggest that a stronger supply chain network structure is associated with lower systemic tail risk in the future. We further 
demonstrate that this effect operates through mechanisms of enhanced information efficiency and risk diversification. Additionally, 
the effect is more pronounced under conditions of greater attention from information intermediaries, higher quality accounting dis
closures, closer proximity to customers or suppliers, and lower financing constraints.

Our findings have practical implications for both governments and firms. This research provides micro-level evidence of the 
economic effects of supply chain network structure and suggests that enhancing supply chain network structure contributes to 
financial market stability. To build a robust supply chain network structure, managers should foster synergies among firms within the 
network through contractual arrangements that promote collaboration. Meanwhile, policymakers should focus on facilitating the 
efficient flow of information and resources within the network.
