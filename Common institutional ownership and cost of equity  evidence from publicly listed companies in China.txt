1. Introduction

Common institutional ownership (CIO) refers to the situation where the same institutional investors sim-
ultaneously hold shares in multiple companies in the same industry. According to Lewellen and Lowry
(2021), only 17% of the companies in the S&P 500 Index had CIO in 1999. By the end of 2015, this pro-
portion had increased to 81%.

One vein of the literature suggests that CIO facilitates synergistic governance among publicly listed
companies (PLCs) within an industry, provides convenience for information exchange and resource shar-
ing, promotes positive cooperation, and reduces unnecessary costs of destructive competition (Koch
et al., 2021). For example, Edmans et al. (2019) argue that under common ownership, management has
a higher motivation for active corporate governance because common institutional investors (CIIs) can
use their information advantage on the entire industry to sell stocks, transmitting negative signals to the
capital market and increasing pressure on managers. Park et al. (2019) confirm that CIO helps reduce
malicious competition within an industry, improve market liquidity, decrease proprietary information dis-
closure costs for PLCs, and enhance corporate governance. Gao et al. (2019) argues that the portfolio
maximization objective will prompt CIIs to coordinate actions among PLCs in the same industry within
their portfolio, thereby enhancing corporate governance and supervision. Ramalingegowda et al. (2021)
finds that higher CIO is related to less earnings management. Dennis et al. (2022) demonstrate that
there is an increase in voluntary information disclosure among firms as the level of CIO rises, indicating
that CIO reduces the costs of information disclosure and improves industry coordination mechanisms

in information, experience, and resources, can effectively inhibit corporate misconduct, earnings manage-
ment and stock price crash risk, respectively.

Another vein of literature has raised concerns about potential monopolistic practices and collusive
fraud in CIO. For example, Azar et al. (2018) ests that CIIs are more inclined to pursue short-term gains
without considering long-term development, which may lead to collusion with major shareholders to
encroach on the interests of minority shareholders. Lewellen and Lowry (2021) evaluates various empirical
approaches to identify the effects of CIO and finds little robust evidence that CIO positively affects firm
profitability, investment, mergers, or strategic alliances. Pawliczek et al. (2022) believes that CIIs aim to
maximize profits and have a strong motivation to collude with companies. Corporate collusion not only
reduces the positive effects of competition among companies but also lowers investment sensitivity,
ultimately causing companies to miss investment opportunities and reduce investment efficiency. Cheng
et al. (2022) finds that CIO is negatively associated with the level of corporate social responsibility, which
supports an anti-competitive view. Additionally, the anti-competitive effect is more pronounced for
mature firms, and for firms in industries with lower labor intensity and lower customer sensitivity. Kini
et al. (2024) finds that common ownership can inhibit product market competition and dynamism, espe-
cially in industries more prone to quasi-monopoly outcomes in product spaces. Anton et al. (2023) finds
that managerial incentives are less performance sensitive in firms with more common ownership.

Given the lack of consensus in the academic literature, it is evident that further research is needed to
gain a comprehensive understanding of the implications of CIO. Conducting studies that employ diverse
methodologies and data sources can provide valuable insights into the potential effects of CIO on vari-
ous aspects of corporate governance, performance, and strategic decision-making. However, most stud-
ies focus on developed capital markets, while only a few (e.g., Bai et al., 2023; Li & Liu, 2023; Wicaksono
et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023) explore the effects of CIO in emerging markets. Therefore, the objective of
this article is to examine whether and to what extent CIO influences the COE, using a dataset comprising
2682 Chinese PLCs during the period from 2012 to 2022.

The novelties and marginal contributions of this article are primarily manifested in the following three
aspects: Firstly, while prior research on CIO has primarily focused on its effects on corporate information
disclosure (Park et al., 2019; Pawliczek et al., 2022), innovation efficiency (Gao et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023),
mergers and acquisitions (Lewellen & Lowry, 2021), earnings management (Gao et al., 2024;
Ramalingegowda et al., 2021), financial misconduct (Wang et al., 2023) and stock price crash risk (Chen
et al., 2024) this article represents one of the first efforts to investigate the influence of CIO on COE. By
exploring this relationship, the article enriches the literature on the governance role of CIO and extends
our understanding of how CIO may affect firm valuation.

Secondly, while previous studies have examined the impact of institutional investors in general on
COE (Edmans et al., 2019; Gillan & Starks, 2000; Gompers et al., 2003; He & Huang, 2017), this article spe-
cifically focuses on CIIs and investigates their influence on COE through the lenses of ‘exit threat’ and
‘collaborative governance’ within the framework of CIO. This focused examination allows for a deeper
exploration of the mechanisms through which CIIs affect COE, thereby extending existing research on
the determinants of COE.

Thirdly, this article goes beyond merely examining the direct relationship between CIO and COE by
exploring the moderating roles of industry competition, corporate governance, and financial constraints.
By considering these contextual factors, the study aims to illuminate the complex interplay between CIO
and COE. Specifically, it investigates whether levels of industry competition, the quality of corporate gov-
ernance practices, and financing constraints influence the strength or direction of the relationship
between CIO and COE. This analysis provides a nuanced understanding of the underlying mechanisms
through which CIO affects COE and offers empirical evidence that can inform the formulation of relevant
policies by regulatory authorities.

2. Theoretical analysis and hypothesis development

Under traditional asset pricing theories, CIO would have to be related to a firm’s systematic risk to be
non-diversifiable and therefore priced into the firm’s cost of equity capital. According to existing studies,

CIO can reduce the COE through three different channels: Price informativeness, product market risks
and synergistic governance effects. Specifically, CIO can enhance the transparency and informativeness
of a firm’s financial data. This improved disclosure quality allows investors to better assess the firm’s
expected value and future cash flows, leading to a reduction in uncertainty and, consequently, a lower
COE (Boone & White, 2015; Edmans et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023). In addition, strategic alliances and
incentives to avoid competition in the product market allow firms to access larger markets, benefit from
economies of scale, and internalize negative externalities. This reduces a firm’s product market risk,
which is partially non-diversifiable, ultimately lowering the COE (Bloomfield et al., 2024; Koch et al.,
2021; Park et al., 2019). Furthermore, CIIs have both the incentives and the ability to influence their port-
folio firms’ cost of capital through corporate governance and disclosure changes. By promoting better
corporate governance practices and improving disclosure quality, CIIs can enhance investor confidence
and reduce the uncertainty surrounding future cash flows. This, in turn, reduces the portfolio firms’ COE
(Kang et al., 2018; Ni & Yin, 2023).

Firstly, in terms of price informativeness, CIIs typically hold a diversified portfolio consisting of rela-
tively good and relatively poor PLCs. When faced with liquidity risk or other crises, CIIs can flexibly
decide which assets to sell. In cases where CIIs hold more than 5% of the shares, their divestment can
have a detrimental impact on the underlying PLCs. The first assets to be sold are often the relatively
underperforming ones, which signal negative information of the companies to the market. This process
leads to a survival-of-the-fittest dynamic, exerting pressure on PLCs from various aspects and compelling
them to strengthen their governance practices through the mechanism of ‘exit threat’ (Boone & White,
2015; Edmans et al., 2019; Koch et al., 2021). Such a threat can lead to enhanced disclosure quality,
greater long-term shareholdings, and higher risk-absorption capacity (Khoo et al., 2024). In the Chinese
capital market, where herd behavior is significant, price information signals are more easily captured by
investors, making the informational content of price reactions deeper under the CIO framework. This, in
turn, compels managers to improve information disclosure and increase corporate transparency, which
can lead to lower COE (Wu et al., 2023).

Secondly, in terms of product market risks, CIO creates strategic alliances and incentives that enable
companies to collaborate rather than compete, yielding multiple economic and operational benefits
(Park et al., 2019). For example, firms can leverage their partners’ market presence, distribution networks,
and customer bases. The increased market access can lead to higher sales volumes and revenue streams,
enhancing the firm’s financial stability and reducing the variability in cash flows. This stability is attract-
ive to investors, as it signifies a lower risk profile (Gao et al., 2024). In addition, when companies collab-
orate, they can pool resources, share R&D costs, and optimize production processes. The resulting
efficiencies from larger-scale operations can lead to cost reductions in manufacturing, procurement, and
distribution (Bai et al., 2023; Li & Liu, 2023). These cost savings improve profit margins and provide a
competitive edge in pricing. Enhanced profitability and cost efficiency make the firm more resilient to
market fluctuations and economic downturns, further lowering the perceived risk by investors
(Bloomfield et al., 2024).

Thirdly, from the perspective of synergistic governance effects under the CIO framework, CIIs with
stakes in multiple companies within the same industry have a greater advantage in terms of information
acquisition, industry experience, and corporate influence. Specifically, CIIs can accumulate in-depth
industry-specific knowledge, information, and experience, making it easier for them to identify inaccurate
reports and exercise interventions. The governance experience obtained can reduce monitoring costs
and information uncertainties, enhancing the effectiveness of CIIs’ supervision (Kang et al., 2018).
Moreover, CIIs’ involvement reduces the competitiveness among companies in the same industry,
thereby lowering the possibility and costs of destructive competition (Gilje et al., 2020). Overall, the CIO
framework helps companies to coordinate industry product market strategies, resolve disputes, reduce
destructive competition, and promote positive cooperation (Kini et al., 2024; Liu & He, 2024). This may
lead to a reduction in stock price volatility and investors’ risk expectations towards the PLC, and ultim-
ately help lower its COE.

Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following hypothesis:

[H1]: Firms with common institutional ownership have lower cost of equity capital.

For companies operating in highly competitive industries, the intensity of competition raises concerns
about the survival of businesses. Moreover, stakeholders are wary of contractual loopholes that result in
low levels of inter-firm cooperation and inefficiencies. Specifically, intense industry competition exposes
companies to increased risks and unethical practices. In order to avoid being eliminated in such fierce
competition, companies in highly competitive environments are more motivated to engage in proactive
corporate governance to enhance their value and reduce their COE compared to companies in less com-
petitive environments (He & Huang, 2017).

Additionally, driven by the goal of maximizing individual firm value, the higher the competition
within an industry, the more likely businesses impose externalities on each other, leading to inefficien-
cies. Considering the exit mechanism of CIO, companies facing higher levels of industry competition
release more sensitive signals to the market through the CIO framework. This increased sensitivity cre-
ates stronger incentives for better corporate governance, which ultimately contributes to reducing a
company’s COE.

Moreover, the structure of the product market affects a company’s cash flow risk and equilibrium
return (Li et al., 2023). Through common ownership, competition and inefficiency among companies in
the investment portfolio can be suppressed, and common owners can reduce a company’s product mar-
ket risk (Bloomfield et al., 2024). Therefore, as market competition intensifies, the negative correlation
between CIO and a company’s COE becomes stronger.

Based on these considerations, this study proposes the following hypothesis:

[H2]: The higher the level of industry competition, the more significant the role of common institutional
ownership in reducing a company’s cost of equity capital.

Good corporate governance mechanisms have several benefits. On one hand, they help alleviate
agency problems among management and provide effective supervision of PLCs (Gillan & Starks, 2000;
Sch€auble, 2019). On the other hand, they contribute to improving information asymmetry issues and
enhancing the quality of information disclosure by PLCs (Hartzell & Starks, 2003). As hypothesized earlier,
from the perspective of the synergistic governance effect of CIO, whether through active participation in
corporate governance supervision or by influencing executive behavior, CIIs can achieve the goal of con-
veying positive signals through proactive corporate governance. This, in turn, reduces investors’ risk
expectations and lowers a company’s COE.

From the perspective of information asymmetry, CIIs, driven by the maximization of portfolio value,
often tend to integrate and coordinate industry experience, information resources, economies of scale,
and other factors within their portfolios, resulting in integration effects (He et al., 2019). This is beneficial
for reducing cutthroat competition. In other words, when a company has a high level of corporate gov-
ernance, it not only enables CIIs to exert synergistic governance effects but also facilitates their integra-
tion of resources and information gathering, leading to economies of scale (Dennis et al., 2022). This, in
turn, reduces stock price volatility and investors’ risk expectations toward the PLC, ultimately lowering
the company’s COE.

Based on these considerations, this study proposes the following hypothesis:

[H3]: The higher the level of corporate governance, the more significant the role of common institutional
ownership in reducing a company’s cost of equity capital.

The level of financial constraints on a PLC directly affects its financing costs, which in turn impact its
investment capabilities, production scale, and efficiency. Higher financial constraints lead to increased
financing costs, hindering the company’s ability to invest and affecting its overall operations. Conversely,
lower financial constraints enable PLCs to secure external financing more easily, facilitating timely invest-
ments in profitable projects and improving business performance (Parker & van Praag, 2006).

For PLCs facing significant financial constraints, CIIs can play a crucial role in fostering market cooper-
ation and facilitating strategic acquisitions among their portfolio companies. By mitigating inefficiencies
and reducing cutthroat competition, CIIs enhance innovation, productivity, and profitability, thereby alle-
viating the financial pressures faced by these firms (Gao et al., 2019). Research by Du and Zhang (2023)
further underscores the positive role of CIOs in reducing uncertainty in investment outcomes and

increasing financial flexibility. The presence of a CIO becomes particularly valuable as financial con-
straints on a PLC intensify, contributing significantly to reducing the company’s COE.

CIIs not only reduce competition within the industry but also facilitate the flow of resources and
information among companies, leading to improved profitability and production efficiency (Wu et al.,
2023). This collaboration helps companies overcome high financial constraints and harnesses the syner-
gistic governance effects of the CIO, ultimately lowering equity costs. Consequently, the more financially
constrained a PLC is, the more pronounced the CIO’s negative impact on the company’s COE.

Based on these considerations, this study proposes the following hypothesis:

[H4]: The higher the level of financial constraint, the more significant the role of common institutional
ownership in reducing a company’s cost of equity capital.

3. Data and methodology

This study selects 2682 non-financial listed companies in the A-share market of China from 2012 to 2022
as the research sample. To ensure the validity of the sample, we follow the convention and delete all
financial and insurance companies, firms tagged as Special Treatment (ST) due to consecutive losses in
over two years, and firms with missing data on key variables. The indicator of CIO is manually collected
and compiled based on quarterly financial reports available from the China Securities Market and
Accounting Research (CSMAR) database1. Other variables are obtained from both the CSMAR and Wind
database. To mitigate the impact of outliers on the regression, a winsorization process was employed on
all continuous variables within the regression model, trimming the upper and lower 1% of the dataset.
Ultimately, a total of 35,635 observations was obtained.

Drawing on existing literature (Chen et al., 2024; He & Huang, 2017; Koch et al., 2021), this study con-
structs two indicators to measure CIO of listed companies. The first indicator is a CIO dummy variable
(CIO_1). If a PLC has CIIs holding shares in any single quarter of the year, CIO_1 takes the value 1; other-
wise, it takes value 0. CIIs refer to institutional investors holding at least a 5% stake in two or more com-
panies in the same industry. The second indicator is the degree of connectivity of common ownership
(CIO_2), which is the number of CIIs holding shares in a PLC.

It is worth noting that CIO_1 and CIO_2 are constructed based on quarterly data. If a PLC has CIIs in
any quarter of a given year, it is considered to have been held by CIIs in that year. The average of the
quarterly indicator is taken as the corresponding annual indicator data.

their shareholdings, they are incentivized to improve their corporate governance practices. This proactive

5.2. Endogeneity tests

One potential issue in this study is the influence of firm-specific attributes, such as size, profitability, mar-
ket share, and managerial characteristics, on the CIO. These factors can affect the investment choices of
institutional investors, potentially leading to endogeneity problems. To address this concern, the study
conducts the Heckman test to examine possible sample selection biases. The results of the Heckman
test fail to reject the null hypothesis that the inverse Mill’s ratio is equal to zero, indicating no statistic-
ally significant endogeneity issues resulting from sample selection biases in the regression results.

Additionally, the experience and informational advantages of common institutional investors them-
selves may contribute to their preference for investing in higher-value and lower-risk firms. This could
potentially result in a significant reduction in the COE. Therefore, the findings presented in Tables 3 and



COGENT ECONOMICS & FINANCE 11

4 may not solely reflect the synergistic governance effects of CIO but rather the lower risk faced by the
firms in which CIIs invest. To further address the possibility of reverse causality, the study employs
lagged CIO as an instrumental variable and utilizes Two-stage Least Squares (2SLS) estimation. The esti-
mation results of 2SLS are reported in Table 5.

The first-stage regression results are presented in columns (i) and (ii). These results indicate that the
coefficient estimates of L.CIO_1 and L.CIO_2 are statistically significant and positive, suggesting a posi-
tive correlation between the instrumental variables and the core explanatory variables. The F-statistics of
1361.91 and 1334.19 indicate the absence of weak instrument problems, thus supporting the appropri-
ateness of using lagged CIO as instrumental variables.

The second-stage regression results are reported in columns (iii) and (iv). These results demonstrate
that, after accounting for potential endogeneity issues using instrumental variables, the impact of CIO
on COE remains significantly negative at the 5% level. Therefore, the empirical findings of this study are
robust to potential endogeneity problems.

7. Conclusions and implications

This paper presents an empirical analysis of the impact of CIO on the COE, using all non-financial com-
panies publicly listed in China during 2012 and 2022. The study finds that CIO significantly reduces the
COE for PLCs, aligning with traditional asset pricing theories that suggest CIO is associated with a reduc-
tion in a firm’s non-diversifiable risk. This risk mitigation may result from the exit threat posed by CIIs
and their synergistic governance effects. When companies perceive the potential negative signals and
market consequences from CIIs reducing their shareholdings, they are incentivized to improve their cor-
porate governance practices. This enhancement reduces investor risk expectations, leading to a lower
COE. Furthermore, CIIs contribute industry-specific knowledge, information, and expertise, which helps
lower monitoring costs and uncertainty. They can also facilitate the coordination of industry strategies,
such as product market strategies, competitive approaches, and dispute resolution, which collectively
contribute to reduced stock price volatility and investor risk expectations. Consequently, these factors
lead to a decreased COE for the company.

The study also finds that the impact of CIO on the COE is stronger for firms operating in more com-
petitive industries, those with better corporate governance, and those facing higher financing con-
straints. Furthermore, the effect of CIO on the COE is more pronounced for non-SOEs and companies
located in economically developed eastern regions.

The implications of these findings can be categorized into three main aspects. Firstly, the study
emphasizes the importance of fully harnessing the advantages of CIO in corporate supervision, manage-
ment, and synergistic governance. Rather than focusing on short-term gains from collusive misconduct,
CIIs should utilize their information resources and management expertise to promote cooperative efforts,
drive innovation and development, and enhance governance models. These actions can reduce unethical



14 L. SU

competition, lower information costs and the COE, and help companies establish long-term business
advantages.

Secondly, given that CIO may lead to collusive behavior, monopolistic competition, and inefficiency in
resource allocation in capital markets, relevant authorities should strengthen the supervision and regula-
tion of CIO. This may involve revising and implementing anti-monopoly laws and regulations, maintain-
ing a fair market environment, preventing disorderly capital expansion, and remaining vigilant about
potential collusive monopolistic practices arising from CIO.

Thirdly, relevant authorities should actively establish mechanisms to mitigate the inefficiencies caused
by regional market segmentation. They can leverage the synergistic governance role of CIO, reduce
financing barriers, improve financial service capabilities, and create a favorable financial ecosystem that
benefits companies and investors alike.

While this study yields interesting results and implications, it is important to acknowledge several lim-
itations. Firstly, the study primarily focuses on CIO indicators constructed using data from PLCs, which
may overlook the potential influence of institutional investors simultaneously holding shares in non-
listed companies. This limitation restricts the analysis to a subset of the overall CIO landscape. Future
studies could enhance the robustness and comprehensiveness of CIO measures by incorporating data
on institutional investors’ holdings in both listed and non-listed companies.

Secondly, the measurement of the COE itself introduces certain challenges and complexities. While
this study employs PEG and MPEG models to measure COE, it is important to note that there is no con-
sensus on the best approach. Future research could explore more elaborate measures of the COE, taking
into account additional factors such as firm-specific risk, market conditions, and investor expectations.

Thirdly, the study relies on data from non-financial Chinese PLCs. This may restrict the generalizability
of the findings to other countries. The dynamics between CIO and the COE may vary across different
market conditions, regulatory environments, and industry contexts. Future research could include a
broader range of countries or regions to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relation-
ship between CIO and the COE in different contexts. This would enhance the external validity of the
findings and provide a more robust foundation for drawing broader conclusions.
