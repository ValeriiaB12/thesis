1. Introduction 

Trade credit plays an important role in making payments when customers purchase goods or services from their suppliers and is 
often considered as an important source of informal financing for customers (Fisman and Love, 2003; Levine et al., 2018). Earlier 
literature examined the impact of factors such as financial development (Fisman and Love, 2003), ownership structure (Ge and Qiu, 
2007), supplier competition (Chod et al., 2019), and executive-supplier ties (Kong et al., 2020) on trade credit. However, there is less 
literature examining trade credit from the perspective of equity ties in customer-supplier relationships. This paper extends the line of 
research in this area by examining the impact of common ownership between customers and suppliers on the aspects of firms’ access to 
trade credit. 

Since suppliers provide trade credit without collateral from the customer and without guarantees from third parties or financial 
intermediaries (Costello, 2013; García-Teruel et al., 2014), then firms are exposed to information asymmetry and moral hazard 
problems. Research on firm common ownership that has emerged in recent years has found that when firms are linked by common 
ownership, it can mitigate information asymmetries (Kang et al., 2018) and also improve supervisory efficiency and enhance firms’ 
willingness to disclose (Park et al., 2019). Therefore, I hypothesize that there will be an impact on trade credit when common 
ownership occurs between suppliers and customers, and verify this empirically. 

I study the impact of common ownership along supply chain on trade credit using a sample of listed firms in China, and my main 
contributions are as follows. First, I provide a new perspective for studying trade credit. Second, existing literature on common 
ownership mainly studies common ownership of firms in the same industry (Azar et al., 2018; He and Huang, 2017; Lewellen and 
Lower, 2021; Li and Liu, 2023), and I expand the research related to vertical common ownership from a supply chain perspective. 
Third, I enrich the literature on supplier-customer linkages; previous literature emphasizes social ties between suppliers and customers

2. Data and research design 

2.1. Data sources 

My sample consists of firms listed on the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock exchanges during the sample period from 2009 to 2020. My 
data sources are two. First, I obtain firms’ financial data and data on firms’ top five suppliers and customers from the CSMAR database 
(a database widely used when studying Chinese firms). Second, I obtain information on the top ten shareholders of a firm from the 
firm’s shareholder database. I identified suppliers and customers with the same shareholders by comparing their shareholder infor
mation, and defined them as customer-supplier relationships with common ownership in the supply chain. 

2.2. Measurement of common ownership along supply chain 

Conceptually, common ownership along supply chain indicates the extent to which suppliers and customers are held by the same 
shareholder, and I use three methods to measure common ownership along supply chain. First, a dummy variable cos1, indicating 
whether customer i has a common shareholder with its suppliers, takes the value 1 if it does, and 0 if customer i does not have a 
common shareholder with any of its suppliers. 

Second, the product of the equity of the common shareholders of the supplier and the customer firm is calculated (Lewellen and 
Lower, 2021) and the product of all common shareholders is aggregated as follows:

2.3. Model design 

I construct model (4) to test the effect of common ownership along supply chain on firms’ trade credit, 
∑ ∑ ∑

TCi,t = α + β1(cos1 / cos2 / cos3) + βjcontrolsi,t + firmi + yeart + μit (4) 

Measurement of trade credit(TCi,t). Referring to existing studies, trade credit is defined as total accounts payable divided by total 
assets. In addition, for robustness testing, I measure trade credit in four other ways. First, the variable L TC is created, which is total 
accounts payable divided by total liabilities (Fisman and Love, 2003). Second, use (NET TC) equal to net accounts payable-accounts 
receivable divided by total assets (Liu et al,2016). Third, only the financed portion of accounts payable (F TC) (accounts payable with 
maturity > 1 year) is used, measured as a percentage of total assets (Ge and Qiu,2007). 

To be consistent with the literature, we follow Ge and Qiu (2007), Wu et al. (2014) and Liu et al. (2016) in introducing control 
variables to capture additional determinants of trade credit. Detailed definitions of all variables used in this study are presented in 
Table 1. Fourth, to exclude the effect of industry characteristics, the excess of trade credit relative to the industry average is used as the 
dependent variable. 

3. Empirical results and analysis 

3.1. Descriptive statistics analysis 

The descriptive statistics values of the variables are shown in Table 2. I find, first, that common ownership along supply chain is 
prevalent in my sample with a mean value of 0.144 for cos1, indicating that 14.4% of the customer sample has common ownership with 
at least one supplier. Second, there is a significant difference in the standard deviation and extreme values of common ownership along 
supply chain among the sample firms. The minimum value of cos3 is 0, while the maximum value is 0.143, which indicates that 
common ownership along supply chain is sufficient to have an impact on firms’ behavior. Third, the firms’ trade credit differs 
significantly with a minimum value of 0 at a mean of 0.0893, while the highest customer trade credit reaches 0.493. 

3.2. Baseline regression analysis 

Table 3 shows the baseline results of the regression using model (4). In column (1), the coefficient of the dummy variable cos1 is 
0.0172 and is statistically significant at the 1% level, indicating that customers who have common shareholders with at least one 
supplier will receive an additional 1.72% of total assets in trade credit. The coefficients of cos2 and cos3 in columns (2) and (3) are both 
significantly positive, indicating that common ownership along supply chain increases the level of trade credit of the firm and enables 
customers to obtain more trade credit from their suppliers. 

In addition, the coefficients of the control variables are in line with my expectation. The coefficient of size is negative and sig
nificant, indicating that larger firms receive less trade credit (Ge and Qiu, 2007). The estimated coefficients of cash held by firms (cash) 
and financial leverage (lev) are both positive, which is consistent with the results of Liu et al. (2016). 

3.3. Robust tests 

Table 4 shows the regression results of the robustness tests, using the other three different measures of trade credit. When using 
other measurements for the dependent variable, all coefficients of cos1 and cos3 in Table 4 are statistically significant positive and the 
coefficient of cos2 on NET TC is positive and significant, which confirms the robustness of my findings.

3.4. Endogeneity 

Considering that the existence of common ownership ties between customers and suppliers may not be exogenous, my results may 
be influenced by potential endogeneity, especially reverse causality. For example, supply chain relationships where the same share
holder has a greater probability of holding a higher level of trade credit because the supplier-customer relationship is more stable. In 
this subsection, I use two approaches to address the issue of potential endogeneity. First, the dependent variable is used for period t+1. 
Second, the 2SLS model with instrumental variables is used to test the model. 

Table 5 shows the regression results using the dependent variable in period t+1. After replacing the dependent variable with the 
trade credit fTCin period t+1, the coefficients of the three variables of common ownership along supply chain remain positive and 
significant, indicating that common ownership along supply chain increases trade credit in period t+1, further demonstrating the 
robustness of my results. 

For the selection of instrumental variables, I am inspired by Schmidt and Fahlenbrach (2017), Gao et al. (2019) and Chen et al. 
(2023) to use the entry or exit of a firm’s suppliers into or out of the constituent stocks of the CSI 300 index, which contains 300 stocks 
that are " blue-chip" firms, which are more likely to attract investor interest. Belonging to the CSI 300 index may change the indicator 
that suppliers and customers have a common ownership link, but it does not have a direct impact on a firm’s trade credit. Based on the 
performance and stock price performance of the firms each year, the CSI 300 index adjusts its constituents. Based on this background, I 
define two dummy variables and, which indicate whether a firm’s suppliers entered and exited the constituents of the CSI 300 index in 
the previous period, respectively. And use these two dummy variables to predict common owernership along supply chain. 

The results are shown in Table 6. In the first step of the regression, the coefficient of in300 is significantly positive, while out300 is 
not significant, indicating that the entry of suppliers into the CSI 300 index increases the level of common ownership along supply 
chain. In the second step of the regression, the coefficients of both cos2 and cos3 are significantly positive, indicating that my results are 
not altered by the endogeneity problem.

Based on this, I examines two potential channels for common ownership along supply chain to improve trade credit, alleviating 
information asymmetry and participating in firm decision making. 

First, drawing on the existing literature (Li et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2023), we use the geographical distance from the supplier firm to 
capture the degree of information asymmetry between customers and suppliers. Adding the cross product term cos2 × dist,of common 
ownership and geographic distance to Eq. (4), if the coefficient of this term is positive, indicates that the impact of common ownership 
along supply chain on trade credit is greater when information asymmetry is higher, and there exists a channel to mitigate information 
asymmetry. 

Second, appointing directors may be an effective way for common shareholders to participate in firm governance, which in turn 
helps them to participate directly in making firm investment decisions. Consider that common shareholders have an incentive to 
induce suppliers to provide larger trade credits to their customers. To test this channel, I define shareholder-appointed directors as 
"directors who also work for a common shareholder" and manually collect data on directors appointed by common shareholders to 
suppliers during the sample period. A dummy variable dir is constructed to indicate whether the common shareholder appoints a 
director to the firm’s supplier, and the cross product term cos2 × dir is tested. If the coefficient is positive, it indicates that after the 
common shareholder appoints a director, the customer receives more trade credit from the supplier, proving that the channel of 
participation in the firm’s decision making holds. 

Table 7 shows the regression results of the channel test. The coefficient of the cross product term cos2 × dist is positive and sig
nificant, indicating that the channel of information asymmetry holds. The coefficient of the cross product cos3 × dir is also positive and 
significant, indicating that the channel of participation in firm decision making is valid. 

3.6. Heterogeneity of firm property rights 

In the context of low level of financial development, non-state firms in China have less access to finance compared to state-owned 
firms. As a result, non-state firms in China receive more trade credit (Ge and Qiu, 2007). Whether supply chain common ownership 
linkages help non-state firms to obtain more trade credit when suppliers and customers have common shareholders. I divide the sample 
into state-owned and non-state-owned firms for regressions to test for heterogeneity. The results, shown in Table 8, are positive and 
significant for the non-SOE group, while the results for SOEs are not significant, indicating that common ownership along supply chain 
mainly helps non-SOEs to obtain more trade credit. 

4. Discussions and conclusion 

I studied the impact of customer and supplier common ownership on trade credit from the perspective of supply chain equity 
linkages and obtained the following conclusions. Common ownership along supply chain significantly increases the size of trade credit. 
After applying a series of robustness tests such as the replacement trade credit measure and dealing with endogeneity issues, my 
conclusions remain robust. Further channel tests suggest that common ownership along supply chain enhances firms’ trade credit by 
mitigating information asymmetry between customers and suppliers and dispatching directors to participate in the channel of firm 
decision making. Heterogeneity tests that distinguish between firm ownership indicate that common ownership along supply chain 
improves trade credit of non-state firms more significantly. 

Based on this, I make two suggestions. First, encourage suppliers and customers to overcome the information asymmetry and inter- 
firm trust problems in supply chains, such as through common ownership. Secondly, common shareholders should actively explore 
effective mechanisms to enhance the market value of upstream and downstream firms in the supply chain by virtue of their governance 
advantages, so as to alleviate the information friction and financing constraints between suppliers and customers. Especially Chinese 
firms have been integrated into the global supply chain system, and for supply chain common shareholders, their main advantages are 
information resources and management experience, which should promote the improvement of supply chain governance model of 
Chinese firms. 

For suppliers, the risk of customers’ failure to repay all debts should be considered in the case of providing trade credit to cus
tomers, especially when market demand is unstable and the credit status of downstream firms is poor (Zhang et al., 2014). In addition, 
the structure of supply chains is divided into vertical and horizontal, and different structures of supply chains may be affected by 
common ownership differently, but this paper is limited by data and does not support the distinction between horizontal and vertical 
supply chains, and expects that subsequent studies can answer this question.
